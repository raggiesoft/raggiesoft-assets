He squeezed her hand, the tension in his shoulders easing a little. "I KNOW. YOU’RE RIGHT. IT'S JUST… BAD MEMORIES NEAR THERE."

"And we won't go anywhere near those memories," Chloé affirmed. "This trip is about family, rest, and celebrating how far you’ve come. We’ll spend our days going between your house and mine, just like we always did. A genuine smile spread across Alex's face at the thought. The familiarity of Lisa Court, the comfort of their intertwined childhood homes, the presence of all four of their loving parents – *that* part of Virginia, he was very much looking forward to. The part where he and Chloé could just be Alex and Chloé, best friends, a unit, in the places that held so many good memories before Vera, and so much healing now.

"THAT SOUNDS REALLY GOOD, CHLOÉ," he typed, his voice reflecting his relief and anticipation. "REALLY GOOD. I MISS OUR HOUSES. AND THE PARENTS."

"They miss you too, more than anything," Chloé said. "It's going to be a good break, Alex. A real holiday. You deserve it."

He leaned his head against her shoulder, a familiar gesture of comfort. The apprehension about returning to the state where he’d endured such trauma was still there, a faint echo, but Chloé’s promise, her steadfast presence, and the image of their beloved Virginia Beach homes, a world away from the darkness of Portsmouth and Kent, made it feel manageable. He was going home for the holidays, with his best friend, his wife, by his side. And for now, that was more than enough.

## Chapter 7: The Journey Home

### Part 1: The Cavalry Arrives (Mid-December 2003)

The first snowflakes of the Pacific Northwest winter had just begun to fall, dusting the evergreen trees outside Chloé’s Lynnwood apartment. Inside, a quiet, healing routine had taken hold. The weeks since Alex’s rescue had been a slow, steady process of him reclaiming his strength and his agency. The constant, grounding presence of Chloé, their shared safe language, and the daily, loving video calls with their parents had been a powerful medicine.

Just before Christmas, the cavalry arrived. The four parents—James, Emily, Thomas, and Sophie—landed at Seattle-Tacoma International Airport after a long, tense flight from Norfolk, with a connection through Chicago O'Hare. They had rented a minivan at the airport and driven straight to a grocery store before even heading to the apartment. They arrived at Chloé’s small, first-floor unit not with luggage, but with arms laden with bags of groceries—fresh fruit, bread, milk, eggs, and the ingredients for a simple, nourishing soup. Their first, overwhelming instinct was to provide.

Chloé opened the door, and for a moment, no one spoke. The four parents just stared, their eyes finding Alex, who was sitting in his wheelchair in the small living area. The video calls had prepared them, but seeing him in person was a different, more brutal reality. They saw the hollowness in his cheeks, the way his sweater seemed to hang on his too-thin frame, the deep, haunted weariness in his eyes that no webcam could fully capture.

Emily Miller was the first to move, a small, choked sob escaping her. She crossed the room in three quick steps and knelt before her son, pulling him into a hug that was a torrent of a month's worth of terror and relief. She didn't say anything, just held him, her own tears falling silently as she felt the fragility of his body. James’s hand came to rest on Alex’s shoulder, a silent, powerful promise of protection, his jaw tight with a cold, contained fury for the woman who had done this.

The Masons enveloped both their daughter and their "second son" in warm, all-encompassing hugs. Sophie held Chloé tight, whispering "*Ma brave fille, ma brave fille*" (My brave girl, my brave girl) into her hair, acknowledging the immense burden her daughter had carried alone. Thomas’s hug for Alex was one of quiet, steadfast strength.

For the first time in months, their entire, united family was together under one roof. The small apartment was suddenly crowded, but it was a warmth that chased away the last of the shadows. The healing could truly begin now.

### Part 2: A Gentle Deconstruction

The next few days were a masterclass in gentle, purposeful action. The goal was to pack up Chloé's entire student life for the journey home to Virginia Beach, but every action was calibrated to the needs of Alex's recovery. The air hummed not with frantic haste, but with a carefully choreographed ballet of love and logistics, conducted almost entirely in the soft, flowing cadence of Québécois French—the only language Alex felt completely safe in now.

The apartment had become a busy, loving command center. James and Thomas, with their practical, handy nature, moved with a shared, unspoken understanding. The soft whir of an electric screwdriver was followed by the dull thud of a dismantled bed frame. They carefully wrapped the legs of Chloé’s desk in moving blankets, their movements economical and precise.

Emily and Sophie, meanwhile, were the heart of the packing operation. Surrounded by a sea of cardboard boxes and packing paper, they sorted and packed, their conversations a soft, constant hum.

"*Tu penses qu'elle veut garder ça?*" Sophie murmured, holding up a chipped NPU coffee mug. (Do you think she wants to keep this?)

"*Ah, oui, c'est certain,*" Emily answered with a knowing smile, her French fluent. "*Elle et Alex en ont acheté des pareils leur première semaine ici.*" (Oh, yes, for sure. She and Alex bought matching ones their first week here.)

They folded sweaters, wrapped dishes, and sifted through the tangible memories of the life Chloé was now choosing to leave behind. And they saw, firsthand, the living reality of the bond that had ultimately saved their son.

They saw how Chloé would instinctively pause her task of labeling a box, her head tilting as if listening to a sound only she could hear. She’d turn to Alex. He was positioned near the sliding glass door, watching the rain trace paths down the pane.

"*T'as soif?*" Chloé asked softly. (Are you thirsty?)

Alex wouldn't speak. He wouldn’t even gesture broadly. There was just a subtle shift in his posture, a slight relaxation of his shoulders that no one else would have noticed. But Chloé saw it. She poured him a cup of water, holding the straw to his lips with a practiced steadiness. His eyes, dark and still so shadowed with trauma, met hers. A small, almost imperceptible nod was his thanks.

In that moment, the parents paused. James stopped tightening a bolt. Sophie held a stack of plates mid-wrap. They were a single, orbiting system, Chloé and Alex, and the parents were the careful astronomers charting their course, supporting them without ever breaking their gravitational pull.

"*Dieu merci qu'elle a choisi un appart au rez-de-chaussée,*" Thomas remarked quietly to James, as they wheeled the empty frame of the sofa out the door with ease. (Thank God she chose a ground-floor apartment.) "*J'imagine même pas descendre lui et le fauteuil par des escaliers tous les jours.*" (I can't even imagine getting him and the chair down stairs every day.)

Chloé overheard him from the kitchen. "*C'était l'idée d'Alex,*" she said, her voice clear. (It was Alex's idea.) "*Quand on cherchait. Il disait de toujours prévoir l'imprévu.*" (When we were looking. He said to always plan for the unexpected.) The irony of his foresight hung in the air, a heavy, poignant truth.

Later, as the apartment grew more spartan, Emily found Sophie standing by the empty bookshelf.

"*Il n'a même pas eu besoin de lui demander,*" Emily said, her voice thick with emotion. (He didn't even have to ask her.)

Sophie reached out and squeezed her friend's hand, her gaze fixed on their children by the window. "*Elle était déjà à l'écoute,*" Sophie replied. (She was already listening.) "*Elle écoute avec son cœur.*" (She listens with her heart.)

The room was nearly empty now, filled with the ghosts of posters and the scent of cleaning spray. As the late afternoon sun failed against the persistent clouds, Chloé knelt beside Alex’s chair, leaning her head against his shoulder. For a long moment, they just sat together, watching the rain, the silence between them more comforting than any words.

Alex lifted his hand once more to his keyboard. A few soft taps, a moment of processing, and the synthesized voice broke the quiet again. It was soft, but it carried across the nearly empty room with absolute clarity.

"*Prêt à rentrer.*" (Ready to go home.)

### Part 3: Shipping a Life

Chloé, with her mother's help, made the practical decisions. Taking all her books, her posters, her small collection of kitchenware, on the plane was an impossibility.

"It's just stuff," she said to her mom, a new, adult wisdom in her voice. "The only thing that really matters is that we're all going home together."

They packed box after box, labeling them for a shipping company that would transport her life back to Virginia. As Sophie was carefully wrapping a stack of textbooks, she pulled a well-loved, slightly worn stuffed otter from Chloé’s pillow. "And what about Barnaby?" she asked, a gentle smile on her face. "Does he fly with you, or does he go in a box?"

Chloé looked over from the box she was taping shut. "Oh, no, Barnaby flies," she said with a seriousness that brooked no argument. "He's essential personnel."

Alex, who was quietly observing from his chair, felt a profound warmth spread through his chest at the sight of the familiar stuffed animal. It was the same otter Chloé had brought with her on that first, fateful sleepover four years ago, the night of his nightmare, the night their friendship had deepened into something unbreakable. Barnaby wasn't just a toy; he was a relic, a tangible link to the very beginning of their shared life, a symbol of the night she had first climbed into his bed to chase the darkness away. Seeing him now, a constant in her life even here, 3,000 miles from home, was a powerful, grounding reassurance.

Emily Miller, who also remembered that night well, caught the look on Alex's face. She smiled softly. "Of course he's essential personnel," she said to Sophie. "Barnaby is a very important member of this family."

And so, it was decided. The only things Chloé kept for the flight were the absolute essentials: a suitcase of clothes, her laptop, and her ever-present, very important stuffed otter, Barnaby.

### Part 4: The Flight Home

The trip to Sea-Tac was a stark contrast to the last time Alex had been in a transport hub. This time, he was surrounded by a loving, protective phalanx of family. The check-in process was a chaotic symphony of echoing announcements, rolling suitcases, and a thousand overlapping conversations. Emily, seeing the immediate sensory overload begin to register on Alex's face, quickly produced a pair of foam earplugs, which he gratefully accepted.

Boarding the Gateway Airlines flight was the most difficult part of the journey. At the gate, the airline staff brought out the narrow, high-backed "aisle chair." With James and Thomas providing the strength and Chloé providing the close, intimate support, they carefully transferred Alex from his own wheelchair to the aisle chair. "Okay, Alex," Chloé murmured, her voice a quiet anchor in the noisy gate area, "tuck your arms in tight for me." She gently positioned his limbs, her touch a familiar comfort, ensuring he was secure for the difficult passage down the narrow airplane aisle.

They had pre-boarded, and Chloé guided him to the second row. He took the window seat, his first time on a plane. Chloé slid into the middle seat beside him, with Emily taking the aisle. The other three parents settled into the row directly across from them.

At the check-in counter, James Miller had confirmed their flight path. "Alright," he had said, reviewing the tickets. "Seattle to Norfolk, with a quick stop in Chicago. And I made sure we're connecting through **Midway** this time. A much easier, less chaotic airport than O'Hare. A smoother ride for everyone." He had given Alex a small, knowing look. The unspoken message was clear: *We are not repeating any part of that last journey. We are taking the safest, calmest path home.*

The connection at Midway was a welcome relief. The gates were close, the crowds smaller, and the atmosphere far less frantic than O'Hare would have been. The flight was a quiet, peaceful affair. Alex sat by the window, Chloé beside him, her hand resting on his arm, a constant, reassuring presence. For the first time in months, he wasn't a victim being transported; he was a young man being brought home.

### Part 5: Sanctuary

They landed in Norfolk late in the evening. The familiar, humid Virginia air, even in winter, felt like a welcoming blanket. The deplaning process was slow; they waited patiently until every other passenger had gotten off before the aisle chair was brought back. Chloé never left his side for a moment. The quiet, smaller scale of ORF was a blessed relief after the day's long journey.

The drive back to Lisa Court was filled with a quiet, contented silence. They pulled into the familiar driveway of the Miller house. Home.

As they got inside, a profound, bone-deep weariness, the culmination of months of trauma and stress, finally settled over Alex. He looked at his familiar living room, at his parents, at the Masons, at Chloé. He was safe.

He reached for his AAC, his fingers moving with a slow, deliberate purpose.

I NEED MY BED.

It was a simple statement, but it was laden with a world of meaning. He didn't just want to rest; he wanted the ultimate sanctuary, the one place where he had always felt the most secure, especially since Chloé had entered his life.

Emily Miller’s eyes filled with tears, and she nodded, her expression full of a deep, maternal understanding.

A few minutes later, after a brief, loving check-in from his parents, the familiar evening routine began, but this time, it was Chloé who took the lead. She helped him into his accessible bathroom, assisting him with a quick, warm shower and toileting, her movements practiced and respectful, a seamless return to the caregiving roles they had shared for years. After she had taken her own quick shower, they went to his room. Chloé appeared in the doorway, her own small overnight bag in her hand. She didn't ask. She didn't need to. She just gave him a small, soft smile, and without a word, they climbed into his full-sized bed. Together.

He turned on his side, and she met him, wrapping her arms around him in a warm, protective embrace. He buried his face in her shoulder, breathing in the familiar, safe scent of her. The long, terrifying journey was finally, truly, over. He was home. And Chloé was right where she belonged, by his side.

## Chapter 8: Winter Break Decisions

### Part 1: The Insurance Dilemma and a Loving Proposition (Mid December 2003)

The flight from Seattle to Norfolk for winter break had been Alex Miller’s first, and having Chloé Mason steadfastly by his side had made all the difference. The weeks spent in the quiet sanctuary of Chloé’s Lynnwood apartment since his rescue from Vera in late November had been a period of intense healing. Now, back in Virginia, surrounded by both his parents and Chloé’s, Alex felt a fragile sense of peace.

The upcoming Christmas holiday held a special anticipation for Chloé. "It's going to be so good to see them," she said to Alex one afternoon, her voice bright with excitement. "Tante Geneviève, Oncle David, Mathieu, Isabelle, even Mémère Madeleine... they're all flying in from Montreal for Christmas. Their first time ever spending the holidays in the States! I think... I think it will be good for you, too," she added softly, her gaze full of a deep, protective love. "To be around them. They're loud and crazy, but they're family. They'll just... love you. No questions asked."

A few days before the Leclercs were due to arrive, on a quiet evening in mid-December, the six of them were gathered in the Millers' living room. James Miller, after a thoughtful pause, gently broached the topic that had been on all the parents’ minds.

"Alex, son," he began, "we need to talk about next year. And about long-term support. Especially," he added, his brow furrowing slightly, "health insurance."

Alex nodded, understanding the weight of his father’s words. The fear of being without adequate medical care was a constant, low-level anxiety. I KNOW, DAD. INSURANCE IS EXPENSIVE. I WORRY TOO, he typed.

Chloé, listening intently, spoke up. "Once I graduate and get a good full-time job, those kinds of jobs usually come with really good family health benefits. But," she added, "those benefits are generally for the employee, their legal spouse, and dependent children."

James leaned forward slightly. "Alex, there is… a legal way that you could potentially be considered Chloé’s 'family member' for insurance purposes in the future..."

A tense silence filled the room. Alex felt a sudden, cold dread. He knew, with sickening certainty, what his father was alluding to. Marriage. The word itself felt like a brand, seared into his memory by Vera.

He started to tremble, his fingers flying across his AAC keypad, the English words tumbling out in a rush of panic: NO! DAD, NO! MARRIAGE? CHLOÉ AND I… WE ARE BEST FRIENDS! I CAN’T… VERA… SHE SAID… IT WOULD RUIN EVERYTHING!

His voice, via the device, was high with distress. Chloé was instantly by his side, her hand on his arm, her own face etched with concern as she instinctively switched to their soothing Québécois French.

"*Shhh, Alex, respire avec moi, mon trésor,*" she murmured, her voice a low, calming hum meant only for him. "*C'est correct. Personne ne te force à rien. Je suis là.*" (Shhh, Alex, breathe with me, my treasure. It's okay. No one is forcing you to do anything. I'm here.)

While her focus was entirely on calming Alex, a fierce, private thought crystallized in her mind with the clarity of a diamond. *This is it,* she thought, her resolve hardening into steel. *The insurance is the reason for him, the one he can accept. But for me? This is the shield. If I'm his wife, no one, not Vera, not anyone, can ever hurt him or question my right to protect him again. This is how I keep him safe. Forever.*

"Oh, sweetheart, no," Emily Miller said gently. "That's not what your father meant. We all know your bond with Chloé is a precious, platonic friendship."

James, his voice filled with regret, explained. "Son, I am so sorry. I was suggesting a 'marriage of convenience.' A legal arrangement, purely for the practical purpose of Chloé being able to share her future work benefits with you as her legal spouse. Nothing about your friendship would need to change."

Sophie Mason added her warm, Québécois-inflected English, "Think of it, *mes chéris*, as a very strong promise, a legal way for Chloé to take care of you, like family does. The paper just makes it official for the insurance people."

Chloé, sensing his internal struggle, squeezed his arm. "*Alex, regarde-moi,*" she said softly in their safe language. "*Si cette idée te fait trop peur, si ça te rappelle trop Vera, on trouve une autre solution. D'accord? Ta paix, c'est ça qui compte.*" (Alex, look at me. If this idea scares you too much, if it reminds you too much of Vera, we find another solution. Okay? Your peace is what matters.)

He looked at her, at the absolute sincerity in her eyes. The sheer selflessness of it—marrying him, legally, just so he could be safe—was overwhelming. This wasn't Vera. This was Chloé. His person.

He took a deep breath. He switched his AAC back to English, for all their parents to hear his decision, but his eyes were on Chloé. CHLOÉ. OUR PARENTS. IF THIS MARRIAGE… IS JUST A LEGAL WAY… FOR YOU TO HELP ME... AND IF NOTHING ELSE… NOTHING ABOUT US… CHANGES… THEN… THEN YES. I THINK… I THINK I CAN DO THAT. TO BE SAFE. TO STAY WITH YOU.

Chloé’s eyes welled, but she smiled, a radiant, relieved smile. "Yes, Alex. Absolutely. Still Alex and Chloé. Best friends who are practically family. This would just be… a legal way to make sure you’re always safe."

A quiet, profound understanding settled over the room. The decision was made. Sophie Mason, her own eyes still shimmering, stood up and went to the kitchen phone. "We must call Geneviève," she said to the room. "They must hear this from us, now."

Thomas dialed his sister-in-law's number in Stanstead. After a moment, a cheerful, French voice answered. "*Allô?*"

"*Geneviève, c'est Sophie,*" Sophie said, putting the phone on speaker for everyone to hear. (Geneviève, it's Sophie.)

"*Sophie! Ma sœur! Comment ça va?*" Geneviève's warm voice filled the room. (Sophie! My sister! How are you?) "Are the kids excited for us to come down for Noël?"

"Everyone is very excited," Sophie confirmed, her voice catching slightly. "But, chérie, something has happened. Something important. We... we have some news."

The line was quiet for a moment. "*Qu'est-ce qui se passe?*" Geneviève asked, her tone shifting from cheerful to concerned. (What's going on?)

Sophie took a deep breath. "It's about Alex and Chloé. We were just talking about the future..." She hesitated, looking to James for support.

James Miller stepped closer to the phone, his voice calm and clear. "Geneviève, it's James. We were discussing the reality of Alex's long-term health insurance needs, especially now that he and Chloé plan to build a life together in Washington. It's a big worry for us."

Emily added, her voice soft and full of emotion. "We... we suggested a practical solution. A legal marriage, so that Chloé could one day share her work benefits with him. But the word 'marriage'... *le mot 'mariage'*... it caused Alex a lot of pain. He had a panic attack, Geneviève. Because of what that horrible woman Vera did, how she used that word against him."

There was a sharp intake of breath on the other end of the line.

Thomas then spoke, his own voice steady. "*On lui a tout expliqué, David est là?*" (We explained everything to him, is David there?) "*We told him it was a marriage of convenience. A legal paper to protect him. We made it clear that nothing, absolutely nothing, about his and Chloé's platonic friendship would change. C'est juste pour l'assurer, pour son futur.*" (It's just to insure him, for his future.)

They could hear Geneviève's muffled voice explaining the situation to her husband. A moment later, Uncle David's booming, heartfelt voice came over the line.

"*Sophie? Thomas? C'est David. C'est la bonne décision. C'est une chose merveilleuse qu'ils font, pour l'un et l'autre. C'est ça, la famille.*" (Sophie? Thomas? It's David. It's the right decision. It's a wonderful thing they are doing, for each other. That's what family is.)

"*We want to be there,*" Geneviève said, her voice clear and resolved now. "We must be there. When is it?"

"We were thinking," Emily Miller said softly, "January fifth. It will be a small, simple ceremony at the courthouse."

"*January fifth?*" Geneviève repeated. "*Pas de problème. We will change our flights. We were supposed to leave on the second, but we will stay. All of us. Maman, too. We will be there. Of course, we will be there.*"

A wave of relief and gratitude washed over the six of them in the Millers' living room. The plan was set. Their entire, united, cross-border family would be there to witness the quiet, powerful union of two very special souls.

### Part 2: A Canadian Landing (December 22, 2003)

### Part 3: A Day of Two Cultures (December 23, 2003)

The morning of December 23rd dawned bright and chilly on Lisa Court. In the Masons' house, the "Cousins' Suite"—formerly known as Chloé's bedroom—was stirring. Mathieu and Isabelle emerged, looking a little rumpled and sleep-deprived. Across the lawn, Alex and Chloé were already with the Millers, the six of them enjoying a quiet breakfast. The plan for the day was a perfect fusion of their two families' traditions: a trip to the grocery store to gather supplies for a massive Christmas Eve *Réveillon* feast, followed by an afternoon of holiday baking.

The cousins stared at the can, then at each other. The idea of a cheese that wasn't legally cheese, that came out of a can like paint, was the most bizarre and fascinating thing they had ever seen.

Back at the Masons' house, the kitchen became the warm, fragrant heart of their combined holiday. It also gave them a peaceful island of their own amidst the happy, boisterous energy of the full house, their quiet companionship a familiar anchor in a day full of new family dynamics.

### Part 4: Le Réveillon on Lisa Court (December 24, 2003)

The exchange student whispered a quick translation to his partner, who gasped.

Chloé took another step closer, her voice rising, each French word a lash. *"Tu oses te montrer ici? Après ce que tu as fait? Après Kent? Après avoir failli le tuer avec ta négligence?! Tu penses que tu peux juste réapparaître et le réclamer comme un objet perdu?!"* (You dare show your face here? After what you did? After Kent? After nearly killing him with your neglect?! You think you can just reappear and claim him like a lost object?!)

She gestured sharply towards their rings. *"Ces alliances? Elles représentent la sécurité, la confiance, le SOIN – des choses dont tu ne sais RIEN! Elles représentent le fait qu'il ne sera PLUS JAMAIS à ta merci! PLUS JAMAIS!"* (These rings? They represent safety, trust, CARE – things you know NOTHING about! They represent the fact that he will NEVER be at your mercy again! NEVER AGAIN!)

Vera was pale now, the initial shock giving way to a dawning fear. This wasn't the quiet, polite Chloé she had likely imagined Alex's "best friend" to be. This was a lioness, and apparently, she had an appreciative audience.

*"Va-t'en, Vera!"* Chloé’s voice was a low, dangerous command. *"Sors de ce magasin. Sors de nos vies. Si je te revois encore une fois près d'Alex, je jure devant Dieu que ce ne sera pas seulement une ordonnance restrictive que tu affronteras, ce sera la police de Virginia Beach au complet! DÉCALISSE! HOSTIE DE VIPÈRE!"* (Get lost, Vera! Get out of this store. Get out of our lives. If I see you near Alex one more time, I swear to God it won't just be a restraining order you'll face, it'll be the entire Virginia Beach police force! SCRAM! DAMN VIPER!)

The final, uniquely Québécois expletives, delivered with such force, seemed to physically push Vera back. She looked wildly around, seeing the curious and now disapproving stares of many shoppers, and the distinct, almost approving nods from the few who understood French. The older Québécois woman even let out a soft, satisfied *"Bien dit, ma fille!"* (Well said, my girl!). Vera looked at Alex, who was watching Chloé with a mixture of awe and profound relief, his fear visibly receding in the face of Chloé's powerful defense. He made no move towards Vera, offered no sign that he wanted her.

Vera’s face crumpled. Her delusion, her last vestiges of control, had been utterly demolished by a petite young woman speaking a language she couldn’t understand but whose meaning was terrifyingly clear, and apparently, appreciated by a multilingual peanut gallery. Without another word, Vera turned, almost tripping over her own feet, and fled down the aisle, disappearing into the depths of ValueMart as fast as she could, her tail tucked firmly between her legs.

As Vera scurried away, the older French-speaking woman caught Chloé's eye and gave her a small, emphatic thumbs-up and a warm, conspiratorial wink before turning back to her own shopping. The student couple murmured appreciative comments in French to each other.

Chloé watched Vera go, her chest heaving, the adrenaline still coursing. Then, she slowly turned back to Alex, her expression softening instantly as her gaze met his.

*"Ça va, mon cœur?"* she asked gently, her voice still a little shaky, switching back to their intimate Québécois. (Are you okay, my heart?)

Alex looked at her, his eyes shining with an emotion too deep for his AAC to fully capture in that moment. He simply reached out and took her hand, his grip tight and unwavering. He was safe. Chloé had, once again, been his unwavering shield, and this time, they'd even had a silent, multilingual cheering section. Vera's power over him was well and truly broken.
