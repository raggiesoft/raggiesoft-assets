# Book: Young Adults, New Worlds

## Chapter 1: Standing Tall

### Part 1: Delivery Day (Fall 2005)

The air in Lisa Court was thick with an almost unbearable anticipation. It was a crisp autumn day, nearly a year after the family had made the decision to pursue new equipment for Alex. A year of specialists, evaluations, mountains of paperwork, and a grinding, persistent battle with the insurance company had all led to this moment. Today was Delivery Day.

A large van from their accessibility vendor was parked outside. In their small living room, two large cardboard boxes dominated the space. A friendly, competent technician named Dave was carefully unboxing the first, revealing the sleek, black frame of a state-of-the-art power wheelchair. It looked less like a medical device and more like a high-tech exploration vehicle.

On Chloé’s laptop, a video call was open. All four of their parents were gathered in the Millers' living room in Virginia, their faces a mixture of excitement and nervous hope, determined not to miss this milestone.

While Dave worked on the chair, he unboxed the second package. It was Alex’s new AAC device, a ruggedized tablet that was noticeably thinner and lighter than his old machine. "This one's a beast," Dave said, clearly impressed. "Natively running Quantum OS XN Professional, and we had the vendor pre-install the French-Canadian MUI pack you ordered. It's also got the latest **Vocal-Path Suite Pro** on it," he added, tapping the screen, "fully loaded with the newest **American English, Québécois French, and Metropolitan French** voices. And of course, we put on your Quantum Suite 2003 and the new Developer Suite 2005. She's ready to fly."

After mounting the new device and transferring Alex's core files, Dave spent another hour with Alex and Chloé, meticulously fine-tuning the joystick controls and seating positions. Finally, he sat back on his heels.

"Alright, Alex," Dave said, a grin on his face. "She's all yours. The basic driving and communication is all set." He paused, his eyes twinkling. "Now... for the main event. Ready to try standing?"

A tremor of fear and excitement went through Alex. He looked at Chloé, who stood nearby, her hands clasped together, her eyes shining with unshed tears of joy for him. He looked at the faces of his parents on the laptop screen, their expressions full of love. He took a deep breath and gave Dave a firm nod.

"Okay," Dave said, talking him through it. "It's the toggle on the right side of your joystick. Just a gentle push forward. The chair will do the rest."

Alex reached out, his hand steady, and engaged the control.

A series of soft, powerful whirs emanated from the chair's base. It wasn't the jerky, mechanical movement he had imagined. It was a smooth, fluid, and silent ascent. He felt the complex machinery tilting and lifting him, the straps providing secure support as his legs straightened and, for the first time in years on dry land, bore his own weight.

The world shifted. The perspective he had known his entire life—of looking up at countertops, at faces, at the world—was tilting, expanding, rising. He was going up. Higher. Higher.

The chair stopped, its motors sighing into silence. He was standing. He was at his full height, his head nearly touching the ceiling of their small apartment. He looked down at his own feet, planted firmly on the footplate. He looked across the room, over the back of the sofa. He could see the tops of the kitchen cabinets.

Then he looked at Chloé.

She was standing a few feet away, her face wet with tears, her smile the most beautiful thing he had ever seen. For the first time, he wasn't looking up at her. They were looking directly at each other, eye to eye.

The technician, sensing the profound intimacy of the moment, cleared his throat. "I'm just... going to go organize some of this paperwork in the hall," he said quietly, and discreetly slipped out of the room.

Alex, his heart hammering in his chest, used the joystick to slowly, carefully, turn his chair to face Chloé fully. No words were needed. No device could have captured the emotion that passed between them.

She stepped forward, closing the distance, and wrapped her arms around his neck. Alex’s own arms, strong and sure, went around her waist, holding her close.

This was it.

It wasn't the wonderful, buoyant, equalizing hug of the swimming pool. It was a real, solid, two-feet-on-the-ground hug. He could feel her full weight leaning into him, and he was standing, supporting them both. He could feel her heart beating against his chest. He buried his face in her coppery red hair, breathing in the familiar, beloved scent of her shampoo, and held on as if he would never let go. It was a hug that held a lifetime of friendship, of shared secrets, of sleeping side-by-side for comfort, of her fierce protection and his quiet trust. It was a hug of perfect, balanced reciprocity.

From the living room sofa, their four parents watched, openly weeping with joy.

After a long, perfect moment, they slowly pulled apart, their hands still linked. Chloé was laughing through her tears. Alex, his own eyes shining, reached for his new AAC device. The screen was bright, the new processor impossibly fast. He typed a short, simple sentence.

The new, more natural-sounding synthesized voice spoke the words, clear and full of a wonder that was all Alex's.

CHLOÉ... I CAN SEE YOUR EYES.

## Chapter 2: A Triumphant Return

### Part 1: The Logistics of a Homecoming (Summer 2006)

The Lynnwood apartment was bathed in the long, soft light of a Pacific Northwest summer evening. Alex Miller, now twenty-one, sat in his new power wheelchair, a marvel of modern technology that hummed almost silently. It was sleek and black, a stark contrast to the bulky, beige machine of his teenage years. But its most incredible feature, the one that still felt like a miracle every time he used it, was its ability to stand. After a nearly year-long battle with the insurance company, the chair was his, a testament to his parents’ advocacy and Chloé’s relentless support.

Across the room, Chloé Miller, also twenty-one, was laser-focused on her laptop screen, a familiar sight. She was in full planning mode, a whirlwind of logistics and meticulous detail for their first trip back to the border since they were fifteen.

"Okay, I think I've got it," she announced, a note of triumph in her voice. "The perfect itinerary. I booked the flights."

Alex maneuvered his chair closer to the desk, the smooth electric motors a quiet whir on the hardwood floor. THE FLIGHTS? his AAC device asked, its voice a much more natural-sounding synthesis than the robotic tones of his youth.

"The flights," she confirmed, turning the screen towards him. "Gateway Airlines, SEA to BTV, for the second week of August. And look," she pointed to the connection details, "the layover is in Washington Reagan, DCA. Not Dulles. It's a beyond-perimeter exemption flight. Just like we wanted. It'll be a much calmer connection for us."

PERFECT, Alex voiced, a wave of relief washing over him. The thought of navigating a massive, chaotic hub was always a source of underlying anxiety. Chloé’s savvy planning was, as always, his shield against the world's overwhelming aspects.

"Okay, flights are done," Chloé said, clicking her mouse a few times. "Now, the rental car from BTV." She navigated to another website. "I've confirmed with Apex Rentals that their vans are allowed into Canada, and the non-resident insurance is included in the agreement, so we're covered there." She let out a small, annoyed sigh. "We are going to get hit with that stupid 'young driver fee' since I'm only twenty-one, but it's worth it for the freedom of having our own wheels and not have to rely on my parents or your grandparents the whole time."

She was already making a mental checklist. "So, for the border, I'll need the rental agreement in a folder with our documents. And we'll use our passports and NEXUS cards. It should be faster."

It was a far cry from the old days, from the simple, trusting waves from Jean-Guy and Beth. The post-9/11 world required paperwork, proof, and planning.

Alex watched her, his heart full. She was so capable, so thoughtful. This trip was for both of them, a return to the place that shaped her, but he knew her primary focus was on making it a good, healing experience for him. He typed a new thought. IT WILL BE GOOD TO SEE STANSTEAD AGAIN. AND TO SEE YOUR FAMILY. He paused, then added the words that held the most weight for him. AND THIS TIME... I CAN STAND UP TO SAY HELLO.

Chloé stopped typing. She turned her chair and looked at him, a playful fire lighting her hazel eyes. "Yes, you can," she said, a grin spreading across her face. "You can stand up and look my Uncle David right in the eye and tell him the Brawlers are going all the way this year. I dare you."

A slow, genuine smile spread across Alex’s face. He knew she was baiting him, the die-hard Voyageurs fan teasing the lifelong Brawlers supporter. He typed a response on his AAC.

I DON'T KNOW, CHLOÉ. WALKING INTO A HOUSE FULL OF VOYAGEURS FANS AND DECLARING THE BRAWLERS ARE THE SUPERIOR TEAM SEEMS LIKE A GOOD WAY TO START AN INTERNATIONAL INCIDENT. MAYBE I'LL STICK TO POLITELY ARGUING ABOUT KOKO-KAPSELS.

Chloé laughed, the sound full of warmth. "Okay, okay, you win. We'll be diplomatic," she said, getting up from her desk and walking over to him.

Alex pressed a sequence on his joystick. With a soft whir of motors, his chair began to transform, bringing him smoothly to his full height.

Chloé met him standing, wrapping her arms around his neck in a familiar, perfectly balanced hug. He held her close, the feeling still a quiet miracle to him.

"But for the record," she whispered into his ear, her voice a teasing murmur. "The Voyageurs are still better."

### Part 2: The Red-Eye to a New Day (Summer 2006)

The Lynnwood apartment was quiet in the late evening, the air filled with the charged energy of pre-travel anticipation. Alex and Chloé Miller did one last sweep, ensuring all the lights were off and their luggage was by the door. The trip they had meticulously planned for weeks was finally here.

"Okay," Chloé said, checking her watch. "Time to go, or we'll miss the 511."

Their journey began not with a family car, but with the quiet hiss of a Puget Sound Transit bus arriving at the Lynnwood Transit Center. They navigated the familiar route: the express Route 511 to Downtown Seattle, then a smooth transfer to the Route 194 that took them directly to the airport. It was a journey they had made separately for classes, but doing it together, with luggage, bound for an adventure, felt different—a quiet declaration of their independence.

The red-eye flight from Seattle was a blur of darkened cabins and the low drone of jet engines. Their layover at Washington Reagan (DCA) in the hazy pre-dawn hours was a welcome one; the smaller, calmer terminal was a far cry from the overwhelming chaos of a larger hub. They grabbed much-needed coffee and boarded the smaller regional jet for the final hop to Burlington.

When they landed at BTV, the green Vermont mountains greeting them under a bright morning sky, a new kind of logistical challenge awaited. Alex’s new power wheelchair, a symbol of his incredible new freedom, could not be collapsed and stowed in the trunk of a standard sedan.

Waiting for them at the Apex Rentals counter was an agent who led them out to the special vehicle pickup area. There sat a full-sized van, its interior seats reconfigured to accommodate a wheelchair, a hydraulic lift folded neatly into the side door.

"We had to bring this one in special for you from our Albany location," the agent explained kindly as Chloé signed the final paperwork, including the authorization to take the vehicle into Canada and the painful "young driver fee."

Chloé took the keys, a new sense of responsibility settling on her. She operated the lift, the platform lowering to the ground with a soft whir. Alex expertly maneuvered his chair onto the platform, which then smoothly raised him into the van, where he locked his chair into place. It was a slow, deliberate process, but it was a world of freedom compared to the difficult, manual transfers of the past.

With Alex secure and Chloé behind the wheel, they pulled out of the airport. The two-hour drive north on Interstate 91 was a journey through memory.

"It's so much greener than I remember," Alex's device commented as they passed rolling hills and dense forests.

"Everything's greener when you're not a nervous fifteen-year-old," Chloé replied with a laugh. "Last time we were on this road, all our parents were with us."

THIS TIME, WE’RE THE ADULTS, Alex stated, a note of quiet wonder in his synthesized voice.

"Scary thought, eh?" Chloé grinned.

The landscape grew more familiar, the exits for Newport and the signs for the Canadian border appearing. The easy, casual world they remembered from six years ago was gone, replaced by a more formal, post-9/11 reality they were now prepared for. With her folder of documents—their NEXUS cards, the rental agreement, the letter of permission from Apex—on the passenger seat, Chloé took the exit for Stanstead. The small customs plaza, more built-up than in their memories, loomed ahead. She rolled down the window, the crisp, clean air of the Eastern Townships filling the van, and pulled up to the booth, ready to begin their triumphant return.

### Part 3: Clearing Customs. Bienvenue au Canada! (Summer 2006)

Chloé pulled the rented van up to the Canadian customs booth, a modern, camera-adorned structure that had replaced the simple booth of her childhood memories. She rolled down the window, a practiced calm on her face, and waited. A professional, uniformed Canada Border Services Agency (CBSA) officer with a polite but neutral expression leaned towards the window.

"*Bonjour.* Passports, please," the officer said in standard French.

"*Bonjour, Monsieur,*" Chloé replied, her Québécois French warm and natural. "*Voici nos documents.*" (Hello, sir. Here are our documents.)

She handed over the prepared folder. Inside were her Canadian passport, Alex’s U.S. passport, and both of their NEXUS cards. Tucked behind them were the rental agreement and the official letter from Apex Rentals authorizing cross-border travel.

The officer took the documents, his eyes professionally scanning the passports. He looked at Chloé, then at Alex in the passenger seat. He turned his attention to his computer screen, scanning their passports. It was Alex's turn to speak. He had his AAC device ready.

"*Bonjour, Monsieur. C'est notre première visite depuis que nous étions adolescents,*" Alex's device voiced, the Québécois French flowing with a perfect, natural-sounding cadence from his new, high-quality synthesizer. "*Nous sommes très heureux d'être de retour.*" (Hello, sir. This is our first visit since we were teenagers. We are very happy to be back.)

The CBSA officer looked up from his screen, genuinely surprised. It was rare to hear such fluent, regionally specific French from a U.S. passport holder, and rarer still from an AAC device. A flicker of professional curiosity and respect replaced his neutral expression.

"*Ah, bienvenue de nouveau, Monsieur,*" the officer replied, his own French becoming a little warmer, a little more colloquial. "*Votre français est impeccable. Vous parlez comme un gars de la place.*" (Ah, welcome back, sir. Your French is impeccable. You speak like a local guy.)

Alex’s face lit up with a small, proud smile. Chloé beamed, giving his arm a quick, delighted squeeze. MERCI, MONSIEUR, Alex’s device replied. MA FEMME EST MA MEILLEURE PROFESSEURE. (Thank you, sir. My wife is my best teacher.)

The officer chuckled, glancing at Chloé. "I can see that." His tone reverted to the familiar, professional rhythm of the border. "*Et le véhicule?*" (And the vehicle?)

"*C'est une location de Burlington,*" Chloé answered. "*Voici le contrat et la lettre d'autorisation.*" (It's a rental from Burlington. Here is the contract and the letter of authorization.) She indicated the other documents in the folder. The officer gave them a quick, practiced glance, confirming everything was in order.

"*D'accord. Où allez-vous aujourd'hui?*" (Alright. Where are you headed today?)

"*On va à Stanstead, pour rendre visite à ma famille, les Leclercs,*" Chloé said. (We're going to Stanstead, to visit my family, the Leclercs.)

"*Et combien de temps restez-vous au Canada?*" (And how long are you staying in Canada?)

"Pour toute la semaine. Nous avons une chambre à l'hôtel ici à Stanstead, et nous prévoyons de visiter la famille et de monter à Montréal pour quelques jours." (For the whole week. We have a hotel room here in Stanstead, and we're planning to visit family and go up to Montreal for a few days.)

"Avez-vous de l'alcool, du tabac, des armes à feu ou du cannabis à déclarer?" (Do you have any alcohol, tobacco, firearms, or cannabis to declare?)

"Non, monsieur, rien du tout." (No, sir, nothing at all.)

The officer nodded, satisfied. He handed the folder with all their documents back to Chloé. The formal, post-9/11 procedure was complete, but it had been warmed by a moment of genuine human and linguistic connection.

"*Eh bien, tout est en ordre. Profitez bien de votre visite avec la famille,*" he said, his smile returning. "*Bienvenue au Canada.*" (Well, everything is in order. Enjoy your visit with the family. Welcome to Canada.)

With a final nod, he waved them through. As Chloé drove into the familiar streets of Stanstead, a wave of triumphant emotion washed over them both. They had done it. Their first border crossing as adults, on their own, navigating a new world of rules and procedures. And they had done it perfectly, in their shared language, as a team.

### Part 4: An Aunt's Welcome and a Modern Warning (Summer 2006)

Chloé navigated the familiar streets of Stanstead, a fond smile on her face, and pulled the large rental van into her aunt and uncle's driveway. The front door of the cheerful house flew open and Geneviève Leclerc rushed out, her face beaming with pure delight.

"*Mes amours! Vous êtes enfin arrivés!*" she cried, enveloping Chloé in a fierce, loving hug the moment she stepped out of the van. (My loves! You've finally arrived!)

Her warm gaze then fell on Alex as Chloé operated the van's lift. Geneviève's eyes widened in amazement as he emerged, not in the manual chair she remembered, but in his sleek, new power chair. "Alex! My goodness, look at you! So handsome!"

Alex smiled, his AAC device voicing a warm greeting in Québécois French. BONJOUR, TANTE GENEVIÈVE. IT IS SO GOOD TO SEE YOU. (Hello, Aunt Geneviève.)

After another round of welcoming hugs, they were all ushered into the familiar, cozy kitchen. As Geneviève poured them glasses of lemonade, she asked about their crossing.

"It was smooth," Chloé said. "The officer was very nice. We told him we were here for the week to visit you and to see Montreal."

Geneviève nodded, her expression becoming a little more serious as she set the glasses on the table. "*Bon. C'est bien. Mais, il y a juste une petite chose, mes amours,*" she began, her tone a mix of love and caution. (Good. That's fine. But, there's just one little thing, my loves.)

She looked at them both, her gaze direct. "You must remember, it is not like the old days. When you tell the officer at the border you are here for the week, they take that very seriously now. You cannot just... pop over to Derby Line for a snack like you used to do when you were kids. You must stay on the Canadian side for your whole visit."

She sighed, a sound of fond resignation for a world that was gone. "If you cross back to America, even for five minutes, you have ended your official 'visit' to Canada. To come back again, you would have to go through the whole process, all the questions, all over again. It's a big headache. No more 'one town' feeling, eh?"

Chloé nodded, a flicker of sadness in her eyes. "I know, Tante. We're prepared. Everything in the folder, just in case."

Alex, who had been listening intently, typed a response on his device. I REMEMBER, his synthesized voice said, a note of melancholy in the tone. IT IS A SHAME. I LIKED THE 'ONE TOWN'.

"*Moi aussi, mon cher. Moi aussi,*" Geneviève said, patting his hand. (Me too, my dear. Me too.) She then brightened, shaking off the somber mood. "*Mais!* You are here now, for a whole week! We will have a wonderful time. Now, tell me everything! What are your plans?"

The new reality was set. Their visit, unlike the fluid, borderless adventure of their youth, would be confined to one country. But within that country, a week of new memories with family awaited them.

### Part 5: An Aunt's Advice and a New Itinerary (Summer 2006)

After the warm, welcoming hugs in the driveway, Aunt Geneviève ushered Alex and Chloé into her cozy kitchen, a space that smelled faintly of yeast and cinnamon. As she poured them tall glasses of cold lemonade, she settled at the table with them, her expression full of genuine happiness.

"*Alors, mes amours*, tell me everything," she said in her warm Québécois French. "You are here for a whole week! What are your plans?"

Chloé, feeling a rush of nostalgia and excitement, leaned forward. "Oh, we have so many ideas! We were thinking tomorrow morning, we could drive over to Newport for breakfast at that little diner Dad always liked, then maybe come back here for the afternoon to see the museum. And Tuesday, I want to show Alex my old house in Derby Line again..."

Geneviève listened patiently, a fond but sad smile touching her lips. When Chloé paused for breath, she reached out and gently patted her hand.

"*Ma chérie,*" she began softly, "that sounds like a wonderful trip... from the old days. But you cannot do that anymore."

Chloé blinked, confused. "What do you mean?"

"The border, it is not a revolving door now," Geneviève explained gently. "When you told the officer you are here for a week, they logged it. In their computer. Your visit has started. If you cross back to America tomorrow for breakfast, even for one hour, your 'visit to Canada' is over. To come back, you would have to go through the whole process again, with all the questions. They are very, very strict now. You must stay on the Canadian side for your visit."

The reality of the new rules hit Chloé with a sudden, frustrating thud. "Oh," she said, her enthusiasm deflating. "I... I didn't realize it was that strict. I thought for locals, just for a quick trip..."

"*Non, ma belle.* Not anymore. That world is gone," her aunt said with a sad shake of her head.

A disappointed silence fell over the table. All of Chloé’s fluid, easy plans of weaving back and forth between her two hometowns, just as she had when she was a girl, were impossible.

Alex, who had been listening intently, watched the disappointment on Chloé's face. He reached for his AAC and began to type, his synthesized voice a calm, logical presence in the quiet kitchen. CHLOÉ. IT’S OKAY. WE CAN MAKE A NEW PLAN. WHAT IF WE DO ALL THE CANADA THINGS FIRST? MONTREAL, THE TOWNSHIPS, SEEING EVERYONE HERE. THEN, ON OUR LAST DAY, WE OFFICIALLY LEAVE CANADA, AND SPEND THE WHOLE DAY ON THE AMERICAN SIDE BEFORE WE DRIVE TO THE AIRPORT.

Chloé looked at him, then at the map on the table, a new idea sparking. "You're right," she said, her practical nature taking over. She turned to her aunt. "Okay, Tante Geneviève, what about this? We spend from now until Thursday morning here in Québec. We'll do Montreal, we'll drive around the Townships, and we'll have the big family dinner. Then, on Thursday, we have our last breakfast with you, say our goodbyes, and officially cross back into the U.S. That gives us all of Thursday to be tourists in Derby Line and Newport. We can stay the night in Burlington Thursday, and then we have all day Friday before our flight leaves."

Aunt Geneviève beamed, her warm smile returning. "*Voilà!*" she exclaimed, clapping her hands lightly. "Now that is a good plan! A smart compromise. You see? You can still visit both your worlds. You just have to do it one country at a time."

### Part 6: A Sweet Discovery (Summer 2006)

After a wonderful breakfast, Alex and Chloé said a temporary goodbye to Aunt Geneviève, promising to see her and the family for dinner later that week. Back in their rental van, with their luggage in the back, Chloé checked the time on her phone.

"Well, it's just after ten in the morning," she said. "Our check-in at the hotel isn't until three this afternoon. We've got about five hours to kill. What do you want to do, Mr. Miller?"

Alex thought for a moment, a slow grin spreading across his face as he typed on his AAC. I THINK, MRS. MILLER, THAT WE SHOULD BEGIN OUR TRIP THE SAME WAY WE ENDED OUR LAST ONE. WITH A QUEST FOR FORBIDDEN KNOWLEDGE.

Chloé laughed, catching his meaning immediately. "To the dépanneur it is! A pilgrimage to the site of the great Koko-Kapsel."

The Stanstead corner store was just as they remembered it—a charmingly cluttered haven of snacks and local goods. They browsed the aisles for a few minutes before inevitably making their way to the candy section near the register. There, in a familiar, brightly colored display box, were the classic Koko-Kapsels, the foil-wrapped chocolate eggs containing the little toys that were strictly forbidden south of the border.

They both looked at them, sharing a silent, amused memory of their teenage selves learning about the strange international law.

"Still tempting," Chloé murmured, picking one up and feeling its weight before placing it carefully back.

IT’S A SHAME, Alex’s device voiced. I’VE ALWAYS WONDERED WHAT THE CHOCOLATE TASTES LIKE.

"Oh, it's the best," Chloé said wistfully. "The creamiest milk chocolate. I used to eat them all the time when I was a kid. I'd just leave the toy at Tante Geneviève's house so I wouldn't get in trouble bringing it home."

"Wait a second," she said, her eyes lighting up. She pointed to a bright blue bag hanging on a hook right next to the display. The logo was the same, but the name was different: Koko-Bons. The bag showed a picture of small, bite-sized chocolate eggs. Chloé grabbed the bag and read the description aloud in her fluent Québécois French.

"'Bouchées de chocolat au lait, fourrées d'une crème au lait et aux noisettes'," she translated. "'Bite-sized milk chocolate eggs, filled with a milk and hazelnut cream.' Alex... there's no toy. It's just the chocolate!"

A look of genuine delight dawned on Alex's face. This was a revelation. A loophole, provided by the company itself.

SO… WE CAN ACTUALLY BUY THESE ONES? he typed, his synthesized voice full of hope. AND TAKE THEM HOME TO THE STATES?

"Yes! They're totally legal! There's no toy!" Chloé said, her face lighting up with triumph. She reached for a bag, then paused, her practical, cautious nature taking over as she thought the process through. A flicker of doubt crossed her face. "But... you know what? Let's not risk it."

WHY NOT? Alex typed, confused. YOU SAID THEY ARE LEGAL.

"They are," Chloé affirmed, "but imagine trying to explain the subtle difference between 'Koko-Kapsels' and 'Koko-Bons' to a U.S. border officer who's having a bad day. All they'll see is a chocolate egg with the 'Koko' brand name. It's not worth the hassle or the risk of them wanting to pull the van apart just to prove a point. These will have to be a 'Canada-only' treat for us."

Alex nodded, understanding immediately. She was right. The potential stress was not worth the candy.

"But," Chloé added, a mischievous grin returning, "that doesn't mean we can't enjoy them now."

She grabbed a single bag of the Koko-Bons and put it in their small shopping basket. They left the store a few minutes later and sat on a nearby park bench. Chloé tore open the bag and handed one of the small, solid chocolate eggs to Alex. He tried it. The creamy, rich milk chocolate with the smooth hazelnut filling was unlike any American candy he’d ever had. It was delicious.

"See?" Chloé said, watching his happy reaction. "Isn't it good?"

It was a small victory, but a deeply satisfying one. Alex finally got to taste a piece of Chloé's Canadian childhood, and they had done it by understanding that some things, even when technically legal, were best enjoyed and left right where you find them, respecting the unyielding reality of the border line.

### Part 6: A Walk Through a Changed Town (Summer 2006)

After finishing their Koko-Bons on the park bench, a sense of satisfaction settled over them. They had successfully navigated their first Canadian errand as adults.

"So," Chloé said, checking the time. "Still four and a half hours to kill before we can check in. What's next on the grand tour?"

SHOW ME MORE OF YOUR CHILDHOOD, Alex typed. WHERE DID YOU GO TO SCHOOL?

Chloé’s face lit up. "Great idea! Come on."

They drove a few blocks to a handsome, older brick building surrounded by a chain-link fence: École des Pionniers. The grounds were quiet and empty for the summer. Chloé parked the van, and they got out. Alex used the standing feature of his new chair, allowing him to see easily over the fence as Chloé stood beside him.

"This was it," she said softly, a wave of nostalgia in her voice. "My whole world from kindergarten to second grade. That was my classroom window, right there. And see that big maple tree? That's where we'd play tag. It all seems so much smaller now." She shared a few more stories, her memories painting a vivid picture of her childhood for Alex, who listened intently, absorbing another piece of the puzzle that made her who she was.

After a while, their stomachs started to rumble. "Okay," Chloé said. "I know just the place. There's an old casse-croute (snack bar) my parents used to take me to. I hope it's still here."

It was. Lunch was a simple, delicious affair of steamed hot dogs (steamies), french fries, and Habitant Cola at a small, family-run diner that hadn't changed in twenty years.

With a couple of hours still left, Chloé had one more stop. "Time for the main event," she said. "The library."

She parked the van on Rue Church, just as her family had done six years ago. The stately building of The Stanstead-Derby Line Free Library and Arts Centre stood before them. But the atmosphere was subtly different. A Canadian Border Services Agency (CBSA) vehicle was parked unobtrusively on their side of the street, and across the invisible line, a U.S. Border Patrol SUV was visible near the American entrance.

"Okay," Chloé said, her voice dropping slightly as she grabbed a small bag containing their passports and NEXUS cards. "The rules are different now. We can still walk across the American sidewalk to get to the front door, but it's a known exception and they watch it like hawks. We have to have our documents on us. Just act normal, and don't be surprised if we get stopped."

They got out of the van. The simple act of walking towards the corner of Caswell Avenue now felt charged with a tension that hadn't existed before. As they stepped onto the American sidewalk, a uniformed U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officer who had been standing near the entrance politely but firmly held up a hand.

"Afternoon, folks," he said, his tone professional. "Just doing some spot checks today. May I see your travel documents, please?"

"Of course, officer," Chloé replied, her voice steady. She handed over her U.S. Passport and her NEXUS card. Alex presented his U.S. passport and NEXUS card as well.
