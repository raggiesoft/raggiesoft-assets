# Book: No Boundaries

## Chapter 1: The Weight of Care, Revisited

### Part 1: The Accident (Spring 2010)

The winter of 2010 had been dominated by their trip to Québec and the now-legendary hockey bet that had resulted in Alex's "flight of shame." Back in their Lynnwood apartment, life had settled into its familiar, intense rhythm of full-time jobs and the all-consuming after-hours work of planning the Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center.

The accident, when it happened, was mundane and completely unexpected. On a rainy Saturday afternoon in early spring, Chloé was carrying a heavy box of donated textbooks for a fundraising book drive down the covered but slick concrete steps of their apartment building's exterior walkway. Her foot slipped on a wet patch. She tried to catch herself, but the weight of the box threw her off balance. She went down hard, her **left ankle** twisting beneath her with a sickening, audible snap—a painful, ironic mirror to the right ankle fracture she had suffered as a teenager.

### Part 2: A Different Kind of Hospital Visit

The ride in the ambulance to the hospital was a surreal, terrifying echo of a memory from a decade prior. This time, however, the roles were reversed. It was Chloé on the gurney, her face pale with pain. But Alex was not beside her. His large, complex power wheelchair was too big to fit in a standard ambulance.

As the paramedics loaded Chloé into the back, Alex was left on the walkway, a wave of frustrated helplessness washing over him. He quickly typed on his AAC, the volume turned up to its maximum. "I'LL MEET YOU THERE!" the device blared, the synthesized voice a raw shout of its own. "I PROMISE!"

He watched the ambulance pull away, its sirens wailing, and then his mind snapped into a mode of pure, focused logistics. An accessible taxi would take too long to arrange. He couldn't legally drive. There was only one option. He pulled out his phone, his fingers flying as he brought up the Puget Sound Transit bus schedule. He had to get to the hospital. Now.

The journey on the city bus was an agonizing ordeal of its own. Every stop, every traffic light felt like an eternity. He finally arrived at the hospital, wheeling himself into the emergency room just as Chloé was being taken back for X-rays.

In the ER, the familiar, sterile environment brought back a flood of painful memories for both of them: the agonizing wait, the grim-faced doctors, and the cold, unyielding wall of hospital policy that had separated them when he had pneumonia.

But this time, it was different.

When a radiology technician came to take Chloé for X-rays, he looked at Alex. "Sir, for radiation safety, you'll need to wait out here. We'll have her back in just a few minutes."

Alex nodded, giving Chloé's hand a reassuring squeeze. This separation felt completely different from the one in the pediatric wing. That had been a blanket policy that excluded him. This was a **procedural necessity** based on safety. He waited in the hallway, his presence just outside the lead-lined door a quiet, constant support.

Later, after the doctor confirmed the severe fracture would require surgery, a nurse came to prep Chloé. "Sir," she said to Alex, "you can wait in our surgical waiting area. The doctor will come find you there as soon as the procedure is over."

As he sat alone in the quiet surgical waiting room, the full, profound weight of their decision from six years ago settled over him. He remembered the terror of being in the pediatric waiting room, a helpless teenager desperate for any scrap of information. Now, he was here, unquestioned, his presence a legal right. He was not a "visitor"; he was her husband, her next-of-kin, the designated point of contact. Their platonic marriage, born from a practical need, had just revealed its most powerful, immediate truth: it was a shield. It was a key. It was the legal tool that ensured that in a moment of crisis, they would never, ever be truly separated again.

He looked at the simple gold band on his own finger. The last, lingering shadow of Vera's attempt to poison the word "marriage" for him vanished in that moment, replaced by a wave of pure, unadulterated gratitude. *This*, he thought, *this is why we did it. This is what it's really for.*

### Part 3: The Long Road Ahead

Chloé's surgery was successful, but the prognosis was more complicated than they had hoped. "It was a complex fracture," the orthopedic surgeon explained to Alex in the consultation room. "The good news is, we were able to set it perfectly. The bad news is, the healing process is going to be long. We're looking at eight to ten weeks of absolutely no weight-bearing, followed by extensive physical therapy. It's going to be a while before she's back on her feet."

The news was a heavy blow. This wasn't a simple six-week inconvenience; it was a long-term challenge. When Chloé was discharged, the reality of their new situation became starkly clear. Their apartment, though accessible for Alex, presented new hurdles for Chloé. Her primary mode of transportation would be a rented **electric wheelchair**, a practical necessity given the hilly terrain of Lynnwood and Alex's inability to push a manual chair.

During a tearful video call with their four parents, Emily Miller, ever the practical one, voiced the concern they were all feeling. "Alex, honey, we know you'll take wonderful care of her. But there are some things... like helping her in and out of the shower, or with transfers... that you physically can't manage on your own. It's not safe for either of you."

"Your mother is right," Thomas Mason agreed. "We need to get some professional help in there."

And so, a new member was introduced into their small, self-contained world: a home health nurse named Patricia, a kind, capable woman who would come for a few hours each morning to assist Chloé with the physical tasks Alex couldn't perform, primarily bathing and dressing.

### Part 4: Setback One - The Infection

The first few weeks of recovery were a frustrating grind. Alex was a whirlwind of focused, adaptive energy, taking over all the household chores and acting as Chloé's unwavering logistical support. But about three weeks after her surgery, a new complication arose.

"Alex, my ankle is killing me," Chloé said one evening, her voice tight with a pain that was different from the deep ache of a healing bone. "It's hot, and it's throbbing."

A trip back to the doctor confirmed their fears: a post-surgical infection had set in around the incision site. The news was a major blow. It meant a course of powerful antibiotics that left her feeling nauseous and even more fatigued. More importantly, it delayed the start of her physical therapy, pushing her recovery timeline back by several weeks. The light at the end of the tunnel had just gotten much, much further away.

### Part 5: Setback Two - The Catastrophe

Weeks later, the infection was finally cleared. Chloé, now more than two months into her recovery, was finally given the green light to begin partial weight-bearing physical therapy. She went into her first session full of a desperate, fragile hope.

The reality was brutal. Her ankle was incredibly stiff, the muscles in her leg had atrophied, and the first attempt to put even a little weight on it sent a sharp, terrifying pain through her foot. The physical therapist was patient, guiding her through the simple exercises at the parallel bars.

"Just a little more, Chloé," the therapist encouraged. "You're doing great."

Gritting her teeth, Chloé tried to take another small, supported step. But her weakened leg trembled, her ankle buckled, and in a split second of instability, she stumbled. She reached for the parallel bars, but her grip slipped. She went down.

The sound of her own horrified scream was followed by a sickening, familiar crack. The pain was immediate, white-hot, and a thousand times worse than the first time. She had fallen. She had re-broken the same ankle.

### Part 6: The Depths of Despair

The hours that followed were a blur of pain, tears, and a crushing, all-consuming despair. Another surgery was required, this one more complex, to repair the new damage. When Chloé woke up in the hospital recovery room, with Alex's worried face swimming into view, the first thing she felt was not relief, but a profound, bottomless hopelessness.

"It's over," she sobbed, the words torn from her in a raw, ragged whisper. "Alex, it's over. I'm never going to walk again."

He tried to comfort her, his AAC device voicing words of love and reassurance, but she was inconsolable. The recovery clock had been reset to zero, but this time, it was accompanied by a new, terrifying fear that her body was fundamentally broken beyond repair.

### Part 7: The Slow Path to Healing

The second recovery was a long, arduous journey, not just for Chloé's body, but for her spirit. The despair from that day in the physical therapy room lingered for weeks, a heavy shadow over their apartment.

Alex's role shifted again. He was no longer just a caregiver; he was her cheerleader, her motivator, her unwavering source of hope when she had none. He celebrated every tiny milestone—the first time she could wiggle her toes, the first pain-free night of sleep—with a joy that was infectious. He was the one who, months later, sat with her through every grueling, painful physical therapy session, his steady presence a silent promise that she was not alone in this fight.

Slowly, painstakingly, she began to heal. The journey was measured in millimeters of movement, in single, tentative steps between the parallel bars. And finally, after a recovery that had stretched for the better part of a year, she took her first, shaky, unaided steps across their living room floor, collapsing into Alex's waiting, open arms in a hug of pure, tearful, triumphant relief.

### Part 8: The Hospital Revelation

The true turning point, the moment that redefined everything, had come during that dark, hopeless time in the hospital after her second surgery. Alex was sitting by her bed, just watching over her, his expression a mixture of exhaustion and profound, unwavering concern.

In that moment of profound vulnerability, Chloé had looked at him, at the man who had navigated a city in a panic just to be with her, who had been her rock, her advocate, her husband. And the last, carefully maintained wall in her heart, the one that had separated their deep, platonic love from a full, romantic one, had simply crumbled.

"Alex," she had whispered, her voice thick with this new, overwhelming realization. "I love you. And I don't just mean as my best friend. I'm... I'm *in love* with you."

A slow, brilliant, tearful smile had spread across Alex's face. He had reached for his device, his fingers moving with a speed born of a year of pent-up emotion.

CHLOÉ. I AM SO IN LOVE WITH YOU, his device had voiced, the words a perfect, joyous echo of her own. I HAVE BEEN... SINCE THAT NIGHT IN THE SPORTS BAR IN MONTREAL. WHEN I LOST THE BET.

Chloé had stared, her heart swelling. "Since then? Why didn't you say anything?"

BECAUSE I WAS SCARED, he had typed honestly. I WASN'T SURE IF YOU FELT THE SAME. BUT THAT NIGHT... I REALIZED I WAS COMPLETELY SAFE WITH YOU. SAFE ENOUGH FOR ANYTHING. EVEN SAFE ENOUGH TO WEAR THAT UNIFORM OF SHAME. A playful glint had entered his eyes. AND YOU KNOW WHAT? I ACTUALLY LIKED THE CLOTHES. THE FLEECE PANTS ARE REALLY WARM. AND THAT RED AND WHITE BEANIE... IT'S A REALLY GOOD HAT.

Chloé’s tearful, emotional expression had instantly morphed into one of mock, theatrical horror. "A *what*?"

Alex had paused, a flicker of understanding, then pure mischief, crossing his face. The American word had slipped out on autopilot, his natural term for it. But seeing her reaction, he decided, with a grin, that this was a hill worth dying on. A BEANIE, his device had repeated, the synthesized voice now dripping with a playful, deliberate tease. IT'S A GREAT BEANIE.

"It is a **TOQUE**, you uncivilized American!" she had declared, a weak but genuine laugh bubbling up out of her. "Blasphemy! Sacrilege! In my own hospital bed!" Even now, in a moment of profound emotional vulnerability, her Canadian pride was unyielding. This, she knew with absolute certainty, was a linguistic hill she would happily die on for the rest of her life.

The nurse, a woman with a kind face and a familiar, lilting accent who had been quietly checking Chloé's IV drip, looked up from her task, a quiet chuckle escaping her. "Sorry to interrupt," she had said, her eyes twinkling. "But I couldn't help but overhear. And as a proud daughter of Saskatoon, Saskatchewan, I have to side with the patient on this one. It's a toque, eh?"

Alex and Chloé had both looked at the nurse, then at each other, and burst into a shared, happy laugh.

He had just grinned, his heart soaring. He had reached out and taken her hand again. I LOVE YOU, CHLOÉ. MY WIFE. MY BEST FRIEND. MY GIRLFRIEND.

"I love you too, Alex," she had whispered, her own laughter mixing with happy tears. "My husband. My best friend. My boyfriend."

The last boundary was gone. The final, lingering shadow of Vera's attempt to poison the concept of marriage and romance for him was swept away for good. Their platonic marriage, born of practicality and friendship, had just, in the quiet of a hospital recovery room, blossomed into a love story.

## Chapter 2: A Vision Takes Shape

### Part 1: The Blueprint of Home (Spring 2010)

The aftermath of their winter trip to Québec lingered in their Lynnwood apartment, not in the form of a humiliating Canadian hockey jersey (which had been ceremoniously folded and stored away), but in a renewed sense of purpose. The visit with Chloé's family had been a powerful reminder of their roots and the journey that had brought them together.

It was a quiet Sunday afternoon. The 3D printer was silent, and Chloé’s grant-writing textbooks were neatly stacked. They were in a rare moment of pure brainstorming, a large sheet of butcher paper spread across their dining table, the words "Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center" written at the top.

Chloé, a pen tucked behind her ear, was pacing the small room, her mind buzzing with ideas. "Okay," she began, "I was thinking. This place can't just be a generic shelter. It has to be *us*. It has to tell our story."

Alex looked up from the paper, intrigued. WHAT DO YOU MEAN? his AAC device asked.

"I mean it should be a fusion of all the places that are important to us," she explained, her passion growing. "It should feel like the Pacific Northwest, obviously, because this is our home now. But it should also have a piece of where I'm from, and where you're from. A piece of Virginia, where we grew up together."

She began to sketch on the paper, her ideas flowing. "Imagine a section modeled after the Eastern Townships, with a grove of maple and birch trees for rescued birds. We could have a small, rocky shoreline exhibit that looks just like the banks of Lake Memphremagog. And for your side," she looked at him, her eyes shining, "we could have a 'New England Coast' section, a saltwater touch-tank that feels like the tide pools in Rhode Island."

Alex caught her vision immediately, his own excitement building. He began to type, adding to the brainstorm. A VIRGINIA PIEDMONT HABITAT. FOR RESCUED TURTLES AND FOXES. LIKE THE WOODS BEHIND OUR OLD HOUSES.

"Yes! Exactly!" Chloé exclaimed.

For the next hour, they were lost in the creative storm, their shared history pouring out onto the paper in the form of habitats and exhibits. But as the page filled with sketches of different ecosystems from across the continent, a daunting reality began to set in.

Alex was the one to voice it, his practical, engineering mind assessing the scale of their dream. CHLOÉ. THIS IS... A LOT. A ZOO. IT WOULD BE HUGE. AND INCREDIBLY EXPENSIVE.

Chloé stopped pacing, looking at the sprawling, continent-spanning plan on the table. He was right. The sheer complexity was overwhelming. "You're right," she said with a small, pragmatic sigh. "It's too much to start with. We have to focus."

"Okay," she said, a new resolve in her voice. "New plan. We start with what we know now. We start with home." She drew a bold circle around the words "Pacific Northwest." "We focus on the ecosystems of the Puget Sound area. That's our primary mission. We keep the rest," she tapped the other sketches, "on the back burner. For future expansions."

Alex nodded in firm agreement. It was the logical, achievable first step.

"But," Chloé added, a new idea sparking from the practical constraint, "that doesn't mean we can't still tell a story. What if the 'Discovery' part of the name is just as important as the 'Care' part? What if, alongside the rescue and rehab, we have a museum? A place where we can teach the public, especially kids, about the incredible wildlife and ecosystems right here in our own backyard."

A slow, brilliant smile spread across Alex's face. A museum. An educational center. It was the perfect fusion of their passions: her love for history and storytelling, and his for design and accessible information. The Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center now had a clear, focused mission. It wouldn't just be a shelter; it would be a classroom, a place of learning and connection, rooted in the beauty of the Pacific Northwest. Their dream now had a solid foundation.

### Part 2: A Border of Our Own (Spring 2010)

The decision to focus on the Pacific Northwest brought a new clarity to their brainstorming session. The sprawling, continent-wide plan was reined in, replaced by a manageable, local mission. Chloé sat back down at the table, a thoughtful expression on her face as she looked at the newly circled "Pacific Northwest" section of their blueprint.

"Okay," she said, tapping her pen. "So we focus here. The Puget Sound, the Cascade Mountains, the temperate rainforests..." She paused, her mind clearly picturing the map. "But you know, Alex, the Pacific Northwest... it doesn't just stop at the 49th parallel."

Alex looked up, catching her meaning immediately. The border. It was always there, a line that had defined her past and was now shaping their future.

"The ecosystems we have here in Washington," Chloé continued, a new excitement building in her voice, "they don't just end at the border. They continue right up into British Columbia. It's all one cohesive region, the Salish Sea. To only show the Washington side... it's like only telling half the story."

She looked at him, her eyes shining with the power of the idea. "What if that's what makes our Center special? What if we embrace that? What if we're proudly, intentionally, both American and Canadian? We could have a habitat for the Olympic marmot from right here in Washington right next to a habitat for the Vancouver Island marmot. We could show how the salmon swim in rivers that cross the border without a passport."

A slow, brilliant smile spread across Alex's face. This was it. This was the idea that tied everything together. Their personal history wasn't a "back burner" idea for a future expansion; it was the very soul of their mission. They would build their own version of Derby Line and Stanstead, a place where the border was a line of connection, not division.

ONE ECOSYSTEM. TWO COUNTRIES, his AAC device voiced, the words a perfect summary of their new mission statement. IT'S OUR STORY.

"It's our story," Chloé echoed, her voice full of a quiet, powerful conviction. She picked up a pen and, next to the bold circle around the Pacific Northwest, she drew two flags—a small American one and a small Canadian one, side by side.

The Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center now had its unique identity. It wouldn't just be a local animal rescue; it would be a cross-border institution, a testament to a shared ecosystem and a celebration of the invisible lines that connect, rather than divide.

## Chapter 3: The Northern Annex

### Part 1: The Peace Arch Crossing (2010)

The early Saturday morning air was cool and misty as Alex and Chloé loaded their weekend bags into their car. This trip felt different. Their last journey to Canada had been a high-stakes, emotional family affair centered around a hockey game. This trip was just for them. It was a romantic weekend getaway, but it was also their first official research mission for the Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center.

"Okay," Chloé said, checking a printout from the transit website. "To avoid the hassle of downtown Vancouver parking, we'll park at the Scott Road SkyLink Station Park & Ride in Surrey, just over the border. From there, the whole city is open to us."

The drive north on I-5 from Lynnwood was easy and familiar. They followed the signs for the Peace Arch, a grand, white monument standing serene amidst manicured gardens. In the NEXUS lane, the wait was short. As they pulled up to the Canadian customs booth, a CBSA officer greeted them with a polite, bilingual "*Bonjour, Hello*."

Chloé’s eyes lit up. It was the invitation she always looked for outside of Québec. She immediately switched to her native language. "*Bonjour, Monsieur,*" she replied, her Québécois French warm and clear as she gathered their documents. "***On a tout préparé pour vous.***" (Translation for the reader: "Hello, sir. We have everything ready for you.")

She handed over her credentials: her **Canadian Passport**, her **NEXUS card**, and her **Washington State Enhanced Driver's License**. Alex, following her lead, presented his **U.S. Passport**, his **NEXUS card**, and his **Washington State Enhanced ID card**. While they knew they only needed one WHTI-compliant document each, being over-prepared was second nature to them.

The officer, a bit surprised but pleased by her fluent French, took the offered documents and switched languages as well. "*Merci. Alors, le but de votre visite aujourd'hui?*" (Thank you. So, the purpose of your visit today?)

"*Juste un petit week-end touristique à Vancouver,*" Chloé explained. "*Et un peu de recherche pour un projet.*" (Just a little tourist weekend in Vancouver. And a bit of research for a project.)

The officer nodded, processing their documents. Alex, using his AAC device, added in perfect Québécois French, NOUS ALLONS VOIR LES HABITATS DES ANIMAUX AU PARC STANLEY ET À LA MONTAGNE GROUSE. (We're going to see the animal habitats at Stanley Park and Grouse Mountain.)

"*Ah, pour un projet? Très intéressant,*" the officer said, his curiosity piqued. He asked a few more standard questions, and then handed back their documents with a genuine smile. "*Eh bien, profitez bien de votre fin de semaine. Bienvenue au Canada.*" (Well, enjoy your weekend. Welcome to Canada.)

After clearing customs, they drove the short distance to the park-and-ride, left their car, and walked to the elevated platform of the Scott Road SkyLink station. For the first time, they were about to explore the Canadian side of their Pacific Northwest home, not just as tourists, but as dreamers gathering blueprints for their future. The sleek, automated SkyLink train pulled into the station, its doors hissing open, ready to carry them into the heart of the city.

### Part 2: A Day of Coasts and Crafts (2010)

The automated SkyLink train glided smoothly out of Scott Road Station, lifting them above the suburban landscape of Surrey and offering glimpses of the distant mountains. For Alex and Chloé, it was the beginning of their immersive research mission.

"Okay," Chloé said, consulting the transit map she'd printed. "First, we head downtown to Waterfront Station. From there, we catch a bus to Stanley Park. That's where the big show is."

The journey was a seamless showcase of the Metro Vancouver Transit Authority's accessibility. The transition from the SkyLink to the \#19 bus was easy, and soon they were winding through the massive, temperate rainforest of Stanley Park. They disembarked near their first major stop.

"This is the heart of it, Alex," Chloé said, her voice full of a quiet reverence as they looked at the dense canopy of ancient cedars and Douglas firs. "This is the B.C. coastal habitat we want to learn from."

They spent the next few hours at the **Pacific Marine Science Centre**, a world-class facility. They were less interested in the tropical fish and more fascinated by the local exhibits. They stood for a long time at the massive Strait of Georgia underwater viewing window, watching rockfish and sturgeon native to the Salish Sea. They took detailed notes and photos at the sea otter habitat, studying the design of the enclosure and the enrichment activities provided for the intelligent, playful mammals. It was a masterclass in the kind of ethical, engaging habitat design they dreamed of creating.

After the science centre, they took the bus to their next destination: Granville Island. The place was a vibrant, chaotic feast for the senses. They navigated the bustling public market, the air thick with the smells of fresh produce, artisanal cheeses, and baked goods. They found a small, accessible food stall selling fresh fish and chips, which they ate at a picnic table overlooking the sparkling waters of False Creek, watching the tiny, bathtub-like Harbour Ferries zip back and forth.

As the afternoon began to fade, they browsed the artisan workshops, admiring the work of glassblowers and potters. It was a perfect, inspiring day, but they kept a close eye on the time.

THE PARKING LOT CLOSES AT 2 AM, Alex's device reminded Chloé as they started heading back towards the bus stop. WE NEED TO GET THE CAR BEFORE THEN.

"Exactly," Chloé confirmed. "And we still need to check into our hotel."

They took the bus back to a downtown SkyLink station and began the journey back to Surrey. By the time they arrived at Scott Road Station, the sun had set and the massive parking lot was beginning to empty. They retrieved their car, the first part of their mission a complete success.

"Okay," Chloé said, pulling out of the lot. "Hotel time."

She had booked them a room at an accessible hotel in Surrey, just a short drive from the transit station. It wasn't the glamour of a downtown Vancouver hotel, but it was practical and smart, perfectly suited to their research-focused, budget-conscious trip. They checked in, exhausted but buzzing with the new ideas and inspiration they had gathered for their own future centre. The Canadian annex of their dream now felt real, tangible, and full of incredible possibilities.

### Part 3: The Peak of the City (2010)

Sunday morning began with a smooth, practiced efficiency. Alex and Chloé checked out of their Surrey hotel, loaded their weekend bags into the van, and drove the short distance back to the Scott Road SkyLink Station. After parking their car for the day, they were back on the MVTA system, ready for a day focused on the mountains that form a majestic backdrop to the city.

Their journey was a multi-stage transit adventure. They took the SkyLink to the bustling, fully accessible Waterfront Station, then followed the signs for the **Harbour Ferry**. The ferry, a wide, stable catamaran, offered them a stunning view of the city skyline and the iconic sails of Canada Place as it zipped across the Burrard Inlet.

"This is way better than driving," Chloé said, as Alex positioned his chair by a large window to take in the view.

On the other side, at Lonsdale Quay, they made a seamless transfer to an accessible bus that wound its way up through the hills of North Vancouver. Their first stop was the **"Lynn Canyon Salmon Hatchery."** They spent an hour there, fascinated. It was a perfect research opportunity, walking along the elevated platforms and through the indoor viewing areas, watching salmon at every stage of their life cycle, from tiny alevin to powerful adult fish fighting their way up the fish ladder. Alex took dozens of photos, his mind already designing a potential salmon run exhibit for their own future centre.

Their final destination was the main event. The bus dropped them at the base of **"Summit Peak."** They boarded the **Skyride**, a large aerial tramway that offered a breathtaking, panoramic journey up the steep mountainside. Below them, the dense green of the Douglas firs stretched out, and the city and ocean grew smaller and more spectacular with every foot they climbed.

At the summit, the air was crisp and cool. The view was even more stunning than from Mount Royal in Montreal, a sweeping vista of the entire region. But their main goal was the **Grizzly Bear Refuge**. In a large, protected, and naturalistic habitat, they watched two massive grizzly bears, Grinder and Coola, foraging and playing. They stood for a long time, observing the bears and studying the design of the ethical enclosure. It was direct, powerful inspiration for the kind of large-scale, respectful habitats they dreamed of building.

As the afternoon began to wane, they took the Skyride back down the mountain, their minds buzzing with new ideas. They retraced their path—bus, Harbour Ferry, SkyLink—arriving back at their car in Surrey as the sun began to dip towards the horizon.

They joined the line of cars for the U.S. border, the queue moving slowly through the manicured lawns and flowerbeds of Peace Arch Park. As they waited, Alex looked out the window at the grand white arch and the people strolling freely across the invisible border line within the park's boundaries.

WE DROVE RIGHT THROUGH A PARK TO GET TO THE BORDER, his AAC device commented.

"I know," Chloé said, a note of wonder in her voice. "It's the Peace Arch International Park. We should make a separate trip just to come here sometime. We could have a picnic with one foot in Canada and one in the U.S."

I’D LIKE THAT, Alex voiced, and they both smiled at the thought of a future, simpler adventure.

When they finally pulled up to the U.S. CBP booth, the officer was professional and efficient. As required, both Chloé and Alex presented their U.S. passports and NEXUS cards. The officer asked them what they were bringing back from Canada.

"Just some **smoked salmon** we bought at the Granville Island Market," Chloé replied, "and about a thousand ideas."

The officer just nodded, the declaration of smoked salmon being a common one at this particular crossing. He waved them through. They drove south on I-5, back towards Lynnwood, the successful weekend trip a solid, foundational pillar for the new, shared dream they were now more determined than ever to build.

## Chapter 4: The Revenge of the Brawlers

### Part 1: The Boston Showdown (Fall 2011)

The trip to Southern New England in the fall of 2011 was a nostalgic journey for James and Alex Miller, a chance to show the Masons the places they were from. The week culminated in the ultimate pilgrimage, a high-stakes event that had the entire family buzzing with a nervous, electric energy: a trip to the arena in Boston for Game 7 of the Federated Hockey League Championship Finals. It was the ultimate showdown: the Boston Brawlers versus the Montreal Voyageurs.

This time, the tables were turned. James and Alex were on home turf, a confident island of black and gold amidst the scattered pockets of the visiting Voyageurs fans. Chloé, Thomas, and Sophie were the brave ambassadors of the visiting team, proudly wearing their red, white, and blue jerseys.

Before the game, in the bustling concourse, Alex, with a mischievous glint in his eye, laid down the challenge. CHLOÉ, his AAC device announced, its voice clear over the arena noise. THE ULTIMATE BET. A REMATCH. LOSER WEARS THE WINNER'S COLORS FOR THE REST OF THE TRIP.

Chloé, full of confidence after the legendary 2010 victory, laughed. "Absolument!" she declared. "Get ready to wear red again, Miller!"

The game was a brutal, hard-fought, and emotionally draining battle, a perfect Game 7. It was tied heading into the final minutes of the third period. A face-off in the Voyageurs' end. The Brawlers won the draw, a quick pass, a slapshot... Goal! The Brawlers took the lead with forty seconds left. The Boston crowd erupted in a single, deafening roar that shook the building to its foundations.

This time, it was Chloé, Sophie, and Thomas who slumped in their seats in stunned, horrified disbelief. Alex and James let out a triumphant shout, high-fiving each other. The final horn sounded. Boston had won the championship.

A triumphant, almost feral grin spread across Alex's face. He turned to Chloé, who looked utterly devastated. He typed a single, perfect, merciless sentence.

TIME TO PAY UP, MRS. MILLER.

James, a look of pure, unadulterated glee on his face, reached under his seat and pulled out a large, full bag from the pro shop. "I believe this is for you, Chloé," he said, his voice dripping with a full year of pent-up satisfaction.

He pulled out a pristine Boston Brawlers jersey and held it up. Chloé groaned. But then he pulled out a pair of black and gold fleece pants. And then Brawlers socks. And finally, a black knit hat with the Brawlers logo on it.

Chloé's jaw dropped. "Oh, no," she breathed, realizing the full, pre-meditated horror of her fate. "He even got the **toque**."

James Miller, who overheard her, spun around. In his theatrical, comical outrage, his usually subdued New England accent flared up, the vowels broadening into the unmistakable, slightly less harsh tones of his native Rhode Island.

"A *what*?" he demanded, the word coming out more like 'whaht'. "Honey, I love you like a daughtah, but that is blasphemy! Sacrilege! In this holy temple of Bahston sports, that is a **BEANIE**! A Brawlahs beanie! Show some respect for the sacred headweah!"

Chloé, even in her moment of defeat, drew herself up with a look of unshakable conviction. She looked James right in the eye, a stubborn, fiery glint in her hazel eyes.

"In the civilized world, Mr. Miller," she said, her voice clear and carrying, "it's called a toque."

A group of nearby Brawlers fans, recognizing the familiar cadence of a fellow New Englander, roared with laughter and approval. "Listen to yah fathah-in-law, kid! It's a beanie!" one of them yelled in a thick Boston accent. The supportive chant immediately began. "Put the beanie on! Put the beanie on! Put the beanie on!"

Chloé sighed theatrically, the very picture of a defeated but good sport. With the entire section now watching, she slowly, painfully, pulled her beloved Voyageurs jersey off over her head, revealing her undershirt. She folded it neatly and handed it to a grim-faced Thomas, who took it with the funereal reverence of a man accepting a surrendered flag.

She then took the proffered Brawlers jersey and, to a fresh wave of triumphant and good-natured applause from the Boston fans, pulled it on.

Alex was already there with his phone, snapping a picture to immortalize her "surrender." He leaned in for the photo, throwing his arm around his newly-Brawlerized wife, his face a mask of pure victory. "It looks good on you," he voiced through his device, the deadpan delivery making even his own parents laugh.

This was the moment Alex and James had been waiting for.

"Oh, she's not a new fan!" James called out, his voice full of glee. "She's a die-hard Voyageurs fan! From Québec! She lost the bet on the final!"

Alex, savoring his victory, typed a quick, devastating rebuttal. YOU DIDN'T SEEM VERY 'VERMONT' IN THAT MONTREAL SPORTS BAR LAST YEAR WHEN YOU WERE YELLING AT ME IN FRENCH.

It was her turn to endure the teasing, to wear the uniform of shame, and to be, for one night, the most devoted-looking Boston Brawlers fan in the entire city.

### Part 2: The Boston Victory Parade (Fall 2011)

The morning after the championship win, the city of Boston was a triumphant sea of black and gold. In their hotel room, Chloé Miller stared at the Brawlers uniform of shame, laid out on a chair. "I can't believe I have to wear this in public," she groaned.

"A bet's a bet, my love," Alex's device chirped from across the room, its synthesized voice dripping with feigned sympathy.

Just then, James Miller burst into the room, his face beaming, holding a copy of the Boston Beacon. "Good news!" he announced, his voice full of glee. "They've thrown together a last-minute victory parade! It starts near the arena in two hours. We can't miss it!"

YOU DIDN'T SEEM VERY 'VERMONT' IN THAT MONTREAL SPORTS BAR LAST YEAR WHEN YOU WERE YELLING AT ME IN FRENCH.
