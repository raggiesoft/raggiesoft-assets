# Book: A Dream Built

## Chapter 1: The Great Focus

### Part 1: The Leap of Faith (Spring 2022)

The years between their trip to Boston and their late thirties settled into a rhythm of quiet, relentless work. The dream of the Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center was no longer a distant, hopeful idea; it was the consuming, central project of their lives. Their small apartment had become a dedicated command center, every spare inch of space and every spare moment of time dedicated to the mission.

They lived a frugal, purposeful life. While their friends were buying houses and taking international vacations, Alex and Chloé were living on one of their professional salaries and pouring the other, in its entirety, into the "LDCC Fund." It was a decade-long sacrifice, a slow and steady accumulation of the resources they knew they would need.

By the spring of 2022, they were both 37, and the strain was beginning to show. The burnout was a palpable thing in their small apartment. One rainy Tuesday evening, Chloé was at the dining table, surrounded by a fortress of binders and textbooks on non-profit law, a half-written grant proposal glowing on her laptop screen. She let out a long, weary sigh and rubbed her tired eyes, the words on the screen blurring together.

Across the room, Alex was at his own workstation, trying to finalize a complex 3D model of a custom-designed aquatic habitat. He had put in a full eight-hour day at his demanding tech firm job, and now, deep into the evening, he was trying to summon the creative energy for the work that truly mattered to him. He found himself staring blankly at the screen, his mind refusing to solve the intricate engineering problem before him.

He looked over at Chloé, at the slump of her shoulders, at the sheer exhaustion etched on her face. He knew he felt the same. They were burning the candle at both ends, and the flame was beginning to flicker. He typed a slow, simple, and heavy sentence on his AAC.

CHLOÉ. I CAN'T KEEP DOING BOTH.

She looked up, her expression a mirror of his own weariness. "I know," she said, her voice quiet. "Me neither. I feel like I'm giving my day job 80 percent, and the LDCC 80 percent, which means I'm running at 160 percent and failing at both."

They were at a crossroads. The dream was too big now, too real, to be a side project. It demanded all of them. The savings account, their "runway fund," was substantial, the result of a decade of disciplined sacrifice. Their business plan was a masterpiece of meticulous detail. But the fear was immense.

"If we do this," Chloé said, voicing the terrifying thought, "if we quit our jobs... that's it. The safety net is gone. It's just us and the dream, and if we fail..."

Alex maneuvered his chair over to the table and took her hand. He looked at the mountain of her work, at the complex designs on his own screen, at the decade of their shared life poured into this single, all-consuming purpose. He took a deep breath, his resolve hardening.

WE WON'T FAIL, his device voiced, the tone clear and certain. NOT TOGETHER. IT'S TIME.

Chloé looked at him, at the unwavering confidence in his eyes, and she felt her own fear recede, replaced by a surge of adrenaline. He was right. It was time.

"Okay," she whispered, a tear she didn't know was there tracing a path down her cheek. "Okay, Alex. We jump."

That Friday, they both gave their two weeks' notice. The leap of faith, a decade in the making, was taken.

### Part 2: The Grant (Late 2022)

Quitting their jobs transformed their lives. Their apartment was no longer a home where they worked on their project; it was a full-time fundraising and design studio where they also happened to sleep.

By the late fall of 2022, they were in the final, agonizing stages of submitting their first massive seed grant application to the "Cascade Family Trust," a major Seattle-based philanthropic foundation known for funding ambitious, community-focused projects. This single application was the culmination of all their years of preparation.

The scene was one of controlled chaos. Chloé was the grant writer, the architect of their story. She spent her days translating their vision into the compelling, data-driven prose the foundation required. She wrote about their mission, about the unmet need for specialized animal care, about their commitment to accessibility and education.

"Alex," she called out from the dining table, her brow furrowed in concentration. "I need the final projected water usage numbers for the river otter habitat's filtration system. For the environmental impact statement."

Alex, at his workstation, was the technical expert, the provider of the hard data that gave their dream its credibility. He pulled up a complex spreadsheet. SENDING IT TO YOU NOW, his device replied. IT INCLUDES THE RECIRCULATION EFFICIENCY STATS. WE'RE AT 98% WATER RECLAMATION.

"Perfect," she murmured, typing furiously.

Their partnership was a seamless fusion of skills. She wrote the narrative; he provided the proof. He designed the innovative, accessible habitats; she explained why they were essential. They worked for weeks, fueled by cheap coffee and a shared, unwavering belief in their mission. Finally, one rainy November afternoon, it was done. With a single, ceremonial click of the mouse, Chloé submitted the massive digital file.

An automated email confirmation appeared a moment later. "Thank you for your submission. You will be notified of the board's decision in the first quarter of next year."

A profound silence fell over the room. They had done everything they could. Now, all they could do was wait.

## Chapter 2: A Winter Homecoming

### Part 1: A Much-Needed Breather (Early December 2022)

The weeks after submitting the grant application were a unique form of torture. The intense, focused work had been replaced by an agonizing, nerve-shredding quiet. They had poured every ounce of their energy into the proposal, and now, with their fate in the hands of a committee, they were left in a state of suspended animation. The burnout from the preceding months was catching up with them, leaving them both feeling frayed and exhausted.

One evening, they were on a video call with Chloé's parents and the Leclercs, the familiar, cheerful faces a welcome distraction.

"*Alors, mes amours,*" Aunt Geneviève said, her voice full of warmth. "*Comment ça va? You look tired.*" (So, my loves, how are you?)

"We're okay, Tante," Chloé admitted with a weary smile. "Just... waiting. It's hard."

"You need a break," Sophie said, her mother's intuition cutting through the screen. "You have pushed yourselves too hard. You need to come home. Come for Christmas. To finish the trip that was cut short. Just relax with family."

The idea was a lifeline. A trip. A real break, their first since quitting their jobs. Alex and Chloé exchanged a look. It was the perfect idea.

"We'd love that," Chloé said, a genuine smile returning to her face for the first time in weeks.

As they were making plans, Uncle David boomed, "*Et bien sûr, vous resterez pour le Boxing Day!*" (And of course, you'll stay for Boxing Day!)

Chloé paused, then a look of pure, dawning realization crossed her face. She turned to Alex. "Oh my god," she said in English. "You've never been in Canada for Boxing Day, have you?"

Alex looked back, confused. BOXING DAY? LIKE... THE SPORT?

A delighted laugh erupted from the Canadian contingent on the screen. "*Non, non, mon gars!*" Uncle David roared. "It is the biggest shopping day of the year! The sales! The crowds! It is a national sport, yes, but not that kind!"

"He's right," Chloé explained, a mischievous glint in her eye. "It's like Black Friday, but after Christmas. It's a Canadian institution. Oh, Alex, you have to experience it. It's chaos. You'll love it."

The plan was set. They would fly to Montreal a few days before Christmas and stay through the 26th. The trip wasn't just a vacation anymore; it was a cultural mission.

### Part 2: The Return (December 2022)

The flight from Seattle to Montreal was blessedly, beautifully normal. Gone were the masks, the constant sanitizing, the tense quiet of a world holding its breath. The journey was simply a journey again, filled with the mundane hum of a jet engine and the quiet anticipation of a long-awaited family visit. Alex and Chloé Miller, now 37, were finally "finishing" the trip that had been so violently interrupted by the pandemic nearly three years prior.

Upon arriving at Montréal-Trudeau International Airport (YUL), they collected their luggage and picked up their pre-booked, lift-equipped rental van. The drive south to Stanstead was a journey through a winter wonderland. A fresh blanket of snow covered the rolling hills of the Eastern Townships, the pine trees heavy with white, the sky a crisp, brilliant blue.

When they pulled into the Leclercs' familiar driveway, the front door flew open before they had even come to a complete stop. Aunt Geneviève and Uncle David rushed out, their faces beaming. The reunion on the snowy walkway was a joyful, emotional affair of tight hugs and happy, overlapping chatter in Québécois French.

"*Mes amours! Vous êtes enfin arrivés!*" Geneviève cried, enveloping Chloé in a fierce, loving hug. (My loves! You've finally arrived!)

"*Oncle David!*" Chloé laughed, hugging her uncle. "It's so good to be back."

David clapped a happy hand on Alex's shoulder. "It is so good to see you, my boy. The house has been too quiet without its adopted Brawlers fan."

Inside, the house was warm and smelled of cinnamon and baking tourtière. After settling their bags in the familiar guest room, they all gathered in the cozy living room, mugs of hot chocolate in hand. The usual catching up had a new, more exciting topic.

"Okay," Chloé said, a proud, expectant smile on her face. "We have something to show you."

Alex, with a few commands on his AAC device, connected it to the Leclercs' large television. A moment later, the screen was filled not with a family photo, but with a stunning, photorealistic **architectural rendering** of a beautiful, modern building nestled amongst tall evergreen trees.

THE LYNNWOOD DISCOVERY AND CARE CENTER, Alex's device announced, the synthesized voice full of a quiet, profound pride.

He navigated through a series of images: detailed site plans for a 10-acre wooded plot, digital mockups of the interior, and finally, a breathtaking 3D animated walkthrough of the main building and its habitats.

Geneviève and David stared at the screen, completely captivated. "Oh, *mes amours*," Geneviève whispered, her eyes shining. "It's... it's magnificent. The dream is all there."

"It's all ready to go," Chloé confirmed, her own voice full of a nervous hope. "We've submitted the grant application to the Cascade Family Trust. If it's approved, this is what we will build. We'll be able to buy the land and break ground by the summer."

Alex brought up a detailed 3D model of a planned habitat, a complex, enriching environment for rescued river otters, complete with accessible underwater viewing windows.

THIS IS THE PACIFIC COAST HABITAT, he explained. IT WILL CONNECT TO THE BRITISH COLUMBIA EXHIBIT.

David leaned forward, studying the intricate design, deeply impressed. "*C'est incroyable, Alex,*" he said with genuine admiration. (That's incredible, Alex.) "The work of a true engineer."

They spent the next hour looking at the plans, Alex explaining the technical designs, Chloé detailing the non-profit and educational mission. It was the first time they had been able to share the full, tangible vision of their life's dream with their Canadian family in person. The years of planning, of saving, of enduring a global pandemic—it had all led to this. They were no longer just dreaming; they were building the final blueprint.

### Part 4: The Boxing Day Mission

Christmas Day was a quiet, relaxed affair of leftovers, old movies, and peaceful family time. But on the morning of December 26th, a new, chaotic energy filled the Leclerc house.

"Okay, Alex," Chloé said, a mischievous glint in her eye as she handed him a steaming mug of coffee. "Are you prepared for your first Boxing Day? The most important holiday of the Canadian Christmas season."

MORE IMPORTANT THAN CHRISTMAS? his device asked, confused.

"For a Canadian looking for a deal? Absolutely," Chloé laughed. "Today is the day of the big sales. It's a national sport."

Uncle David, already in his winter coat, held up a stack of store flyers. "We have our battle plan!" he declared. "We drive to the big shopping centre in Sherbrooke. We divide, we conquer, and we meet back at the food court for poutine at one o'clock sharp!"

The drive to Sherbrooke was filled with a giddy, strategic energy. The shopping centre was an absolute zoo, a good-natured but chaotic swarm of people hunting for deals. For Alex, who had never experienced anything like it, the sheer density of the crowd was a sensory overload.

Chloé, remembering the chaos from her youth, was in her element but also completely focused on Alex. She was his anchor, her hand always on his arm or the back of his chair, helping him navigate the throngs of shoppers. She guided him through the noisy, brightly-lit electronics store where David and Mathieu were comparing discounted televisions, and then to the clothing store where Geneviève and Isabelle were triumphantly holding up half-priced sweaters.

Alex was amazed and slightly overwhelmed by the uniquely Canadian tradition. He witnessed the long lines, the massive "50% DE RABAIS" signs, and the happy shoppers emerging with bags of discounted goods.

The day ended as promised, with the entire family gathered in the crowded food court, devouring well-earned plates of poutine. They were tired, but their shopping mission had been a success. As they drove back to Stanstead, the van filled with bags and the happy chatter of a family that had successfully navigated the wonderful chaos of Boxing Day, Alex felt like he had been initiated into another, essential part of Chloé's world.

## Chapter 3: Breaking Ground

### Part 1: The Call and the Land

The waiting was the hardest part. Their "runway fund" was slowly shrinking, a constant, quiet pressure in the back of their minds. They poured their nervous energy into refining their plans and volunteering at the Evergreen Animal Shelter, but the fate of their dream rested in the hands of a committee they had never met.

The call came on a Tuesday morning in early March. Chloé’s phone buzzed with an unknown number. She answered, her voice hesitant. "Hello, Chloé Miller speaking."

She listened, her eyes widening. She sank into a chair, her hand flying to her mouth. "Yes... Yes, I understand... Oh my God... Thank you. Thank you so much."

She hung up the phone, her face pale with shock. She turned to Alex, who was watching her, his own heart pounding.

"We got it," she whispered, the words barely audible. Then, her voice rose to a joyous, disbelieving shout. "Alex, we got it! The Cascade Family Trust! They're funding us! The whole grant! We got it!"

The sound that escaped Alex was a raw, guttural cry of pure, explosive joy. The tension of the last three months, of the last thirteen years, shattered in an instant. Chloé was laughing and crying at the same time as she launched herself into his arms, a chaotic, triumphant hug that was the culmination of a lifetime of shared dreams.

The grant didn't just give them money; it gave them the power to act. The first call they made was to a real estate agent. A week later, they were standing on a beautiful, wooded 10-acre plot of land in Lynnwood. The air was cool and smelled of damp earth and pine. They had just signed the papers. It was theirs.

Chloé held up her phone, a map of the Puget Sound Transit system glowing on the screen. She pointed to a cluster of bus stop icons on a main road just a short, accessible walk from their new property line.

"There it is," she said, her voice full of a quiet, triumphant satisfaction. "Route 112. And the Swift Orange Line. Thirty-minute service, seven days a week. It took us two years of searching to find a piece of land this perfect that was this close to the Transit Center, but it was non-negotiable."

Alex nodded, his own expression full of a deep sense of purpose. IT HAS TO BE ACCESSIBLE, his AAC device voiced. FOR EVERYONE. THE VOLUNTEERS, THE STAFF... THE KIDS WHO COME TO VISIT. NOT JUST FOR PEOPLE WITH CARS.

"Exactly," Chloé agreed.

They looked out over the expanse of trees and meadows, a blank canvas for their shared dream. The years of planning, of saving, of quitting their jobs to chase this one, all-consuming goal had led them to this exact spot. The Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center was no longer just a business plan or a 3D model on Alex's computer. It was real. It had a home. And the first thing they had ensured was that the world could find its way to their door. The dream was finally about to be built.

**Part 2: From Blueprint to Reality (Spring 2023)**

The euphoria of securing the grant and the land lasted for a glorious week. Then, the new, even more daunting reality set in: they actually had to build the thing. Their apartment, once a fundraising headquarters, transformed again, this time into a project management office.

Their first step was to hire a local, well-respected architectural firm known for its work on public and sustainable buildings. The initial meetings were a perfect demonstration of their partnership. Chloé, armed with her binders and budgets, was the project manager. She handled the contracts, the timelines, and the financial oversight.

Alex, however, was the lead designer. He wasn't just a client; he was an integral part of the architectural team. He would sit in on meetings, his "Quantum Design Studio 2" connected to the large screen, pulling up his own intricate 3D models. He would point out subtle but crucial flaws in the initial blueprints that only a full-time wheelchair user would notice.

THIS CORRIDOR IS TOO NARROW FOR TWO POWER CHAIRS TO PASS COMFORTABLY, his device would state, and the architects would nod, immediately adjusting the plans. THE SENSORY ROOM NEEDS INDIRECT, DIMMABLE LIGHTING, NOT FLUORESCENTS. THE SLOPE ON THIS ACCESSIBLE PATH IS HALF A DEGREE TOO STEEP. IT WILL CAUSE FATIGUE FOR MANUAL CHAIR USERS.

His expertise was invaluable, his insights shaping every aspect of the Center's design, ensuring it would be a truly, deeply accessible space.

### Part 2: The Groundbreaking

A few months later, on a bright, clear day in early summer, a small group gathered on their 10-acre plot. The land had been cleared, and survey stakes dotted the landscape. All four of their parents had flown in from Virginia, and the Leclercs had come down from Québec. A representative from the Cascade Family Trust was there, as was the mayor of Lynnwood.

It was the official groundbreaking ceremony for the Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center.

Chloé, standing before the small crowd, gave a short, heartfelt speech, her voice full of emotion as she thanked their families, the foundation, and the city for their unwavering support.

Then, it was time. James Miller and Thomas Mason came forward, carrying a single, silver-plated shovel. Alex had spent the previous week in his workshop, designing and 3D-printing a custom, ergonomic handle that clamped securely to the shovel's shaft, allowing him to use it comfortably from his chair.

He maneuvered his chair into position beside Chloé. Together, they placed their hands on the shovel. With their entire, united, cross-border family watching, their eyes shining with pride, Alex and Chloé pushed the shovel into the rich, dark earth of their new home and turned over the first symbolic shovelful of dirt.

A cheer went up from the small crowd. As they all stood there, looking at the first small break in the ground, they could hear the distant rumble of the first bulldozer arriving to begin the real work. The dream was no longer a plan or a promise. It was happening.

## Chapter 4: Echoes and Reunions

### Part 1: A Familiar Face (Fall 2023)

The Northwood High School gymnasium, usually echoing with the squeak of sneakers, was tonight awash with slightly more formal footwear and the murmur of adults reconnecting. Banners proclaiming "Class of 2003 – 20 Year Reunion!" hung with a charming lack of professional precision. The trip itself had been a gift from their four parents, who had insisted Alex and Chloé take a much-needed break from the all-consuming work of overseeing the LDCC's construction.

Alex and Chloé Miller were making their way through the crowd, exchanging polite greetings with familiar, if now older, faces. Suddenly, a woman with kind, intelligent eyes and a warm smile approached them.

"Alex! Chloé! I thought that was you!"

Alex looked up, and a slow, genuine smile of recognition spread across his face. "HANNAH," his AAC device voiced, the tone full of a pleasant surprise.

It was Hannah Peterson, his lab partner from sophomore year Biology. She looked happy and successful. "It's Hannah Davis now," she said, gesturing to the man beside her, a handsome but tense-looking man in an expensive suit. "This is my husband, Mark."

"It's so good to see you both," Hannah continued, her warmth genuine. She immediately pulled Chloé into a warm, friendly hug. "Chloé, you look amazing! Running a whole non-profit, I hear? That's incredible."

"Hannah! It's so good to see you too!" Chloé said, returning the hug warmly. "And you! Alex always said what a kind and brilliant lab partner you were."

Hannah beamed, then turned to Alex. Without a second thought, she leaned in and gave him an equally warm, friendly hug, a simple gesture between old friends. "You look wonderful, Alex. Truly."

As she pulled back, she saw her husband Mark's expression. It was tight, his eyes narrowed, a look of undisguised suspicion and jealousy on his face as he stared at the easy affection between Hannah and her two old friends.

"So," Mark said, his voice clipped, not looking at either Alex or Chloé but directing his question to Hannah. "These are the friends you were talking about?"

The possessive, almost accusatory tone was unmistakable. Hannah's smile faltered, a flicker of familiar weariness in her eyes. "Yes, Mark. This is my friend, Alex, and my friend, Chloé."

"Right," Mark said, his gaze flicking dismissively between them. "Well. Good to see you're... out."

The awkward, tense silence was broken by Chloé, who stepped forward slightly, her hand resting protectively on the back of Alex's chair. "It was lovely to see you, Hannah," she said, her voice polite but with an edge of steel that was a clear dismissal of Mark's attitude. "We were just about to go grab a drink."

"Oh! Of course," Hannah said, looking flustered but grateful for the exit. "It was so good to see you both."

As Alex and Chloé moved away, a quiet, sad understanding passed between them. Hannah was still the kind, perceptive person they remembered, but it was clear she was now tethered to a man whose insecurity and jealousy were a stark, ugly contrast to her own warmth. It was a brief, bittersweet encounter, a reminder that not all high school connections blossom into happy endings.

### Part 2: The Unchanging Bully

Near the refreshment table, Ben Carter, looking sharp and confident in a well-tailored suit, was laughing easily with his wife, Morgan McAllister Carter. Morgan, equally poised and successful as an upper-level executive, had an arm looped comfortably through his.

A few tables away, Alex and Chloé were observing the scene, a quiet contentment between them. They had been showing photos on their phone to a small group of interested former classmates—images of the LDCC's steel framework now fully erected, the roof being installed, a tangible sign of their incredible progress.

Josh Riley, a bit thicker around the middle now, his youthful sneer settled into a permanent cynical downturn of his lips, was holding court with a couple of other men. He turned, his eyes narrowing as he focused on Ben and Morgan across the room.

"Carter? Married?" Josh bellowed, loud enough to turn several heads. "To McAllister? You gotta be kidding me! The guy who used to be my goon, married to 'Morgue' McAllister?" He threw his head back and roared with laughter, a harsh, unpleasant sound.

Then, his gaze flickered over to Alex and Chloé. His sneer deepened. "And look over there! Miller still dragging that robot voice around, and he's married to Mason? What is this, a reunion for freaks and losers who somehow tricked someone into marrying them?"

Before Josh could continue his tirade, a familiar, clear voice cut through the air. "Mr. Riley."

Josh Riley froze mid-sneer, a look of genuine, twenty-year-old fear momentarily flashing across his face.

Mrs. DeMarco, her hair grayer but her eyes as sharp as ever, had risen silently from a table. She approached Josh, her posture erect and unwavering.

"Mrs. DeMarco," Josh stammered. "Just… catching up."

"Indeed, Mr. Riley," she said, her voice dangerously calm. "It seems your methods of 'catching up' still involve disparaging remarks about your former classmates." Her gaze flickered warmly towards Ben and Morgan, and then with equal warmth towards Alex and Chloé, a hint of a proud smile touching her lips before returning, like chilled steel, to Josh. "Mr. and Mrs. Carter, and Mr. and Mrs. Miller," she used their married names with clear emphasis, "are testaments to growth, resilience, and enduring partnership. Qualities that, I observe, still seem to elude you."

Josh started to bluster, "Now, hold on, I was just—"

"Were you perhaps about to volunteer for clean-up duty after this event, Mr. Riley?" Mrs. DeMarco interjected smoothly. "I believe the janitorial staff could use an experienced hand tomorrow morning."

The blood drained from Josh’s face. "N-no, Mrs. DeMarco," he mumbled. "Understood."

"Excellent," she said crisply. "Do try to enjoy the remainder of your evening with a renewed focus on civility."

Later, as Alex and Chloé were preparing to leave, Mrs. DeMarco approached them. Her earlier sternness was replaced by a genuine warmth.

"*Monsieur Miller, Madame Miller,*" she said, her Parisian French as flawless as ever. "*C'est un réel plaisir de vous voir. J'ai entendu parler du travail merveilleux que vous faites à Lynnwood. C'est un délice de voir comment 'mes enfants' ont prospéré.*" (Mr. Miller, Mrs. Miller, it's a real pleasure to see you. I've heard about the wonderful work you're doing in Lynnwood. It's a delight to see how 'my kids' have flourished.)

Chloé smiled, replying in her own fluent French, "Merci, Madame DeMarco. C'était merveilleux de vous revoir aussi." (Thank you, Mrs. DeMarco. It was wonderful to see you too.)

Alex, via his AAC, typed: MRS. DEMARCO. YOU ARE STILL A LEGEND. THANK YOU. FOR EVERYTHING. ALWAYS.

Mrs. DeMarco actually chuckled, a warm, genuine sound. "One does what one can, Mr. Miller. And some lessons, it seems, bear repeating across the decades." She gave them a knowing wink. "Safe travels back to Lynnwood."

### Part 3: The Last Straw

As Hannah and Mark walked away from Alex and Chloé, the forced smile on Hannah's face vanished, replaced by a look of weary frustration. Mark, however, was just getting started.

"I can't believe you," he hissed, his voice a low, angry buzz. "Throwing yourself at them like that. Hugging that guy in the wheelchair. What was that all about?"

"They're my friends, Mark," Hannah said, her own voice tight. "My old friends. That's what friends do. They hug."

"It was inappropriate," he snapped. "You were all over him. And her. It's embarrassing. You made me look like a fool."

Hannah stopped walking and turned to face him, a new, cold clarity in her eyes. "No, Mark. You're embarrassing yourself. That was a simple, friendly hug between old classmates. The fact that you see it as anything else... the fact that you're so jealous and possessive that I can't even greet a friend without you turning it into a scene... this isn't the first time, is it? But it is going to be the last."

The finality in her tone seemed to finally penetrate his self-absorbed anger. He looked at her, a flicker of genuine surprise on his face.

"I'm done," Hannah said, her voice quiet but unshakeable. "I am done being made to feel guilty for having friends. I'm done walking on eggshells around your jealousy." She took a deep, shuddering breath. "I'm getting a hotel room tonight. And on Monday, I'm calling a lawyer. I want a divorce."

Without waiting for his sputtering reply, she turned and walked away, not back towards the exit, but back towards the one place in the room where she had just seen a glimpse of a real, supportive partnership.

She found Alex and Chloé by the refreshment table. They saw her approaching, alone, and the concern on their faces was immediate and genuine.

"Hannah? Is everything okay?" Chloé asked, her voice soft.

Hannah managed a small, watery, but resolute smile. "Actually," she said, her voice a little shaky, "I think, for the first time in a long time, it's going to be." She took a breath. "Mark... has left. And I'm not going home with him. I was wondering... would it be okay if I just sat with you guys for a while?"

"Of course," Chloé said instantly, pulling out a chair for her. Alex's AAC device voiced a simple, unequivocal YES. PLEASE.

Hannah sat down, a profound sense of relief washing over her. She was a long way from okay, but in that moment, sitting with two old friends whose quiet, unwavering support for each other was a tangible thing, she felt like she had finally found a safe harbor to begin figuring out what came next.

### Part 4: A United Front

The three of them sat together, talking in low, comforting tones. "So, the non-profit is really up and running?" Hannah asked, her voice a little shaky but genuinely interested, latching onto a safe topic. "That's amazing. What kind of work do you do?"

Chloé and Alex exchanged a warm look. Chloé began to explain, her passion for their work a welcome, positive energy. "It's called the Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center. We focus on rescuing and rehabilitating special needs animals."

I DESIGN ACCESSIBLE HABITATS AND PROSTHETICS, Alex's device added, a quiet pride in the synthesized voice. CHLOÉ HANDLES THE BUSINESS SIDE. SHE'S THE REAL BOSS.

Chloé laughed, a genuine, easy sound. "We're a team." She then looked at Hannah, her expression softening. "And you, Hannah? What have you been up to for the last twenty years? Besides... you know." She gestured vaguely in the direction Mark had stormed off.

Hannah managed a small, watery smile. "Well, I actually put that Biology credit with Alex to good use. I'm a botanist, working for a conservation group. And... I have two kids. A boy and a girl. They're... they're the best thing in my life."

"That's wonderful, Hannah," Chloé said sincerely.

Hannah's smile faltered, and she looked down at her hands. "It is," she said, her voice dropping. "It's just... hard. Mark... he's not a bad man, not really. But he's so... insecure. He needs to be the center of everything. My job, my friends... even the kids sometimes. It's like he sees everything that makes me happy as a threat to him."

She looked up, her eyes full of a sad admiration. "And then I saw you two. And Ben and Morgan. The way you support each other, the way you're a team. It's so... real. I'd forgotten what that looked like."

Just then, Mark reappeared, his expression now a mask of practiced contrition. He approached their table, ignoring Alex and Chloé and focusing only on Hannah.

"Hannah, baby, I'm sorry," he began, his voice a low, placating murmur. "I overreacted. You know how I get. It's just because I love you so much. Let's just go home. We can talk about this. I'll change, I promise."

It was a script Hannah had heard before. She visibly flinched, steeling herself. "No, Mark. Not this time."

Before he could press further, a calm, clear voice interjected. "Mr. Davis."

They all looked up. Mrs. DeMarco was standing a few feet away, her earlier interaction with Josh Riley clearly not the only thing on her radar. She had been observing the new drama with a quiet, discerning eye.

"I believe," Mrs. DeMarco said, her voice polite but with an undercurrent of unyielding steel, "that your wife has made her wishes quite clear. She has asked for space. I suggest you provide it."

Mark whirled on her, his composure cracking. "This is a private conversation! This has nothing to in do with you!"

"On the contrary," Mrs. DeMarco replied smoothly. "This is a school event. And I am a chaperone. Ensuring that all my former students feel safe and respected falls squarely within my purview. Your wife," she repeated, emphasizing the word, "is currently feeling neither. Therefore, it has everything to do with me."

With a final, warm nod to the three of them, Mrs. DeMarco turned and walked away, her work for the evening finally, truly, done.

### Part 5: A New Beginning

After Mark had left for good, a quiet, supportive silence settled over their small table. Hannah took a few deep, shuddering breaths, the finality of her decision settling in. She looked at Alex and Chloé, her eyes full of a raw, fragile gratitude.
