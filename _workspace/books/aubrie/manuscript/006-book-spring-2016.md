# Book: Spring 2016

## Chapter 1: The Announcement

### Part 1: The Electric Alert

It was a gray, slushy Tuesday in mid-February 2016. The excitement of the holidays and the "Black Friday Road Trip" had faded into the grinding rhythm of the Spring semester. Jordan sat at the dining table in the Brooks' basement, his laptop open to his Astrophysics homework, but his mind was wandering.

He clicked over to his favorite bookmark: *TheStardustEngine.com*. He checked it every day out of habit, usually just seeing tour dates for cities in Europe or Asia.

Today, the homepage was different. The usual tour calendar was gone. In its place was a massive, pulsing graphic in neon teal and magenta.

THE STARDUST ENGINE: COMING HOME. THE ELECTRIC COLOR TOUR FINALE. LIVE RECORDING AT THE CRUCIBLE. SATURDAY, MAY 21, 2016.

Jordan stopped breathing for a second. He read it again. *Live Recording. The Crucible.*

"Guys!" he yelled, his voice cracking with excitement. "Guys, look!"

Aubrie, Madison, and Zoe, who were studying nearby, rushed over. Mark and Hannah poked their heads out of their room upstairs.

"What is it?" Aubrie asked, looking at the screen.

"They're coming," Jordan said, pointing. "The Stardust Engine. They're playing The Crucible. And they're recording a live album. Here. In Blacksburg."

The room exploded with excitement. "No way!" Mark shouted, bounding down the stairs. "A stadium show? That's going to be insane!"

"We have to go," Jordan said, his eyes wide. "We have to be there. I need to be there."

Zoe, ever the pragmatist, immediately leaned in to read the fine print. "We need to analyze the ticketing protocols immediately. A show of this magnitude will sell out instantly."

She clicked on the "TICKETS" link. The rules were strict, designed to protect the fans.

FORGER NATION PRESALE (Students & Alumni Only)

- **Opens:** Friday @ 10:00 AM.

- **Requirement:** Valid CPI Student Poly Pass or Alumni Association Member ID.

- **Limit:** 4 Tickets per ID.

Jordan’s face fell slightly. "Four tickets? But... there are nine of us. Plus Daniel and Laura. We need eleven." He looked at Megan, who was working quietly at the other end of the table. "And Megan... she's not a student anymore."

"I'm an alumna," Megan said, smiling. "I have an Alumni ID. But the alumni presale usually puts you in the upper deck. You guys want to be on the floor, right?"

"We want to be together," Jordan insisted. "All of us."

Zoe scanned the FAQ section, her eyes darting back and forth. "Found it," she announced. "There is a 'Guest Protocol' for the Student Presale."

She read aloud:

"Current CPI Students may purchase up to 4 tickets for their personal group. Guests do not need to be students, but **must enter the venue with the purchasing student**. The Student ID will serve as the digital key for the entire party."

"Okay, that's the solution," Zoe said, her mind already building the spreadsheet. "We create 'Ticket Cells.' We have enough current students to cover everyone."

She grabbed a notepad and sketched out the plan:

- **Cell Alpha (Jordan's ID):** Buys 4 Tickets. (Jordan, Aubrie, Madison, **Megan**).

  - *Logic:* Jordan uses his student priority to secure a spot for Megan as his guest. They enter together.

- **Cell Beta (Mark's ID):** Buys 4 Tickets. (Mark, Hannah, Daniel, Laura).

  - *Logic:* Mark secures his parents-in-law.

- **Cell Gamma (Zoe's ID):** Buys 3 Tickets. (Zoe, Lauren, Sarah).

"We all log in at exactly 10:00 AM," Zoe commanded. "We select the 'Accessible Field Level' section. If we coordinate our clicks, we can secure three adjacent blocks."

Jordan looked at the plan. It was perfect. He looked at Megan. "You're with me," he said firmly. "In my cell."

Megan smiled, touched by his immediate need to include her. "I wouldn't miss it for the world, Jordan."

"Okay," Jordan said, turning back to the screen, the date glowing in neon letters. "Friday at 10:00 AM. We're going to The Crucible."

### Part 2: The 10:00 AM Rush

It was Friday morning. The air in the Brooks' basement was thick with the same intensity as a launch control room. The dining table had been transformed into a command center. Three laptops were open, screens glowing.

At **9:50 AM sharp**, the "virtual waiting room" for the CPI Events portal opened.

"Go, go, go!" Zoe commanded.

Jordan, Mark, and Zoe all hit the login button simultaneously. They typed in their student credentials, their fingers flying. A moment later, all three screens refreshed to show a spinning maroon circle and the message: **"YOU ARE IN LINE. DO NOT REFRESH. TICKET SALES BEGIN AT 10:00 AM."**

"We're in the chute," Mark said, leaning back but keeping his hand hovering near the mouse.

The next ten minutes were an agony of waiting. 9:55 AM came and went.

"Status check," Zoe called out, her eyes fixed on the atomic clock on her tablet.

"Cell Alpha, ready," Jordan reported. He was sitting in his wheelchair, his mouse hovering. His heart was hammering against his ribs. Beside him, Aubrie and Madison were watching his screen like hawks. Megan, the guest of honor for his cell, stood behind him, her hand resting supportively on his shoulder.

"Cell Beta, standing by," Mark said from the other end of the table. He had Hannah sitting next to him. His mission was to secure tickets for himself, Hannah, and Daniel and Laura.

"Cell Gamma, green," Zoe confirmed. She was buying for herself, Lauren, and Sarah.

"Remember the protocol," Zoe instructed, her voice calm but sharp. "We are targeting **Section 104, Row A**. That is the accessible field-level row. Jordan needs the wheel-in spot, and we need the companion seats directly adjacent."

9:59:30 AM.

"Hands off the keyboards," Zoe commanded. "Do not refresh. The page will auto-update via AJAX. Wait for the signal."

They watched the seconds tick down on the atomic clock. 57... 58... 59...

10:00:00 AM.

On all three screens simultaneously, the gray "COMING SOON" button flashed and instantly turned a vibrant, electric green: **"BUY TICKETS."**

"Go!" Zoe shouted.

"I'm in!" Jordan yelled, clicking the button the millisecond it changed. The seating chart loaded instantly. He scanned the field map and saw the blue wheelchair icon for Section 104. "I see it! Row A, Seat 1!"

"Grab it!" Megan cheered.

Jordan selected the wheelchair space and the three companion seats next to it. "Cell Alpha locked."

"Cell Beta locked," Mark called out seconds later.

"Cell Gamma locked," Zoe finished.

They all hit "Next." But instead of the payment screen, a new page appeared: **"EXCLUSIVE FORGER NATION ADD-ONS."**

Jordan scanned the options.

**1. Ceremonial Ticket Stub (\$5.00)** *High-quality, gold-foil souvenir printed on heavy cardstock. Featuring custom art of The Iron Forger.* **WARNING:** SOUVENIR ONLY. NOT VALID FOR ENTRY. POLY PASS REQUIRED. NO EXCEPTIONS.

**2. The Soundboard Direct: Live at The Crucible (\$15.00)** A raw, unmixed audio CD-R burned directly from the soundboard feed. Available for pickup at The Foundry 24 hours after the concert. Note: This is a raw archival recording to hold you over until the official mixed album release in the Fall.

"Guys, look!" Jordan said, pointing at the screen. "We can get physical stubs! And a CD of the show right after it happens!"

"Get them," Aubrie said instantly. "We need the stubs for the memory box. And you *definitely* need that CD."

Jordan grinned and clicked "Add to Order" for four stubs and one copy of the Soundboard Direct CD-R.

"Cell Alpha checking out," he announced.

"Same here," Mark said. "Getting stubs for everyone."

They hit "Complete Purchase." The screens spun for a terrifying second... processing... processing...

"CONFIRMED. ORDER COMPLETE."

The room erupted. Madison shrieked and hugged Aubrie. Mark high-fived Hannah. Jordan slumped back in his chair, letting out a massive breath of relief as the digital confirmation appeared on his screen.

The Stardust Engine: Live at The Crucible Section 104, Row A. Ticket Holder: Jordan Shawn Kelly Access Method: Poly Pass (Ending in 8842) Add-Ons: (4) Ceremonial Stubs, (1) Soundboard Direct CD-R

He looked up at Megan, beaming. "We got 'em. We're going to the front row. And we're taking the music home with us."

### Part 3: The Scalper Meltdown

On Friday morning, 72 hours after Jordan had secured his ticket in the student presale, the floodgates opened for the rest of the world.

At 10:00 AM sharp, TicketSphere launched the general sale for *The Stardust Engine: Live at The Crucible*. The Group of 9, still gathered around the dining table in the Brooks' basement, watched the spectacle unfold not on the ticket purchase page, but on the forums and message boards.

"Here it comes," Mark said, hitting refresh on a popular scalper forum.

Instantly, the threads began to populate with confusion and rage.

**User: TicketKing99:** WTF? Sale just opened 10 seconds ago and the entire floor is grayed out? Glitch? **User: BotMaster:** My scripts are bouncing. Everything in the 100 level and Field Level is showing 'Restricted'. Only upper deck nosebleeds are pulling up. **User: ScalpEmAll:** This is BS. How is it sold out already? There wasn't even a queue!

"They are so mad," Madison giggled, reading a furious tweet from a broker in New York who had planned to buy fifty floor seats. "They can't figure out why the good tickets are already gone."

"They're gone because Forger Nation took them," Jordan said, a profound sense of satisfaction settling in his chest. He looked at the confirmation on his own screen—Row A, Seat 1. He had beaten the bots simply by belonging.

Zoe tapped her screen. "Look. The band just posted an update to the main site. It explains everything."

The group huddled around her laptop. On the homepage of *TheStardustEngine.com*, a new letter had appeared, written in the band's signature maroon type.

### Part 4: The Letter from Home

Zoe read the title aloud. "To the scalpers, the bots, and the fans asking about the front row."

She scrolled down, and the group read the message together.

"To everyone wondering why the floor seats for The Crucible show vanished before the public sale even started:

We didn't start this band in a boardroom in Los Angeles. We didn't get discovered on a reality show. We started this band in a sweaty, unheated garage at the corner of **Progress Street NE & Turner Street NE**—just one block from campus—trying to play loud enough to keep warm.

We learned how to be a band in the cramped, soundproof practice rooms of Henderson Hall, staying late after class until the janitors kicked us out. We played our first gigs at the local bars downtown for tips and pizza.

Blacksburg isn't just a tour stop on a spreadsheet. This is where we found our sound. This is where we found each other. This is our home.

And the way we were raised, when you come home for a celebration, you don't make your family sit outside. You take care of them first.

So, we made a call. We told the promoters and the ticket brokers to back off. We reserved the entire field and the lower bowl exclusively for the people who walk the same halls we did.

To the general public: We love you, and we can't wait to see you in the stands. It’s going to be a legendary night.

To the Forger Nation student body: The front row is yours. The barrier is yours. The energy is yours. We aren't just playing for you; we're playing with you. Bring the noise.

See you at The Crucible.

— Cassidy, Ryan, Evan, Holly, & Tyler (Class of '89)"

Jordan sat back, staring at the screen. The Stardust Engine, the biggest band in the world, hadn't forgotten. They were CPI alumni, and they had used their power to ensure that he, Aubrie, Madison, and Megan would be right there in the front row.

"Forgers take care of Forgers," Mark said quietly, summing it up perfectly.

"Bring the noise," Jordan whispered, a smile spreading across his face. "Oh, we will."

### Part 5: The Box Office Confrontation

It was Wednesday afternoon, five days after the ticket drop. The email notification had arrived that morning: **"Your Ceremonial Ticket Stubs are ready for pickup at The Crucible Box Office, Gate 1."**

The walk from the transit center to the stadium was short, but for Jordan, it felt like walking into a war zone. The massive concrete structure of **The Crucible** loomed over them, casting a long shadow. His heart began to race, a phantom echo of the panic he had felt two years ago during the 38-0 shutout.

He wasn't alone. Zoe was pushing his wheelchair, her presence a steady, logical force behind him. On his left, his wife Aubrie held his hand, her thumb rubbing his knuckles in a soothing rhythm. On his right, Megan held his other hand, her grip firm and sisterly. He was flanked by his fortress.

"You okay, J?" Megan asked softly.

"Yeah," Jordan breathed, staring at the gate where he had once fled in terror. "Just... memories."

"We're making new ones today," Aubrie promised.

They joined the short line at the box office window. Directly in front of them was a man in a cheap suit, his face red, waving a stack of printed papers at the young woman behind the glass.

"This is unacceptable!" the man shouted, his voice echoing off the concrete walls. "I have clients willing to pay top dollar for floor seats! I have a bot net that hit your server at 10:00:01! How is it possible that *every single* floor seat was unavailable?"

The box office worker, a student with a bored expression, didn't flinch. "Sir, as the website stated, the floor and lower bowl were reserved for the Forger Nation presale. Those tickets are sold out."

"That's discrimination!" the scalper spat. "I'm a legitimate broker! I demand to speak to a manager. I want to know who bought those tickets!"

"Students, sir," the worker said, popping her gum. "Students bought them. And since the tickets are locked to their student IDs, you couldn't resell them even if you had them. Now, do you want to buy a nosebleed seat or not?"

The scalper looked like he was going to explode. He slammed his hand on the counter. "This band is a joke! They're leaving money on the table! Amateur hour!"

He stormed away, muttering furiously, brushing past Jordan's wheelchair without even seeing him.

Zoe watched him go, a small, satisfied smirk on her face. "Inefficient business model," she noted dryly. "He failed to account for the 'Loyalty' variable."

Jordan looked at the retreating scalper, then at the stadium. The fear in his chest loosened. The big bad wolf of the ticket world had just been defeated by a student worker and a band that cared more about *him* than about money.

"Next," the worker called out.

Zoe rolled Jordan up to the window. He let go of Megan's hand just long enough to pull his wallet from his pocket. He slapped his **Poly Pass** onto the metal tray.

"Pickup for Jordan Shawn Kelly," he said, his voice steady. "Section 104, Row A."

The worker's bored expression vanished instantly. She smiled, recognizing the prime location. "Oh, front row. Nice. Let me grab those."

She disappeared for a moment and returned with a heavy, gold-foiled envelope. She slid it through the slot.

Jordan picked it up. He opened the flap and pulled out the four heavy cardstock stubs. They were beautiful—maroon and orange, with gold foil embossing of **The Iron Forger** mascot and the tour dates.

He handed one to Aubrie, one to Megan, and reached back to hand one to Zoe. He held his own up to the light. It was just a piece of cardboard, a souvenir that couldn't even get him into the building. But holding it in the shadow of the stadium that had once broken him, it felt like a medal of valor.

"We got 'em," he whispered. "We're really going."

## Chapter 2: The Stardust Engine Concert

### Part 1: The Shuttle

On the afternoon of Saturday, May 21, 2016, the Group of 11 gathered at the **Falling Branch Park & Ride**. The lot was transformed. Usually a quiet commuter hub, it was now a bustling staging ground filled with fans in maroon and orange.

Waiting at the curb wasn't the usual scheduled bus. It was a 60-foot articulated Blue Ridge Transit bus, its digital destination sign cycling between two high-energy messages:

CRUCIBLE SHUTTLE

...and then...

GO FORGERS!

The front door stood open, and a loud, clear automated voice called out on a repeated loop for the crowd:

"Crucible Shuttle. GO FORGERS! ... Crucible Shuttle. GO FORGERS!"

"Look at that," Mark grinned, pointing as the sign flashed over and the voice boomed. "They really pulled out all the stops."

"Blue Ridge Transit suspends all regular service for Tier 1 events," Zoe explained, checking her watch. "Every bus they own is running shuttle loops. It's the most efficient way to move 60,000 people."

Jordan wheeled himself to the front of the line. The driver deployed the ramp—simple and fast, unlike the coach lifts.

"Heading to the show?" the driver asked with a smile. He was wearing a Stardust Engine t-shirt over his uniform.

"Front row," Jordan beamed.

They boarded the massive bus. It didn't make the usual stops. It didn't wind through town. It roared onto the highway, bypassing the traffic jams, and drove straight to the stadium.

When they arrived, the bus pulled into the dedicated **Crucible Bus Loop**, dropping them off right at the foot of the stadium gates. Jordan looked out the window at the massive crowd and the looming stadium. The shuttle had delivered them right to the doorstep of history.

As the Group of 11 bus pulled into **The Crucible Bus Loop**, Jordan looked out the window at the adjacent "Stadium Gold" parking lot.

He saw a line of cars stuck in gridlock, waiting to pay. A frustrated driver in a sedan was handing a wad of cash to an attendant.

"Look at that sign," Mark said, pointing. "**EVENT PARKING: \$50. CASH ONLY.**"

"Inefficient," Zoe noted, shaking her head. "They are paying a premium for the privilege of sitting in traffic."

Their bus driver, meanwhile, opened the doors. "Alright folks, we're here! Thanks for riding the shuttle. Enjoy the show!"

No wallets. No swiping. No cash. They just rolled off the bus, right to the front gate, for free.

"Fifty bucks to park," Daniel Brooks chuckled, guiding Jordan toward the security checkpoint. "I love this school, but I love not paying for parking even more."

"That's why we take the bus," Jordan smiled. "The *real* VIP experience."

### Part 2: Forger Tailgate

The sun was high and bright over Blacksburg on Saturday, May 21, 2016. The area surrounding **The Crucible** had been transformed. It wasn't a football game day, but the energy was identical, if not more intense.

The university had sanctioned an official pre-concert tailgate, but with a specific twist: the inner perimeter, the massive paved plaza directly in front of Gate 1, was cordoned off by heavy steel barricades.

Jordan wheeled himself up to the checkpoint, flanked by his fortress of family. Before they even reached the staff members scanning tickets, they were met by large, high-contrast signs zip-tied to the barricades at eye level.

> NOTICE OF FILMING AND LIVE RECORDING
>
> Today's event is being recorded for commercial release. By entering the "Forger Nation" Tailgate Plaza and The Crucible, you consent to being filmed and recorded as part of the "Live at The Crucible" concert film and live album. Be loud. Be proud. You are part of history.

"Look at that," Mark said, grinning as he read the sign. "We're not just watching the show. We're in the show."

"That means the documentary crew is probably inside the plaza right now," Zoe noted, scanning the area. "We should ensure our energy levels are optimal."

Jordan tapped his **Poly Pass** on the scanner held by event staff. The device chirped, verifying his student status and his Tier 1 ticket.

"Welcome in, Jordan," the staffer smiled. "Enjoy the show."

He rolled through the barrier, passing the sign and crossing the threshold into the exclusive zone. The plaza was a sea of maroon and orange, but instead of jerseys, everyone was wearing band merch. It was a massive, exclusive block party for the people who made the university what it was.

There were food trucks serving local favorites (including a **Smokin' Anvil** stand serving BBQ), corn hole games set up with band logos, and a DJ blasting the band's deep cuts. And true to the sign, Jordan saw a camera crew moving through the crowd, capturing the joy of the fans.

"This is incredible," Megan said, looking around at the thousands of people who, like her, were alumni back home for the night.

"It's Forger Nation," Jordan said, a grin splitting his face. "It's just us."

They found a spot near the center, claiming a table. They spent the afternoon eating, laughing, and soaking in the atmosphere of a stadium that had once terrified him, now turned into the ultimate safe space—and the set of his favorite band's movie.

### Part 2: Return to The Crucible

The sun had set over Blacksburg, painting the sky in deep shades of purple and orange that matched the anticipation buzzing through the air. The massive concrete bowl of **The Crucible** glowed against the night, a beacon of light and sound visible from miles away.

For Jordan Kelly, approaching the stadium gates felt like walking toward a sleeping giant that had once tried to eat him alive.

It had been nearly two years since Saturday, September 27, 2014. That date was burned into his memory, the day of the **Homecoming Humiliation**. The last time he had wheeled through these gates, he had been a terrified freshman hoping for a win to validate his existence. Instead, he had watched his sanctuary be systematically destroyed by the symbolic forces of his mother's world, triggering a massive autistic meltdown that had sent him and his friends fleeing at halftime.

He gripped the push-rings of his wheelchair, his knuckles white. The roar of the crowd inside was already building, a low, thrumming vibration that he could feel in his chest. It was the same sound that had overwhelmed him two years ago, the same wall of noise that had driven him to silence.

But as they passed through the turnstiles, Jordan noticed the difference. The energy wasn't aggressive. There were no angry chants of "Beat Norfolk!" or tense anxieties about rankings. The crowd of 60,000 wasn't divided into maroon and blue; they were united. They were there for **The Stardust Engine**, the world-famous band of CPI alumni who had come home to end their **Electric Color Tour** where it all began.

The Group of 8 moved as a phalanx, their formation practiced and protective. They guided Jordan to the accessible seating area on the concourse, a prime spot with a clear view of the massive stage that had been erected over the football field.

Jordan locked his brakes. He wasn't rocking, but his body was rigid with tension. His eyes scanned the massive, vertical walls of people, the memory of the 38-0 scoreboard flashing in his mind like a phantom limb.

Mark, sitting in the row immediately behind him with Hannah, leaned over the railing. He placed a firm, brotherly hand on Jordan's shoulder.

"You okay, J?" Mark asked, his voice low so only Jordan could hear. "We don't have to prove anything. We can leave whenever you want. Just say the word."

Jordan looked at the field. He saw the stage lights testing their colors—vibrant teals, neon pinks, and electric blues—cutting through the darkness. He thought about the fear that had chased him out of here two years ago. He was done running.

"I'm okay," Jordan said, his voice steadying as he looked at the massive crowd. "I want to see this."

Aubrie and Madison, his two original roommates and safest people, sat in the chairs on either side of him. They didn't need to ask; they knew what he needed. Madison reached out and took his left hand, her grip warm and grounding. Aubrie took his right hand, lacing her fingers through his, her thumb gently rubbing his knuckles in a soothing rhythm.

"This is going to be different," Aubrie promised, leaning in close so he could hear her over the buzzing crowd. "We're rewriting the memory tonight."

Jordan squeezed their hands. He took a deep breath, inhaling the cool mountain air mixed with the scent of popcorn and electricity. He was surrounded by his fortress. He was safe. He turned his gaze to the stage, ready to let the music wash away the ghosts of the football game.

### Part 3: The Anvil Strike (The Opener)

The stadium was pitch black. The massive crowd of 60,000 was buzzing, a low-frequency hum of anticipation.

Jordan sat in the front row, gripping his wheel rims. He looked at the massive stage. The setup was dark, but he could see the silhouette of a giant prop in the center: a massive, iron anvil.

Then, a single spotlight hit center stage, illuminating **Ryan O'Connell** holding his battered, original 1986 electric guitar. A single, high feedback note whined through the speakers.

"Thirty years ago..." Ryan said into the mic, his voice echoing across the silent field. "We were just five kids in a garage on Progress Street. We didn't have a record deal. We didn't have a tour bus. All we had was a cassette tape and an idea."

He looked out at the darkness.

"We entered a contest to write a song for *this* stadium. We wanted to make something loud enough to scare the hell out of anyone who dared to come into our house!"

The crowd roared, a deafening sound of recognition.

"For thirty years, you guys have been stomping your feet to our song every single Saturday!" Ryan shouted. "So tonight, for the 30th Anniversary... let's see if we can break the seismographs! IGNITION!"

TING!

The single, high-pitched metallic note rang out. Clear and cold.

BOOM!

60,000 people stomped in unison. The ground shook.

TING! BOOM!

The rhythm sped up—*TING-BOOM, TING-BOOM*—building immense tension.

Then, Ryan slammed his hand down on the strings. The heavy, distorted industrial riff exploded, and the stage lights blasted Maroon and Orange into the night.

"The lights go dark, the tension builds..."

Ryan’s vocals were gritty and powerful, a call to battle. When the chorus hit, the entire stadium screamed the response.

"IGNITION!" (Crowd: IGNITION!) "This is the Forger's Call!"

Jordan threw his head back, screaming the words along with his family, feeling the vibration of the stomp in his chest. This wasn't just a song; it was a physical force.

As the song crashed to its chaotic, pyrotechnic finale, Ryan shouted one last time over the feedback.

"WELCOME TO THE CRUCIBLE!"

The crowd noise was deafening. Ryan smiled, breathless.

"Thank you! Oh, it feels so good to be home," he said, wiping sweat from his brow. "We wrote this next one about that feeling... that feeling of finally finding your own personal paradise... right here on this campus."

He signaled the band, ready for the next track. Jordan sat back, his heart racing, his face glowing. The concert had begun.

### Part 4: The Anthem in the Dark

The sun had fully set, and the anticipation in **The Crucible** was reaching a fever pitch. Jordan, Aubrie, Madison, and Megan were settled into their prime spots in **Section 104, Row A**. They were right against the barrier, perfectly positioned for the show.

A massive camera boom, suspended on a crane, swooped down over the crowd, its lens capturing the sea of maroon and orange. Jordan cheered, waving his **Ceremonial Ticket Stub** at the lens, hoping to make the final cut.

"Get that thing out of my face!"

The shout came from directly behind them. Jordan turned his head. A man in the row behind the accessible section—likely a guest of an alumnus given his lack of school colors—was standing up, yelling at a nearby security supervisor.

"I paid good money for these seats!" the man shouted, pointing at the camera crane. "I didn't pay to have a camera blocking my view every two minutes. I didn't sign a release for this! You can't film me without my permission!"

The security supervisor, a woman who looked like she had been dealing with this all night, didn't even blink. She calmly pointed a finger toward the massive digital scoreboard at the end of the field.

On the screen, in fifty-foot-tall letters, was the disclaimer: **NOTICE: FILMING IN PROGRESS.**

"Sir," the supervisor said, her voice polite but bored. "You consented when you scanned your ticket. It was on the purchase page. It is on the signs at the gate. It is on the scoreboard right now. If you do not wish to be recorded, you are free to leave the venue. Otherwise, please sit down."

The man sputtered, looking around for support. "This is ridiculous! Who even does a live album anymore?"

"We do!" Madison yelled, turning around in her seat with a dazzling, defiant grin. "We're Forger Nation! Smile for the camera!"

The students around the man laughed and cheered. Realizing he was outnumbered and legally wrong, the man grumbled and sat back down, crossing his arms.

Jordan turned back to the stage, sharing a conspiratorial grin with Aubrie. He looked at the camera boom swinging overhead again. He didn't care about his permission; he *wanted* the world to see him here. He wanted the proof that he had survived.

"Ready for your close-up?" Aubrie asked, squeezing his hand.

"Born ready," Jordan said.

Moments later, the stadium lights suddenly cut out, plunging The Crucible into a profound, absolute darkness. A roar went up from the crowd of 60,000, but it wasn't the aggressive, territorial roar of a football game that Jordan remembered with such dread. This was different. It was a wave of pure, unified anticipation.

A single, brilliant white spotlight snapped on, cutting through the black void to hit the center of the stage. It illuminated **Cassidy O’Connell**, the band's lead singer. She was standing at the mic stand, gripping it with both hands, vibrating with energy.

Behind her, the band kicked in with a soft melody that shook the ground under Jordan's wheels. It was loud, powerful, and unfamiliar. Jordan leaned forward, intrigued. He didn't know this track.

Cassidy leaned into the mic, her voice booming over the massive sound system, shouting over the roaring crowd and the driving beat.

"Blacksburg! We've been all over the world... but there is no better feeling... Than ending this entire tour... right here at home, in The Crucible!"

The crowd screamed, a sound of pure love. Jordan felt the vibration in his chest, rattling his ribs. Two years ago, that vibration had felt like a threat. Tonight, it felt like a heartbeat.

"Are you with us one last time, Forger Nation?" Cassidy shouted. "Let's go!"

The band surged forward, the guitars crunching and the drums thundering. Jordan watched, mesmerized by the spectacle, the lights sweeping over the sea of maroon and orange. He was enjoying the show, feeling safe between Aubrie and Madison, but he still didn't know what was coming.

Then, at exactly one minute and fifteen seconds in, the instrumental intro cut to a rhythmic pulse, and Cassidy began to sing.

Woke up this morning, the world was gray... Didn't know the colors had all faded away...

The lyrics hit Jordan like a physical shockwave.

His eyes widened, and a gasp stuck in his throat. He knew those words. He knew them by heart. It was **"Electric Color"**—his favorite song, his anthem. But this wasn't the synth-pop track from the 1987 record Mark had played for him in the basement. This was something new. It was a massive, roaring rock anthem version, transformed for the stadium.

Been living in a room with the curtains drawn tight... Then you flipped a switch and turned on the light...

Jordan’s heart exploded with pure, unadulterated joy. The realization washed over him, hot and electric. They were playing *his* song.

In that single instant, the dark, traumatic memory of the "Homecoming Humiliation"—the 38-0 scoreboard, the panic, the flight at halftime—was simply deleted. It was overwritten by this moment. The gray world of his mother's control, the fear of this stadium, the shame of his past... it all evaporated under the bright, crashing waves of the music.

He turned his head and looked at Aubrie. She was watching him, a brilliant smile on her face, singing along. She was the one who had flipped the switch.

He reached out and grabbed her hand, squeezing it as hard as he could. She squeezed back, anchoring him in the present, far away from the gray, and ready for the color to explode.

### Part 5: The Anthem Explodes

The driving beat from the verse didn't let up; it intensified. As Cassidy finished the line about flipping the switch, the tension on stage broke, and the song exploded into the massive, hook-heavy chorus.

Oh, I'm seeing in electric color!

The stage erupted with light. Brilliant beams of neon teal, magenta, and violet shot into the night sky, illuminating the entire bowl of The Crucible. Massive stadium drums thundered, and a soaring synthesizer melody filled the air, lifting the crowd off their feet in a single, unified wave of joy.

Jordan didn't flinch. He didn't cover his ears. He threw his head back and laughed. The sheer, overwhelming volume of the moment washed over him, cleansing him of the fear that had lived in this space for two years. He wasn't scared. The noise wasn't an assault; it was a song. It was the sound of 60,000 people sharing happiness.

It's an electric-colored feeling, And it all started with you!

Madison grabbed his left hand and sang at the top of her lungs, her face glowing in the stage lights. Jordan squeezed back, singing with her, his voice lost in the roar but his spirit soaring.

He looked to his right. Zoe, usually the most reserved and analytical member of their family, was dancing in the aisle. She wasn't analyzing the acoustics or checking the time; she was spinning, her arms in the air, lost in the rhythm. Even Mark and Hannah were swaying together, caught up in the anthem.

The song moved into the bridge. The beat got stronger, the tension building with every measure. The lights pulsed faster. The energy was reaching a fever pitch.

The beat is getting stronger I can't hide it any longer...

Suddenly, the music cut out completely.

The stadium fell into a hushed, electric silence, leaving only the hum of the amplifiers and the collective held breath of the crowd. **Ryan O'Connell**, the lead guitarist, wheeled into the spotlight next to Cassidy. He looked out at the sea of faces—his fellow alumni.

"Can you feel that, CPI?" Cassidy whispered into the mic, his voice intimate and echoing. "That's the sound of a new world. No more gray. Just... color."

Jordan felt a chill run down his spine. It felt as if the band were speaking directly to him, acknowledging the gray prison he had escaped and the vibrant life he had found.

Cassidy pointed a finger toward Ryan. "Here comes the solo!"

Ryan struck a note that screamed through the stadium. The solo soared, a high, piercing melody that seemed to reach up and touch the stars above them. The lights went wild, sweeping across the stands.

Jordan watched, mesmerized. He looked at the field where the football team had fallen, where he had felt so small and defeated. That ghost was gone. This wasn't the place where he had failed. This was the place where he was celebrating. He was surrounded by his pact, by his family, and he was seeing everything in electric color.

### Part 6: The Grand Finale

The song reached its breakdown. The driving 80s beat dropped out, leaving only a shimmering, suspended synth pad that hung in the air like stardust. Cassidy stepped to the edge of the stage, looking out at the sea of 60,000 illuminated faces.

"Just you!" she commanded, pointing to the stands. "Let me hear you sing it!"

She cut the mic.

Sixty thousand voices sang in unison, a massive, acapella choir echoing against the concrete walls of the stadium.

Oh-oh-oh! Electric Color!

Jordan joined in. His voice, usually so quiet, was strong and clear, blending perfectly with the thousands of others. He wasn't just an observer; he was a participant. He was part of the Forger Nation.

"I can't hear you!" Cassidy shouted, cupping her ear. "For the end of the tour!"

The crowd roared the chant again, louder this time, shaking the very foundations of The Crucible.

Oh-oh-oh! Electric Color!

And then, the explosion happened.

Tyler O’Connell stood up behind his kit and slammed into the drums with a force that shook the ground. The full weight of the band crashed back in—Evan's driving bass line locking in with the drums, while Ryan's guitar screamed a high, triumphant melody that soared over the stadium.

The sound was a colossal wall of 80s arena rock power.

"COME ON!"

The finale was a massive explosion of sound and light. Pyrotechnics erupted from the stage roof, shooting pillars of fire into the night sky. Confetti cannons fired from every corner of the field, filling the air with a blizzard of maroon and orange paper.

IT'S AN ELECTRIC-COLORED FEELING AND IT ALL STARTED WITH YOU!

Jordan watched the confetti rain down, the maroon and orange colors no longer symbols of a failed football game, but of a triumphant night. He felt the bass vibrating in his chest, overwriting the memory of the Homecoming Humiliation with a new, indelible memory of joy.

The song ended on a massive, sustained chord, the guitar feedback blending with the swelling synths, punctuated by a giant, final gong hit from Tyler's percussion riser that rang out into the night.

"Thank you Forger Nation! Thank you Blacksburg! We love you! We're home! Goodnight!"

The stage went black for a second before the house lights flooded the stadium, bright and abrupt.

Jordan sat in his chair, covered in maroon and orange confetti, breathless and beaming. His ears were ringing, his heart was racing, and he had never felt more alive. He looked at Aubrie, Madison, and the rest of his family, who were all grinning just as widely.

"That," he said, his voice filled with awe, "was way better than football."

The memory of the 38-0 loss was gone, buried under a mountain of electric color. He had reclaimed The Crucible.

### Part 7: The Departure Loop

The lights of **The Crucible** faded behind them as the Group of 11 navigated the crush of 60,000 fans exiting the stadium. The air was cool, filled with the smell of exhaust and the buzzing, high-energy chatter of the post-concert crowd.

They reached the **Crucible Bus Loop**, which had been transformed into a massive, humming transit hub. A long line of Blue Ridge Transit buses idled in the dedicated lanes, their engines rumbling in a low unison.

As they moved toward their designated sector, they passed a knot of people standing near a pole marked **ROANOKE EXPRESS**. They were shouting at a weary-looking transit supervisor.

"What do you mean it's gone?" a man in a denim jacket yelled. "We need to get back to the Third Street Station!"

"Sir, as the signs stated," the supervisor said, pointing to a large, illuminated board, "the last Roanoke Express departed at 7:45 PM. RVT service has ended for the night."

"But the concert just ended!" a woman next to him cried. "How are we supposed to get home?"

"There is no return service," the supervisor repeated. "You will need to call a cab or a rideshare."

Jordan watched them as he rolled past, feeling a profound relief that his family had planned their logistics with military precision.

Just past the angry Roanoke crowd, however, was a very different scene. At the **Bluewater Transit** bay, a large, familiar coach bus was idling, its engine purring. It was the last **Maroon Express** of the night.

**Sign:** MAROON EXPRESS ... BLUEWATER via FALLING BRANCH **Voice:** "Bluewater Transit Maroon Express: Bluewater via Falling Branch."

"Look at that," Madison said, pointing to the happy fans boarding the coach. "My hometown bus didn't leave them stranded. 10:15 PM departure. Right on time."

Unlike the Roanoke fans, the Bluewater crew was cheering, high-fiving the driver as they boarded. The concert had finished just in time for the final scheduled run, saving them from being stuck.

They continued to **Sector 4**. The Blue Ridge Transit queue was organized chaos.

To their left, a bus idled with its doors open. **Sign:** FOXRIDGE ... FORGERS SHUTTLE **Voice:** *"Shuttle to Foxridge. GO FORGERS!"*

Ahead of them, another bus was loading a massive crowd of students. **Sign:** HETHWOOD ... FORGERS SHUTTLE **Voice:** *"Shuttle to Hethwood. GO FORGERS!"*

And right in front of them, waiting at the curb with the ramp already deploying, was their bus. **Sign:** FALLING BRANCH ... FORGERS SHUTTLE **Voice:** *"Shuttle to Falling Branch. GO FORGERS!"*

"That's us," Daniel said, cutting through the noise. "Let's go home."

Jordan wheeled himself up the ramp. The driver, looking exhausted but happy, nodded at him. "Great show, right?"

"The best," Jordan said.

He maneuvered into the securement spot. Aubrie took the seat next to him, and the rest of the family filled the rows behind them. The doors hissed shut, cutting off the noise of the crowd.

"Next stop, Falling Branch," the automated voice announced, followed by one last, triumphant, *"GO FORGERS!"*

The bus pulled away, merging onto the dedicated lane and leaving the chaos of the stadium in the rearview mirror. Jordan leaned his head on Aubrie's shoulder, his ears ringing with the phantom sound of the gong, his heart full of electric color. The night was over, but the memory was just beginning.
