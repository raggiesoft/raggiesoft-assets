# Book: Fall 2016

## Chapter 1: A Familiar Face

### Part 1: Reunion in the Hallway

It was the first day of their junior year, Fall 2016. The Group of 8 was gathered in the hallway outside their Latin 101 class, a small classroom designed for around thirty students. Jordan Shawn Kelly, his legal name now reflecting his new life, was chatting with Aubrie, his wife, while Mark and Madison argued playfully about the upcoming football season.

A new student approached, looking at the room number, her face a little nervous. She glanced at the group, and then her gaze locked onto Jordan. She stopped, her expression shifting from uncertainty to a look of profound, hopeful shock.

"Aubrey?" she asked, her voice hesitant.

Jordan looked up from his conversation with Aubrie. He froze. He couldn't believe it. He was looking into those same warm, welcoming hazel eyes he hadn't seen in four years. The face was more mature, but the kindness was identical.

"Jessica?" he breathed, his voice full of shocked disbelief. "Is it really you?"

"Yes, Aubrey, it's me! It's Jessica!" she said, her own smile widening as tears welled in her eyes.

The rest of the Group of 8 went quiet, instantly recognizing that something powerful and private was happening. Aubrie, his wife, watched, her expression open and supportive.

"Oh, Jess," Jordan said, his voice thick with emotion. Jordan was the only one ever allowed to call her Jess. "I missed you so much."

He pushed his wheelchair forward slightly, his arms opening. "I go by Jordan now," he said, just as he pulled her into a hug that was four long, painful years in the making.

Jessica knelt to meet his embrace, holding him tightly. "I missed you, too," she whispered into his shoulder, her voice cracking. "I thought about you all the time. I... I'm so sorry about your mother. I never..."

"I know," he said, pulling back. He understood. He had been rescued by his mother's actions, even as she had tried to destroy his life.

The Group of 8 looked on, seeing the profound, healing weight of the moment for their friend. Jessica finally stood, wiping a tear from her eye. Jordan, his face glowing, turned to the woman at his side.

"Jess, this is... my family," he said. He started with the woman holding his hand. "This is my wife, Aubrie Jean Kelly."

Jessica’s eyes went wide, but she smiled, seeing the pure, unconditional love between them. Jordan then proceeded to introduce her to Madison, Zoe, Lauren, Sarah, Hannah, and Mark.

"Wow," Jessica said, looking at the circle of kind, smiling faces. "It's... it's amazing to meet all of you."

"You, too," Hannah said warmly, speaking for the group. "It looks like we're in the same class. You should sit with us, unless there are assigned seats".

Jessica looked back at Jordan—the boy she had tried to save, who was now a man surrounded by a fortress of love. She smiled, a real, genuine smile. "I'd like that. I'd like that a lot."

### Part 2: The Cosmic Coincidence

The Latin 101 class ended. The "Group of 8" gathered their things, buzzing with the excitement of the reunion. As they filed out of the small classroom, the group began to split off.

"Alright, J, we'll see you at lunch," Mark said, giving him a supportive nod as he and Hannah headed toward the engineering quad.

It was just Jordan, Aubrie, and Jessica left in the hallway. Jordan turned to Jessica, his heart full but already bracing for the goodbye. "It was so good to see you, Jess. I still can't believe it. Which way are you heading?"

"Oh," Jessica said, pointing down the main walkway. "I have to get to the science complex. My next class is Astrophysics."

Jordan and Aubrie both stopped, staring at her. "No way!" Jordan said, his voice full of excitement. "Us too! You should walk with us".

Jessica immediately looked hesitant, her gaze flickering to Aubrie. She was acutely aware that this was his *wife*. "Are you sure?" she asked, her voice quiet. "I don't want to intrude on you two."

Aubrie, seeing the hesitation, immediately stepped in, her smile warm and genuine. "Intrude? Don't be silly. Of course you're walking with us". She looped her arm through Jessica's. "What professor do you have?"

"Dr. Alistair, at 10:30," Jessica replied.

Jordan and Aubrie stopped again, this time looking at each other in shared, stunned delight. "Us too!" Jordan nearly shouted. "And the Tuesday morning lab section?"

"Yes!" Jessica said, finally letting herself believe the coincidence. "I can't believe it!"

"Then it's settled," Aubrie said, her voice full of a happy finality as she started walking, pulling Jessica along with her. "You're sitting with us".

Jordan wheeled his chair forward, placing himself between Aubrie and Jessica. He was so excited. He couldn't believe it. The friend he had lost, the one his mother had stolen from him, was back. And now, by some miracle, she was in not only his Latin class, but also his Astrophysics class. His past and his present had just collided, and his world was, once again, made whole.

### Part 3: An Unexpected Free Block

The trio made their way to the science complex, arriving at the large lecture hall for their 10:30 AM Astrophysics class. But when they got to the door, they saw a single piece of paper taped to the glass.

"Oh, look," Aubrie said, leaning in to read it. "Class is canceled. The professor is sick".

"Huh," Jordan said, a little disappointed, but mostly relieved. "Well, that gives us a 50-minute free block". He turned to Jessica, his face bright. "We can actually talk. We should go grab a drink at Newman Hall."

Jessica's smile immediately faltered. She glanced nervously at Aubrie, then back at Jordan. "Oh, I shouldn't... I mean, you two are married. I don't want to intrude on your time".

Aubrie just laughed, a warm, genuine sound that dismissed the very idea. "Intrude? Jessica, you're the first real friend he ever had. You're not intruding, you're catching up. We insist. We're not letting you go".

Jessica still looked hesitant, her high school memories of social rules and boundaries making her anxious. Aubrie, seeing this, decided to be more direct. She gently took Jessica's hand. Then she took Jordan's hand. She placed Jessica's hand into Jordan's and gently, deliberately, laced their fingers together.

"See?" Aubrie said, her voice full of bliss as she looked at their joined hands. "No jealousy. He's your friend. Hold his hand".

Jessica was stunned. She was holding a married man's hand, and his *wife* was smiling at her. It was the most radical, unconditional act of trust she had ever witnessed.

Jordan's grip tightened on her hand, a grounding, familiar connection. He needed to tell her. "Jess," he said, using the old nickname that only he was allowed to use. "So much has happened. I... I never got to thank you. My mother..."

At the mention of his mother, Jessica flinched, the memory still sharp and painful. She **remembers this** all too well. She wasn't just told to stay away; Aubrie Sinclair had found her home phone number. Jessica remembered the rude and nasty voice on the line, the sound of her own mother's shocked silence as Aubrie Sinclair went on a tirade, telling them that Jessica was a manipulative predator and was to have nothing to do with Aubrey ever again.

Seeing the fear flash in Jessica's eyes, Jordan's grip tightened. "It's okay, Jess. She's... she's not..."

"She's gone," Aubrie Kelly said, her voice calm and absolute. She sat on Jessica's other side, taking her free hand. "Jessica, I need you to hear this. **Jordan has cut his mother completely out of his life**".

Aubrie proceeded to give her the short, factual version of the war they had all survived: the failed Fall Break confrontation, the official no-contact orders filed with the university and the police, and the final, humiliating court battle where a judge had dismissed his mother's guardianship case with prejudice.

"She has no power," Aubrie finished. "She can't find him, and she can't hurt him. Not ever again. **You are absolutely welcomed back into Jordan’s life**".

Jessica looked from Aubrie's fierce, protective face to Jordan's, who was nodding, his eyes full of relief. The fear that she had carried for four years—the fear of that horrible woman—finally began to dissolve.

"Now," Jordan said, a quiet pride in his voice. "About that 4.0 GPA...".

He continued his story, telling her about the Homecoming Humiliation, and then his voice dropped. "But... it hasn't all been easy. Last year... Jess, I had a heart attack".

Jessica's head snapped toward him, her eyes wide with terror. "Wait, what? A heart attack? Jordan, you're only 20! What do you mean, a heart attack?".

Aubrie, sitting on Jessica's other side, took her hand. "It's true," she said softly, and she began to fill in the details of the Homecoming Humiliation, the failed balloon angioplasty, and the open-heart CABG surgery.

Jordan's grip tightened on Jessica's hand as his wife spoke, the memory of the trauma still fresh. Jessica was reeling, trying to process everything: his new life, his marriage, his near-death, and the impossible, wonderful fact that she was holding his hand while his wife, Aubrie Kelly, looked on with nothing but love. This was a family unlike any she had ever known, and she knew, with a sudden, dawning clarity, that she wanted to be a part of it.

### Part 4: The Fortress of Truth (Redux)

With their 50-minute Astrophysics block unexpectedly free, the trio decided to walk from the science complex to Newman Hall to grab a coffee and find a quiet place to talk. Jordan’s heart was hammering, a mixture of joy and disbelief. He was still holding Jessica’s hand, his fingers laced with hers.

They found a quiet table in a corner of the bustling student union. Jessica was still trying to process the situation. She was holding a married man's hand, and his wife, Aubrie, was sitting right next to her, smiling and encouraging it. It was a wonderful, terrifying, and completely illogical feeling.

"It's almost lunchtime," Aubrie said, pulling out her mPhone. "We should get the others."

She sent a quick message to the Jordan Comms chat. "Hey everyone, class was canceled. Jordan and I are already at Newman Hall. We've got Jessica with us, and we have news we'd like to share".

A few minutes later, the rest of the Group of 8—Mark, Hannah, Madison, Zoe, Lauren, and Sarah—arrived, their own morning classes finished.

"Hey!" Madison called out, her voice cheerful as they approached the table. "What's the big—"

She stopped, her eyes landing on Jordan's and Jessica's intertwined hands. The rest of the group saw it, too. Jessica tensed, a flush rising on her neck, bracing herself for the inevitable confrontation.

But the confrontation never came. The Group of 8 was completely unfazed. They were used to Jordan being openly affectionate with the women of his pact; to them, seeing him hold hands with a new woman wasn't shocking, just new.

"So, who's your new friend, J?" Mark asked, grabbing a chair and sitting down.

"That's the news," Jordan said, his voice thick with emotion. He looked at Aubrie, who gave him an encouraging nod. "Guys, this is Jessica. She's... she's not a new friend."

The group leaned in, and Jordan, still holding Jessica's hand, told them the entire story. He told them how he and Jess—a nickname he alone was allowed to use —had a secret relationship back in high school, how she was his first real friend and girlfriend. He explained how she was also autistic and understood him, and how his mother, Aubrie Sinclair, had discovered their secret.

His voice grew cold as he recounted how his mother, in a bitter rage, had called Jessica's parents with rude, nasty accusations, forbidding him from ever seeing her again and successfully shutting down the only good relationship he had ever known.

The group was silent, stunned. This wasn't just a new friend; this was the *original* victim of his mother's abuse.

Hannah, the group's mom friend, was the first to speak, her eyes full of warmth as she looked at Jessica. "Oh, Jessica," she said, her voice soft. "I am so, so sorry that happened to you. But you're here now."

"Yeah," Madison added, her voice firm. "You're with us now." She smiled, breaking the tension. "And we're all starving. Jessica, you have to join us for lunch".

Jessica looked around at the circle of kind, smiling, and fiercely protective faces. She looked down at Jordan, who was squeezing her hand. "I'd love to," she whispered.

### Part 5: The Separation Crash

The first day of their junior year, Monday, was drawing to a close. The group of nine—the Group of 8 plus Jessica—had spent the entire day together, moving from Latin, to lunch, to their shared Astrophysics lecture, and then to the library for a study session. Jordan was vibrating with a joy he hadn't felt in years, his hand securely holding Jessica's.

They arrived at the CPI Transit Center, the central hub where their paths would have to diverge. The reality of the moment began to settle in. The express bus back to the Christiansburg compound was arriving, and Jessica, who lived in a dorm on campus, would have to walk back.

It was time to say goodbye.

"Well," Jessica said, her voice hesitant as she started to gently pull her hand away. "This was... this was an amazing day, Jordan. I'll see you in class tomorrow."

The simple act of her letting go was the trigger.

Jordan's grip on her hand tightened with a desperate, white-knuckled strength. "No," he whispered, his voice suddenly small and tight. "You... you can't leave."

"Jordan, I have to," she said gently. "My dorm is that way."

"No!" he said, his voice cracking. The terror of his 16-year-old self resurfaced, the memory of his mother's rude and nasty phone call and the agony of losing his first, secret girlfriend. His brain couldn't distinguish between a temporary goodbye and the permanent, traumatic separation his mother had forced on them. He was losing her all over again.

A low, guttural hum started in his chest. His body began to rock back and forth in his wheelchair. He was having a massive autistic meltdown, right there on the crowded bus platform.

The Group of 8, who could not bear to see Jordan in so much emotional distress, immediately formed their protective fortress around him.

"J, you're okay, you're safe," Madison said, her hand on his shoulder.

Mark and Hannah exchanged a panicked look. They knew he was no longer on sternal precautions, but his sternum was still surgically repaired. A full, violent meltdown where he tensed his chest was still dangerous. They had to pull him out of the meltdown, not just wait for it to pass.

"Jordan, stop tensing!" Mark said, his voice firm.

But Jordan was lost, his eyes squeezed shut, shaking his head. "She's leaving... she's leaving..."

Aubrie Kelly, his wife, immediately understood. This wasn't a normal overload; this was a trauma response. She stepped forward, putting her hand on Jessica's arm.

"He can't say goodbye," Aubrie said, her decision absolute. "So don't. Come home with us. To Christiansburg. Just for the night. He just needs to know you're not gone."

Jessica stared at her, her eyes wide with confusion. "What? To your... your *house*?" she stammered. "Aubrie, I can't. You're his *wife*. I'm not... I can't intrude."

"This is the No Jealousy Pact," Aubrie insisted, her voice firm but kind. "He's hurting. He needs *you*. That's all that matters. Please."

Jessica looked past Aubrie at Jordan, who was now openly crying, his face a mask of terror, still rocking. She couldn't stand to see him in this much pain. Then, she thought of her own dorm. She thought of her loud, obnoxious roommates she was not getting along with. A night away from them, in a safe, quiet house, was a blessing in disguise.

She made her decision. She took Jordan's other hand. "Okay," she said, her voice clear. "Okay, Jordan. I'm not leaving. I'm coming with you."

Instantly, the rocking slowed. The low hum in his chest subsided. He let out a long, shuddering, tearful breath. He was still overwhelmed, but the trigger—the separation—was gone.

### Part 6: Forge Hall

Zoe, the logistician, immediately took charge. "Okay. We missed the 5:15 PM express. Jessica, you need to pack an overnight bag. We'll all go with you."

The entire group of nine left the transit center, their formation a familiar, protective bubble around Jordan and Jessica. They walked across campus to Forge Hall, one of the newer dorms that had been built after Bennett Hall was demolished.

Jessica swiped her Poly Pass to let them in. When she unlocked her dorm room door, the Group of 8 saw for themselves exactly why she had been so quick to accept their offer. The room was a mess, loud music was blaring from a speaker, and two other girls were sitting on their beds, barely looking up as nine people filed into their small space.

"Hey, Jess," one roommate said, not unkindly, but with a total lack of warmth. "Who are all these people?"

"They're my friends," Jessica said quietly. "I'm just grabbing some things. I'm staying with them tonight."

The other roommate just scoffed, not even looking up from her phone. "Whatever."

The Group of 8 exchanged a single, silent look. They saw for themselves that the environment was cold, cliquey, and that Jessica clearly did not get along with her roommates.

Hannah, ever the "mom friend," immediately went into action. "Okay, Jess, where's your bag?"

The group helped her pack everything she'd need: toiletries, her laptop, and clothes for the night and for class the next day. They were in and out in less than five minutes.

As the door clicked shut behind them, Madison put a supportive arm around Jessica. "Okay, yeah, you're *definitely* better off with us."

The group of nine walked back to the CPI Transit Center, arriving just in time for the 6:15 PM express bus to Christiansburg. Jessica, with her overnight bag at her feet, sat next to Jordan (he transferred out of his wheelchair to be with her), her hand safely and securely back in his.

### Part 7: The Christiansburg Sanctuary

The express bus from CPI pulled into the Falling Branch Park & Ride just after 6:45 PM. The group of nine, now including Jessica, made the short, quiet walk to the Brooks' house. Jessica was nervous, clutching the straps of her overnight bag, her mind still reeling from the events at the transit center.

They entered the house to the warm smell of dinner cooking. Daniel and Laura Brooks, who had already been briefed via the Jordan Comms chat, greeted them at the door.

"You must be Jessica," Laura said, her voice warm and immediately putting Jessica at ease. She didn't look surprised or judgmental, just kind. "We're so glad you're here. Welcome to our home."

"Thank you for having me, ma'am," Jessica said politely.

"Oh, it's just Laura," she smiled.

A woman with Zoe's analytical eyes and a kind smile came over. "Hi, Jessica, I'm Megan, Zoe's older sister." She offered a hand. "I just got integrated into this crazy family myself. Don't worry, they're the best."

It was a little past 7 PM by the time the entire, expanded family—now twelve people—was gathered around the massive dining table for dinner. Jessica sat between Jordan and Megan. The atmosphere was loud, loving, and chaotic. She watched as this massive family passed plates, argued about classes, and included Jordan in every conversation. It was overwhelming, but in the best way.

### Part 8: The Sofa Assumption

After dinner, the Group of 8 and Jessica migrated down to the basement, their designated territory. Mark and Hannah settled onto one of the queen beds to watch a movie. The others—Aubrie, Madison, Zoe, Lauren, and Sarah—piled onto the massive Alaskan King bed with Jordan, a familiar, comfortable "puppy pile."

Jessica stood awkwardly by the stairs, her overnight bag at her feet. She was an island in their ocean of intimacy. She assumed, logically, that she would be sleeping on the sectional sofa or in a guest room.

Megan, sensing her unease, got up from the table where she'd been working and sat with her on the sofa. "Hey," Megan said softly. "It's... a lot, isn't it?"

"I just... I don't understand," Jessica whispered, looking at the king bed where Jordan was cuddled between his wife, Aubrie, and Madison. "How is she... okay with this?"

"Because she means it," Megan said. "It took me a long time to get it, too. I'm 27, and my brain just about broke trying to understand. But the pact is real. The no jealousy rule is real. They just... love him. And they love each other. They're not just a couple; they're a system."

### Part 9: The Separation Trigger

As the hour grew later, Jessica knew it was time. She stood up, her stomach twisting. "Well," she said, her voice small. "I should probably figure out the sofa..."

The word sofa cut through Jordan's relaxed state. He had been fine, surrounded by his pact, knowing Jessica was in the room. But sofa meant separate. It meant other." It meant she wasn't *with* him.

His breath hitched. He sat up, his eyes wide with a familiar, rising panic. He looked at Jessica, and his mind was no longer in the safe basement; it was back on the bus platform, watching her walk away. He was terrified of waking up and finding her gone, a repeat of the trauma his mother had inflicted.

"No," he said, his voice tight. "You... you're not leaving, are you?"

"I'm just going to the sofa, Jordan..."

"No!" he said, his voice cracking. He started to rock, the low hum of his anxiety returning. "You can't. Please. Don't leave."

### Part 10: The Invitation

The pact, seeing the pre-meltdown signals, immediately went on high alert. Aubrie, his wife, slid off the bed and knelt in front of Jessica.

"He's not melting down because of you," Aubrie said, her voice calm and steady. "He's terrified of losing you again. His mother's 'shut down' must have done more damage than any of us knew."

"But... what do I do?" Jessica asked, her own eyes wide with distress. "I don't want to upset him!"

"Then don't leave," Aubrie said, her smile warm. "We don't want you on the sofa. We want you *here*. With us. In the puppy pile."

Jessica was floored. "In... in the *bed*? With you? And... and all of you?"

"Yes," Madison confirmed from the bed. "But you have a choice. This is the most important rule. You *always* have a choice. We have a guest room upstairs if you want it. No one will be mad. But we want you to know you are welcome here. We *want* you to join us."

Jessica looked at Jordan. He was still rocking, his face a mask of fear, terrified she would choose the guest room. She knew he wouldn't calm down until he knew she was secure. She didn't want to see him go through another meltdown.

She took a deep breath. "Okay," she whispered. "I... I want the puppy pile."

### Part 11: The Rules of the Pile

The relief that washed over Jordan was visible; the rocking stopped instantly. He let out a long, shaky breath.

As Jessica hesitantly moved toward the massive Alaskan King bed, Hannah stepped in.

"Okay," Hannah said, her "mom friend" voice gentle but firm. "Welcome to the family, Jessica. Now, the rules."

Jessica tensed again. "Rules?"

"Just one, really," Hannah said, "and it's for his safety. You know he had open-heart surgery, right?" Jessica nodded.

"His sternum is surgically repaired," Hannah explained. "It's healed, but it's a lifelong change. That means the puppy pile is calm. No sudden weight on his chest, no jostling, and no pulling on his arms or chest. We're here to cuddle and keep him safe, not to be chaotic. We just... we have to be careful."

Jessica nodded, understanding. This wasn't just an invitation; it was an act of shared care.

She kicked off her shoes and climbed onto the massive bed. The six other women of the pact made room for her, and Jordan, with a look of profound, tearful gratitude, pulled her into the pile. She was home.

### Part 12: The Final Straw

On Tuesday afternoon, the Group of 8 walked Jessica back to Forge Hall. The plan was for her to quickly grab her books and a change of clothes before heading to the transit center with them. Jordan, Aubrie, and Madison accompanied her up to her room while the rest of the group waited in the lobby.

"I'll just be a second," Jessica said, fumbling for her Poly Pass.

She unlocked her door and pushed it open. The three of them froze on the threshold.

The room was dark, the curtains drawn, but it was not empty. Loud, thumping bass music was still playing, as it always was. And in the dim light, they could clearly see that both of her roommates were in their respective beds, and neither was alone. They were actively engaged with their boyfriends, completely oblivious to the door opening. The room was a mess, clothes strewn everywhere, and the air felt thick and stale.

Jessica let out a small, strangled sound, a mixture of disgust and humiliation. This was her *home*. Her *space*. And it had been completely **violated**.

"Oh, my god," Madison whispered, her face a mask of disgust.

Jessica immediately slammed the door shut, her entire body shaking. "I... I can't," she whispered, leaning against the wall in the hallway. "I'm not going back in there. I **never want to come back** to this room. Ever".

Aubrie, her face a mask of cold fury, put her arm around Jessica's shoulders. "You're not," she said, her voice an absolute promise. She pulled out her mPhone. "You're moving out. Tonight."

She immediately sent a message to the Jordan Comms chat.

**Aubrie:** \[URGENT\] \[LOGISTICS\] Change of plans. Jessica is moving out. Now. Her roommates are disgusting pigs. We're at Forge Hall. Daniel, Laura, we need a car. We're getting her things.

### Part 13: The Extraction

The group's response was instantaneous. Zoe, Mark, and the others came up from the lobby. Within fifteen minutes, **Daniel and Laura Brooks arrived** in the minivan, Daniel's face set with a grim determination.

This time, Mark and Daniel led the charge. They didn't knock. Mark threw the door open, flooding the room with light from the hallway.

The occupants of the beds scrambled, shouting in protest.

"Everyone out," Daniel's voice boomed, carrying the full authority of a father. "Now. You have sixty seconds to become decent and leave the room. My daughter's friends are moving her things."

The roommates and their boyfriends, terrified and embarrassed, grabbed their clothes and fled into the hallway, muttering curses.

The Group of 8 descended on the room like a practiced extraction team. Hannah and Lauren grabbed her bedding, Zoe and Sarah packed her desk, while the others grabbed her clothes. They had all of her possessions packed and loaded into the minivan in under ten minutes.

As they walked away from Forge Hall for the last time, Jessica looked at the chaotic, loud, and disrespectful dorm. She then looked at Jordan, waiting for her by the van, his hand outstretched. She knew, with absolute certainty, that she would rather have the puppy pile and its serene calmness than the chaos of her roommates. She had found her real home.

### Part 14: The New Social Script

It was Tuesday night, their second night with Jessica. After the traumatic "extraction" from Forge Hall, the group settled into the basement. The mood was calm, safe, and pointedly non-sexual, a direct response to the trauma Jessica had experienced. As bedtime approached, the group began their puppy pile ritual, climbing onto the massive Alaskan King bed.

Jessica, however, stood awkwardly by the sofa, her overnight bag clutched in her hands. She was quietly sobbing, her body shaking as she processed the day. She was completely overwhelmed. Her mind was stuck on the scene from her dorm. It was not just the violation of her space; it was the betrayal. She had witnessed affairs in progress. She had seen two girls casually cheating on their boyfriends. Her old social programming was screaming at her: This is what happens. Intimacy is a lie. Relationships are a violation.

Aubrie Kelly paused and looked over, her expression soft. "Jess? You're not sleeping on the sofa. Come on."

Jessica flinched, her sob catching in her throat. "Oh, no, I... I can't. That's... that's your bed. With... with your husband. I can't."

Her autistic mind was in a deadlock. Her old social programming was clear: You do not climb into a bed with another woman's husband. That is what my disgusting roommates did. That is an affair.

Aubrie, whose own autistic mind understood the need for clear, logical rules, knew she had to provide an explicit override. She slid off the bed and walked over to Jessica, leading her to the bed. She took her by the hands.

"Hey. Look at me," Aubrie said, her voice kind but firm. "This is not Forge Hall. You didn't just see a betrayal. You saw a violation without consent or respect. This," she gestured to the king bed, "is our family. It is built on consent and respect."

She began to walk Jessica toward the bed. "I am Jordan's wife," she continued, "and I am giving you permission".

Jessica tensed, her mind still fighting it.

Aubrie understood. She knew this would take time and she would need to be reminded a few times. "He needs his friends, Jess. He needs you. He had a meltdown yesterday because he was terrified of losing you again. We are not letting that happen."

She sat Jessica on the edge of the bed, then sat next to her, between her and Jordan. She took Jordan's hand, then took Jessica's hand, placing them near each other on the blanket. "This is okay," Aubrie said. "This is safe. This is what we do."

Jordan, sensing her hesitation, just gave her a small, hopeful smile. He didn't push, he just waited.

Jessica's mind was lagging, trying to process the new data. The wife is encouraging this. The wife is sitting right here. This is not a violation. This is a new rule. It was the first step. For tonight, she just sat on the edge of the bed, her hand near his, and let herself be part of the serene calmness until she finally, nervously, agreed to lie down on the very edge of the massive mattress.

Aubrie knew this reprogramming would need to be reminded a few times. She would have to lead this exercise every night, patiently demonstrating that in this family, it's perfectly acceptable to cuddle the man they all loved.

She sat Jessica on the edge of the bed, then sat next to her, between her and Jordan. She took Jordan's hand, then took Jessica's hand, placing them near each other on the blanket. "This is okay," Aubrie said. "This is safe. This is what we do."

Jordan, sensing her hesitation, just gave her a small, hopeful smile. He didn't push, he just waited.

Jessica's mind was lagging, trying to process the new data. *The wife is encouraging this. The wife is sitting right here. This is not a violation. This is a new rule.* It was the first step. For tonight, she just sat on the edge of the bed, her hand near his, and let herself be part of the serene calmness until she finally, nervously, agreed to lie down on the very edge of the massive mattress.

Aubrie knew this reprogramming would need to be reminded a few times. She would have to lead this exercise every night, patiently demonstrating that in this family, it's perfectly acceptable to cuddle the man they all loved.

Jessica's social script was slowly rewritten; she began to accept that cuddling with a married man was okay, in this specific case with Jordan. The other women of the pact, seeing her hesitation, slowly gathered onto the bed, gently introducing Jessica to the concept of the puppy pile. They were all careful of Jordan's surgically repaired sternum, which was healed but still a lifelong consideration.

Finally, Jessica embraced Jordan, cuddling with him properly. It was the hug she had been wanting to give him since he was 16 years old, four long years in the making.

The physical contact, the reality of finally holding him, broke the last of her composure. She was sobbing quietly as she clung to him, not from sadness, but from a profound, overwhelming sense of relief.

Jordan just held her, his own tears running silently into her hair. "I missed you so much, Jess," he whispered.

"I missed you, too," she cried.

The rest of the women of the pact were in the bed, making sure that Jessica was okay. Aubrie, his wife, settled in on Jordan's other side, placing her hand on his arm. Madison, Megan, and the others found their spots, creating the full, calm, and protective puppy pile. They were a fortress of love, and Jessica was finally, truly, safe inside.

## Chapter 2: Homecoming 2016

### Part 1: Midnight at The Foundry

It was 11:55 PM on Thursday night, the eve of the album release. A line of excited students snaked out the doors of The Foundry, winding through the lobby of Newman Hall. In 2014, Jordan had felt like an invisible ghost in this building, too poor to buy a hoodie and too scared to speak.

Tonight, he was at the front of the line.

The entire Group of 9 was with him. They were all decked out in their maroon and orange gear, buzzing with caffeine and excitement. Jessica, fully integrated into the family, stood comfortably between Jordan and Aubrie, her hand resting on the handle of his wheelchair.

At midnight, the doors opened. The industrial-chic interior of The Foundry was transformed. Neon teal and magenta lights—the colors of the Electric Color tour—washed over the exposed brick walls, mixing with the university's maroon and orange branding.

Jordan wheeled himself directly to the display tower in the center of the store. There it was. The Deluxe Vinyl Box Set of Live at The Crucible. The cover art was a stunning, high-contrast shot of the stadium lit up during the finale, the confetti raining down like stardust.

He picked up the heavy box set. He didn't look at the price tag with fear this time. He pulled out his wallet, took out his debit card—linked to the bank account he managed with Hannah's help —and paid for it himself.

"Receipt in the bag?" the cashier asked.

"No," Jordan smiled, holding the box set tight against his chest. "I'll carry it."

### Part 2: The Homecoming Concert

Later that Friday afternoon, the celebration moved outside. As part of the official Homecoming festivities, **The Stardust Engine** was playing a free, open-air mini-concert on the steps of **Newman Hall**, facing down the Alumni Mall toward the Pylons.

But it wasn't just a concert; it was a feast.

The air wasn't just crisp with autumn leaves; it was thick with the delicious, savory smell of burgers, fries, and tacos. Lined up along the bottom of the steps were a dozen official CPI Dining Services food carts. But there were no cash registers, and no one was swiping a meal plan.

A massive banner draped over the center cart read: **LUNCH IS ON THE ENGINE ROOM. EAT UP, FORGERS.**

Jordan sat in his wheelchair in the front row, a half-eaten taco on a paper plate in his lap and a cold soda in the cupholder of his chair. His family formed their familiar, protective semi-circle around him. On his left, his wife Aubrie held his hand. On his right, Jessica held the other. They were all eating, laughing, and surrounded by 5,000 other students who were currently enjoying the universal currency of college: free food.

Ryan O'Connell, the band's guitarist, stepped up to the mic, strumming a lazy, happy chord on his acoustic guitar.

"How's the food?" Ryan asked, his voice booming over the lawn. "You guys getting enough to eat?"

The crowd cheered, many raising their paper plates and sodas in a toast, mouths full.

"Good," Ryan said, his smile turning a little wistful as he looked up at the building behind him. "Because I remember starving on these exact steps waiting for a student loan check that never came. We used to scrape up nickels just to buy a bag of chips in that hallway behind me."

He gestured to the carts. "But today... the menu is open, and the tab is settled. Eat up, Forgers!"

The crowd roared their appreciation. It was the ultimate "Rich Alumni" move, but it felt grounded and real. They weren't buying a library; they were buying lunch for the kids who needed it.

"This set is for the ones building the future on an empty stomach," Ryan said, kicking the band into a tight, high-energy set of their hits.

They played for an hour, feeding off the energy of the home crowd. Then, as the sun began to dip lower, **Cassidy O’Connell**, the lead singer, stepped to the mic for the finale.

"We couldn't leave without playing this one," she said, smiling at the crowd. "This is for everyone who found their color right here in Blacksburg."

She hit the opening piano motif of **"Electric Color"**.

The crowd cheered, but for Jordan, the world fell away. He wasn't thinking about the football game, or his mother, or the surgery. He was just feeling the music.

Oh, I'm seeing in electric color!

When the chorus hit, Jordan sang along at the top of his lungs, his voice joining the thousands of other Forgers. He squeezed Aubrie's hand. He squeezed Jessica's hand. He looked back at Mark and Hannah, at Zoe and Madison, at Lauren and Sarah and Megan.

He was so happy. The song swelled to its final, triumphant chord, ringing out across the campus. Jordan closed his eyes, letting the sound wash over him. He was home, he was loved, and his life was bright, electric, and entirely his own.

### Part 3: The Basement Premiere

"Screw the football game," he said, looking at the TV with a dismissive wave. "I want to be back there."

He looked at the two options in his hands. The Blu-Ray disc offered the visuals, but the TV in the basement was just a standard 1080p set with basic built-in stereo speakers. It would sound tinny and flat.

Then he looked at the Maroon and Orange splatter vinyl. He looked over at Mark’s setup—the high-end turntable, the dedicated pre-amp, and the floor-standing speakers that filled the room with rich, warm sound.

"Vinyl," Jordan stated decisively. "I want to feel it. The TV speakers can't handle the gong."

Mark grinned, a look of audiophile pride on his face. "Excellent choice, brother. Let's spin the wax."

The Group of 9 settled into their comfortable spots. Mark carefully removed the heavy, splatter-colored record from its sleeve, placing it on the platter. He cleaned it with a brush, dropped the needle, and cranked the volume on the pre-amp.

The basement filled with the warm, crackling silence of the lead-in groove, and then... BOOM.

The sound was massive. The high-fidelity system captured the depth of the stadium drums and the roar of the 60,000 fans in a way the TV never could. It wasn't just noise; it was a physical presence in the room.

They listened to the entire concert, singing along, eating pizza, and ignoring the football stats rolling in on their phones.

When the final side of the third LP began—the encore—the room went quiet. The piano motif of "Electric Color" floated out of the speakers, crystal clear and heartbreakingly beautiful.

Jordan closed his eyes. He wasn't watching a screen; he was back in his chair in the front row. He could feel Aubrie's hand in his left, Jessica's in his right. He could feel the vibration of the bass in his chest.

When the final gong hit rang out, decaying slowly into the silence of the room, Jordan opened his eyes. He looked at his family, bathed in the warm light of their sanctuary.

"That," he whispered, staring at the spinning record, "is what victory sounds like."

He didn't need to see the game. He had already won.
