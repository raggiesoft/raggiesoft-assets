# Book 1: The Eleventh Child, Off to College

## Chapter 1: The Mix-Up

### Part 1: The Administrative Prelude

The journey began on a Wednesday in late August. The seven-hour drive from the flat, humid landscape of Hampton Roads to the rolling hills of the New River Valley was a tense affair. Aubrey sat silently in the passenger seat, watching the world change outside his window, while his mother, Aubrie Sinclair, filled the car with a steady stream of anxious instructions.

They checked into a hotel in Christiansburg, one of the several clustered around the busy I-81 interchange at exit 118C. When they opened the door to their room, there was only one queen-sized bed.

"Oh, dear," his mother said with a sigh that held no real disappointment. "I asked for two beds, but this must have been all they had available with the students coming back to town. Well," she said with a shrug that made it clear it wasn't a question, "I guess we'll just have to make do for a couple of nights."

Aubrey lay stiffly on his side of the bed that night, his mother's breathing a familiar, oppressive presence beside him. He stared into the darkness of the unfamiliar room, counting the hours until Friday, when he would finally have his own bed in his own room. “Aubrey dear, come comfort your mother,” Aubrie said, pulling him close. He felt smothered but was used to it by now. He hated it but put up with it. What could he do anyways? She is his mother and is all he has ever known.

Thursday was spent on the bustling campus of Commonwealth Polytechnic Institute. Their first stop was to get his student ID, a plastic card with the name AUBREY JORDAN FULLER printed starkly beneath his nervous face.

Their next and final stop was the Office of Educational Accessibility, to verify that all his accommodations—both academic and residential—were in place.

"Hello," Aubrie Sinclair said to the counselor. "We're just here to verify that all of my son Aubrey's accommodations are in order for the semester."

The counselor smiled kindly, but addressed her questions directly to the student. "Yes, Aubrey, I have your file right here. We have you down for extra time on exams and access to a university-provided note-taker. Does that all sound correct to you?"

"Yes," Aubrey said, his voice quiet. "That's correct."

"Excellent," the counselor continued, looking at her screen. "And I also see that our office coordinated with Housing to get you an early move-in accommodation due to your disability. You are all set for tomorrow, Friday, at 9:00 AM. You'll be in Bennett Hall, Room 214, Bed A."

With all the administrative tasks verified, they returned to the hotel for one final night in the shared bed. Tomorrow, he would move in. Tomorrow, his new life would begin.

### Part 2: Getting Settled

After his mother, Aubrie, left with a final wave and a promise to call that evening, Aubrey Jordan Fuller found himself truly alone for the first time in his new dorm room. The quiet was a welcome change from the earlier chaos of moving in. He took a long, steadying breath and began to take a proper inventory of the small space—his space.

The room was tiny, clearly designed for two people but now cramped to fit three due to the university's housing crisis. He flipped the main switch, and a large, glaring fluorescent light in the ceiling flickered on, bathing the room in a harsh, sterile glow.

He looked back at the heavy door he'd just entered. From the hallway, he'd tapped his new CPI Poly Pass on the reader, and the door had swung open for him automatically. On the inside, he saw the corresponding silver handicap push button next to the frame.

To his immediate left was a closed door, which he knew led to the shared toilet room. He wheeled over and opened it, confirming what he'd seen on the housing tour: a small, functional room containing only a toilet, but fitted with the necessary ADA-compliant grab bars. He saw the second door on the opposite wall, the one that connected to Room 216.

Back in the main room, he noted the sink and mirror area next to the toilet door. He rolled his wheelchair closer, a small smile touching his lips as he saw the open space underneath. It was a roll-under sink, intentionally designed without a cabinet so he could get close to the basin.

He turned his attention to the rest of the room. A single window was set in the center of the far wall.

- To the far left was a standalone, single Twin XL bed—Bed A, his assigned spot.

- To the far right, in the opposite corner, was a metal bunk bed, also with Twin XL mattresses.

- Both bed setups had their foot facing him, and both were just bare mattresses waiting for the pillows and blankets he'd brought.

He counted the furniture. Two desks, two dressers, and two nightstands. This confirmed the room's original two-person design. His future roommates, the ones assigned to the bunk beds, would have to share a desk and dresser. This was it. College.

He decided to set up his most important tool first: his laptop. He wheeled himself over to his desk, placed the laptop on it, and opened the lid. The battery icon blinked red. He sighed and looked around for an outlet. He spotted one on the wall, but its standard height from the floor was just awkwardly out of comfortable reach from his wheelchair.

He maneuvered his chair closer, stretching to try and plug in the bulky adapter. He could just barely touch the outlet, but he couldn't get the leverage to push the prongs in securely. He grunted with frustration, a small but significant reminder that even in an "accessible" room, the world wasn't always designed with him in mind.

"Hey there, need a hand?"

A friendly voice came from his open doorway. A young woman with a lanyard and an "RA" name tag stood there, her expression kind. "I'm Isabel, one of the RAs for one of the other floors. Just doing my rounds, helping the RA of this floor."

Aubrey felt a flush of embarrassment at being caught struggling. "Uh, yeah. Hi. I'm Aubrey. Just trying to plug this in," he said, gesturing to the power cord.

Isabel saw the problem instantly. "Oh yeah, that's a terrible spot for an outlet. No problem at all. Let's make this easier." He handed her the power strip that he pulled out of his backpack. She plugged the power strip into the wall outlet and then neatly ran the cord up to his desk, securing the strip on top with a piece of reusable adhesive she produced from her pocket. "There," she said with a smile. "Now you can plug anything you want in right from your desk."

"Wow. Thanks. Thank you," Aubrey said, genuinely grateful.

"Don't mention it. Do you need a hand with anything else? Getting your bed set up?"

Aubrey hesitated, then nodded. "Actually, yeah. The pillows and blankets are kind of a pain."

For the next ten minutes, Isabel helped him make his bed, placing his pillows and blankets exactly how he liked them. She even helped him unpack his main suitcase, putting his clothes away in the dresser drawers. She was efficient, friendly, and didn't make him feel like a burden.

"There you go," she said finally. "All set." She paused at the door. "Seriously, Aubrey, if you need any help with anything, you just find me or any of the other RAs, or the hall director. That's what we're here for."

"I will. Thank you, Isabel," he said, "Do you know who my two roommates are? Or when they will arrive?"

Isabel gave him an apologetic look. "I'm sorry, I don't have that information," she explained. "Since I'm not the regular RA for this floor, I don't have access to the detailed room roster with names and arrival times. I'm just helping out with early check-ins today."

She then offered a reassuring smile. "But I do know that the main move-in day starts tomorrow morning. So, you can expect them to start arriving tomorrow at the soonest. Looks like you've got the whole suite to yourself for tonight!"

Aubrey nodded, a wave of both relief and nervousness washing over him. One night of peace before meeting the two strangers who would become his new roommates.

She gave him a final wave and left. Aubrey looked around his room, which now felt much more functional and comfortable. He easily plugged his laptop into the power strip on his desk and watched it spring to life. He spent the rest of the day in his room alone, content in his new space. He had snacks and sandwiches his mom had packed in his bag, and he didn't want to venture outside anyway. The clouds that had been gathering when he arrived had finally opened up, and a steady, pouring rain now washed over the campus, making his quiet, solitary room feel like the coziest place in the world.

As evening fell, the rain continued its soothing rhythm against the window. Aubrey finished the last of his sandwiches and felt a wave of exhaustion from the emotional and physical toll of the day. It was time for bed. He wheeled himself over to his dresser where Isabel had so neatly placed his clothes and pulled out a soft t-shirt and a pair of pajama pants. He looked at them, then down at the jeans and layered shirts he was still wearing.

A familiar knot of anxiety tightened in his stomach. At home, his mother would help him. Her assistance was a routine part of his day, a seamless act of care. Here, alone, the simple act of changing clothes felt like an insurmountable mountain. He thought about what Isabel had said: *if you need any help with anything*. But the thought of asking a complete stranger for help with something so personal filled him with a profound sense of mortification.

He sat there for a long time, weighing the physical discomfort of sleeping in his day clothes against the social agony of asking for help. The denim of his jeans felt restrictive, and he knew he would be hot and twisted in them by morning. With a sigh of resignation, he put the pajamas back in the drawer.

He maneuvered himself from his wheelchair onto his bed, a practiced but still strenuous motion. At least he could get his shoes off. He hooked the heel of one sneaker on the toe of the other and pushed until it came loose, dropping to the floor with a soft thud. He did the same with the second shoe. It was a small measure of comfort, but it came with a sharp cost. He stared at the shoes on the floor. He wouldn't be able to put them back on. He couldn't reach them from his chair, and even if he could, he didn't know how to tie the laces. His mother had never taught him. She had never prepared him for any of this.

Her parting words echoed in his mind, dripping with a pride that now felt corrosive. *"I'm so proud of you, a real college kid now! An adult, living on your own."*

*Living on my own,* he thought with a bitter pang of clarity. She had said it with a beaming smile, but what did it really mean? She had dropped him in a dorm room hours from home without ever teaching him how to dress himself, cook a simple meal, or navigate the hundred small tasks she had always just... done. The honest, painful truth began to settle in his chest: maybe she wasn't proud of his independence. Maybe she was just glad to be rid of the burden. The thought was a cold stone in his gut. All her talk of him being an adult felt like a convenient excuse to finally be free of him.

Lying on top of the covers, still in his constricting day clothes, shoeless and stranded, he felt a profound loneliness he had never known. It wasn't just the physical discomfort; it was the chilling realization that he had been left behind. He closed his eyes, listening to the rain, and eventually drifted into a restless sleep, already dreading the stiff, unprepared morning that awaited him.

### Part 3: An Unlikely Welcome

Aubrey woke up stiff and sore, the restrictive denim of his jeans clinging to him. He’d tossed and turned all night, his sleep shallow and plagued by a recurring sense of falling. He felt a dull ache in his back and a profound exhaustion that had nothing to do with the physical effort of moving in. He was an adult on his own, just as his mother had said with that bright, brittle smile. And he was already failing.

The morning quiet was shattered by the official start of freshman move-in day. The hallway outside his door became a cacophony of shouting, laughing, and the rolling of carts. The noise was a persistent, grating hum that vibrated through the floor, putting him on edge.

He tried to focus on arranging his desk, but the anxiety from the night before, the raw feeling of being a burden, mingled with the sensory assault. After struggling for nearly an hour just to get his books stacked, the exhaustion from his sleepless night and the sensory overload became too much. "I just need to rest," he muttered, his head pounding. He transferred from his wheelchair to his bed—Bed A—and, still fully clothed, pulled the blanket over himself, desperate for just a few minutes of quiet before his roommates arrived. The steady, grating hum from the hallway eventually lulled him into a fitful, uneasy sleep.

Around mid-morning, his door swung open. A young woman with her arms full of bags stopped short. She saw the room, clearly already occupied. And then she saw him: a boy, fast asleep in the single bed.

"Oh! I am so sorry," she said, her eyes wide with confusion. "I must have the wrong room."

Her voice cut through his sleep. Aubrey jolted awake, his heart hammering. He slowly sat up, disoriented, and saw a girl standing in his room. His stomach clenched. He was stiff, uncomfortable, and felt a flush of shame.

"Uh... this is room 214, right?" he asked, his voice hesitant.

"Yeah, but..." She glanced at the name card on the door. "My room is 214. I'm Aubrie Jean Kelly."

Aubey’s own confused gaze followed hers to the door card. He saw her name, and his own right below it: Aubrey Jordan Fuller. He also saw another name: Madison Elizabeth Scott. The names hit him with the force of a physical blow. Females. His roommates were females.

The overwhelming sensory noise from the hallway, the social pressure of a stranger of the opposite gender, and the sudden, terrifying certainty that he was the one in the wrong room... it all converged. In a blind panic, he threw the blanket off and clumsily transferred himself from the bed into his wheelchair. He had to get out. He had to pack. He had to fix this. But the panic was too fast. His control shattered. The world became too loud, too bright, too much. A low, distressed hum started in his throat, and he began to rock back and forth in his wheelchair, his hands flying up to cover his ears. He squeezed his eyes shut, overwhelmed by a complete system failure.

The girl, Aubrie Jean, saw his reaction, and her own expression shifted from confusion to a deep, immediate understanding. She was also autistic, and she recognized the signs of a meltdown instantly.

"It's okay," she said to her parents, who were standing worriedly in the hall behind her. "He's not angry. He's overwhelmed."

She put her things down and took a slow, deliberate step into the room. "Aubrey," she said, her voice quiet and even. "My name is Aubrie. It's okay. You're safe. Focus on my voice".

He continued to rock. "Is it okay if I put my hand on your shoulder?" she asked gently. Slowly, Aubrey gave a short, sharp nod. Aubrie Jean placed her hand firmly on his shoulder. Aubrey’s shoulders shuddered, and he gave another, almost desperate nod, a silent plea for more. Understanding washed over Aubrie’s face. She moved behind his wheelchair and wrapped her arms around his shoulders in a firm, secure hug.

This was the first time in his life he had ever received an unconditional hug.

It was at that exact moment that the door opened again. A second young woman, Madison Scott, entered hauling a large box. She froze, taking in the scene. "Uh... is this room 214?"

Aubrie didn’t break her focus. "It is," she said quietly. "He's really overwhelmed. Could you maybe close the door?"

Madison immediately pushed the door closed, dulling the hallway noise. She looked at Aubrey, at his wrinkled clothes and the sheer distress on his face, and her heart went out to him. "What can I do?" she whispered compassionately.

"Just... be calm," Aubrie replied. "It'll pass. We just have to ride it out".

Madison nodded again, pulling up a desk chair and sitting a few feet away, simply offering a quiet, non-judgmental presence. Over the next hour, as Aubrey’s meltdown slowly subsided into shuddering breaths and exhaustion, the chaotic move-in continued. A girl with vibrant purple hair, Zoe Brooks, poked her head in from the connecting bathroom door, her eyes wide. "Uh, hi. I'm in 216. Is everything okay? There's a... guy in here. Is he helping you two with move in?"

Before Aubrie could explain, two more girls, Hannah Parker and Lauren Barnes, appeared behind Zoe, their expressions ranging from curious to concerned.

Zoe, ever the pragmatist, took one look at the three names on the door card for Room 214 and the exhausted frightened boy in the wheelchair, and her face set with determination. "Okay, something's definitely wrong here. I'm going to get the RA".

A few minutes later, she returned with a harried-looking young man. "I'm Henry, the RA for this floor," he announced, then stopped, his eyes darting between the six students crammed into the small room. He looked at the door card, then at Aubrey. "Aubrey Fuller?" he asked. Aubrey, drained but calm, nodded. A look of dawning horror crossed Henry's face. "Oh no. I think... I think Housing made a mistake. 'Aubrey' is usually a girl's name. I... I have to get the Hall Director".

Henry hurried away, leaving the six of them in an awkward silence. Soon, he returned with a woman in her late twenties who introduced herself as Kelsey, the Hall Director. Her expression was weary but kind.

"Okay, folks," Kelsey began, sighing heavily. "It appears we have a significant clerical error from the Housing Office. Aubrey, they assigned you here assuming you were female".

She then laid out the grim reality of the situation. "To be blunt, the university has a massive housing crisis this year. We've overbooked every dorm. That's why your rooms are 'triples' designed for two people. On top of that, we're already putting students up in the hotel next to campus, and when that filled up, we started using another hotel down the road in Christiansburg, which is a twenty-to-thirty-minute bus ride away". Aubrey glanced up when Kelsey said that. He remembered the signs at the largest of the hotels that was next to the one he stayed at with his mother: something about overflow student housing for the university.

She looked directly at the five young women. "Here's the situation. Aubrey is staying. This is the designated handicap-accessible suite on the floor, and we have no other place for him. For the rest of you, I can start the process of finding you a new room assignment, but I cannot guarantee it won't be in one of those hotels. And I can't guarantee you'd all stay together".

The six strangers looked at each other. The idea of being shipped off to a hotel, separated from the main campus, was deeply unappealing.

Lauren was the first to speak. "So, we can either stay here, with... Aubrey," she said, giving him a small, reassuring smile, "or get shipped off to a hotel thirty minutes away?".

Kelsey nodded. "That's the choice, at least for the next two weeks. The official roommate change window opens then. You could try to move then, but again, no guarantees".

The five women huddled together, whispering. Aubrey watched them, his anxiety returning. After a moment, Aubrie turned to him, including him in their circle. "What do you think?" she asked. "Are you okay with this?".

Aubrey, who had spent most of his life on the outside looking in, was taken aback by the simple act of being asked. "I... I don't want to cause a problem," he stammered.

"It's not your fault," Madison said firmly. "It's the university's mess".

After a minute of quiet consensus, Zoe looked at Kelsey. "We'll tough it out," she declared. "For two weeks, at least. We'll stay".

A wave of relief washed over Kelsey's face. "Thank you," she said, genuinely grateful. "Truly. Let us know if you need anything." She and a relieved-looking Henry took their leave.

In the sudden, heavy quiet, the six of them looked at each other, the reality of their situation sinking in. It was Madison who finally broke the silence, her curiosity getting the better of her.

"Okay, I have to ask," she said, looking at Aubrey with a friendly, puzzled expression. "No offense, but... how did you not notice our names on the door when you moved in yesterday? You must have seen 'Aubrie Kelly' and 'Madison Scott' and realized something was up".

The other girls nodded, clearly wondering the same thing.

Aubrey flushed slightly, shaking his head. "There weren't any names on the door when I got here yesterday," he explained quietly. "My mom and I just had the room number. After she left, I just... stayed in here. I had food she packed for me, and it was raining, so I didn't leave". He looked around at them. "The first time the door opened again after my mom left was this morning, when Aubrie," he gestured to Aubrie Jean, "walked in. That was the first I knew about any of this. They just checked my name off their list and told me I was assigned to Bed A in Room 214. My Student ID unlocked the door to the building entrance, then it unlocked the door to the hallway after they told me my room number, then it unlocked the door to the room, so I didn’t know anything was amiss yet".

"Ohhh," Lauren said, a wave of understanding passing through the group. "So the RAs must have put the name cards up sometime yesterday afternoon after you were already settled in".

"That makes sense," Hannah agreed. The small mystery was solved, another piece of their strange first day clicking into place.

"Well," Aubrie said, breaking the silence with a wry smile. "This is... cozy".

A nervous laugh rippled through the group. The tension broken, Aubrie turned to him. "So, Aubrey and Aubrie, huh? My mom and dad liked the name so much they gave it to me". She stifled a giggle.

He managed a small, pained smile. "My mom's name is Aubrie, too. Different spelling. She said she put 'Aubrey' on my birth certificate as a joke that just kind of... stuck". He looked at Aubrie Jean, then at Madison, and a new, quiet resolve settled in his chest. This was his chance. This whole embarrassing, terrifying situation had been caused by that name. A name he hated, a name that was *hers*. He took a breath, the decision his first, small act of defiance against a lifetime of his mother's control. "But... you can call me Jordan. It's my middle name. I think I'll go by Jordan here".

"Jordan," Aubrie repeated, nodding. "I like it. Hey, what's your major?"

Jordan hesitated, his stomach tightening. He looked down, the name of the major tasting like a trap. "Astrophysics," he replied, his voice barely a whisper. He braced himself, expecting her to look confused or, worse, to feel pity for him.

Instead, her eyes lit up. "No way! Me too!".

His head snapped up. He stared at her, his mind reeling. *What?* This insurmountable mountain his mother had picked out for him, this complex, impossible degree she had chosen just to prove he would fail—this girl, this kind, safe girl, had *chosen* it?

For the first time, a fragile, terrifying hope sparked in his chest. Maybe he wasn't set up to fail. Maybe he would have someone to climb the mountain with him.

"Are you..." he asked, his voice suddenly earnest, "Are you in Astronomy 101 with Dr. Alistair?"

"And the Friday lab section!" she confirmed, her own excitement bubbling over.

"Me too!" he said, the words coming out in a rush of pure relief. A genuine spark of excitement lit his voice for the first time.

"We should sit together, if there aren't assigned seats," she beamed.

Jordan nodded, a slow, watery smile spreading across his face. His mother had pushed him off a cliff. And this girl, this *Aubrie* who was the antidote to his *Aubrie*, had just shown up at the bottom with a safety net and a climbing rope. For the first time all day, for the first time in his life, he felt a sense of belonging. He saw a small, actual light at the end of the tunnel.

### Part 4: Making It Official

It was Madison who brought up the topic, her tone gentle and curious. "So, Jordan," she began, "back there when you were talking to Aubrie, you said you wanted to go by Jordan. Is that... like, for real? Is that what you want all of us to call you?"

Jordan looked at Aubrie, who gave him a small, encouraging smile. He felt a surge of confidence. "Yeah," he said, his voice firmer than it had been all day. "It is. I've always hated 'Aubrey.' I want to be Jordan here." He glanced at the door to their room. "I just wish the card on the door didn't..."

"We can ask Henry to change it," Aubrie said immediately, her support unwavering. "I bet he can print a new one. Let's go see him right now."

A few minutes later, Jordan, Aubrie, and Madison were sitting in the small, functional Resident Advisor office down the hall. Henry sat at his desk, and he motioned for the three of them to take the chairs opposite him. As Jordan wheeled up next to Aubrie, he nervously reached out and took her hand under the table. She responded instantly, her fingers lacing through his, holding his hand firmly in her lap. It was the first time they had held hands in front of anyone else, a quiet, public declaration of their budding connection.

"Hi, Henry," Jordan began, feeling braver with Aubrie's hand in his. "Um... so the name on my door card is 'Aubrey Jordan Fuller.' But I go by my middle name, Jordan. I was wondering if it would be possible to get a new card that just says 'Jordan Fuller'?"

Henry smiled warmly. "Yeah, absolutely, man. Not a problem at all. I can print you a new door card right now." He started typing. "But we should do one better," he continued. "The door card is just for the hallway. You'll want all your professors and the university staff to know. Let me show you something."

He turned his monitor toward them and guided Jordan through the CPI student portal to the "Personal Information" section. He pointed to a field. "Right here. It says 'Preferred Name.' If you type 'Jordan' into this field, that's the name that will show up on all the official class rosters for your instructors. It'll also be the name that shows up for advisors, the library, everywhere."

He then leaned forward, offering some practical advice. "Now, the system can take a day or so to update, and some professors print their rosters on day one and never look again. The best thing you can do is send a quick, polite email to each of your instructors tonight, before classes start tomorrow. Just introduce yourself and let them know you go by Jordan."

"Wow," Jordan said, feeling a sense of empowerment he'd never known. "Okay. Thank you, Henry. So much."

"No problem at all," Henry said, handing him the freshly printed door card that read, simply, Jordan Fuller.

They left the office and headed back to their suite, Jordan and Aubrie still holding hands. He had a mission for the evening: to email his professors and formally introduce himself to the world, for the very first time, as Jordan.

"I'll walk back with you guys and swap that card out right now," Henry offered, grabbing the small, freshly printed name card from his desk.

The three of them walked with him down the short hall back to Room 214, where the other suitemates were now talking quietly in the adjoining room. Henry used a small tool to pop open the clear plastic holder on the door frame. He slid out the old card, with the typed name Aubrey Jordan Fuller, and handed it to Jordan as a small souvenir. Then, he slid in the new one.

The effect was immediate and powerful. There, on the door to his new home, were the words: Jordan Fuller.

Jordan stared at it, a deep sense of satisfaction washing over him. It was real.

"Now, just so you know," Henry said, tapping his own plastic CPI ID card that hung from a lanyard around his neck. "This doesn't change your official university ID. I can change the door card because it's just for display, but the main CPI ID has to match the legal name from your birth certificate for security and official records."

He continued, wanting to be clear. "So your ID card will still say 'Aubrey Jordan Fuller' next to your photo and student number. But don't worry. Because you updated your preferred name in the portal, whenever you swipe your card—at the dining hall, the library, anywhere—the name that will pop up on the staff's screen will be 'Jordan.' It's just the physical card itself that can't be changed without a legal court order."

Jordan nodded, understanding. He touched the wallet in his pocket where his own new ID was sitting. A small, new flicker of dissatisfaction was there, the knowledge that "Aubrey" would still follow him around on that piece of plastic. But for now, this was a huge victory.

"Thank you, Henry," Aubrie said sincerely. "For everything."

"No problem at all," Henry said with a final, warm smile. "Enjoy the rest of your night. Welcome to Bennett Hall, Jordan." He gave a small wave and headed back down the hall to his office.

Jordan, Aubrie, and Madison stood there for a moment longer, just looking at the door. The first official piece of his new life was finally in place.

### Part 5: Logistics of a Co-Ed Suite

The practicalities of their arrangement soon came to the forefront. It was Lauren who brought it up. "Okay, so... logistics," she said, gesturing between the two rooms and the single connecting toilet. "Six people, one toilet, and no privacy. Jordan, you were expecting guy roommates, and we were expecting another girl. How do we make this not... super awkward?"

There was a beat of silence before Madison shrugged, a pragmatic look on her face. "Look, we're all adults. We have one toilet. The doors connect. There's no room to be shy. I say we just agree that what happens in the suite, stays in the suite. No need to hide, no need to turn away. There's literally no room for privacy, so let's not pretend there is."

A surprising murmur of agreement went through the group. It was a radical solution born of necessity, but it felt right.

As evening approached, the five women decided to head to the dining hall for their first official college meal. They were gathering their student IDs when Zoe noticed Jordan sitting quietly by his desk. He looked small and isolated, and a wave of collective understanding passed between the girls. They recognized the look of someone who was used to being left out.

"Hey, Jordan," Lauren said kindly. "We're all going to dinner. You should come with us."

Jordan looked up, surprised. "Oh. You don't have to..."

"We want to," Aubrie insisted, already moving to position herself behind his wheelchair. "You're our suitemate. Our family, for the next two weeks at least. We stick together."

A genuine, wide smile spread across Jordan's face. As the six of them trooped down the hall, with Aubrie pushing his chair, they were a strange and motley crew. In the bustling cafeteria, they found a large table and sat together, sharing stories about their hometowns and their anxieties about starting college. For the first time, Jordan didn't feel like an outsider; he felt like a friend.

Back in the suite, the reality of their shared life continued. As everyone started changing into pajamas, Lauren noticed Jordan’s hesitation. "Everything okay, Jordan?"

He flushed, looking down at his hands. "I... um... I can't... My mom usually helps me." The admission was quiet, laced with shame.

"Hey, not a problem," Lauren said, her tone gentle and completely free of judgment. "You've been in those all day? You must be so uncomfortable. C'mon."

She helped him into his room and, with a kindness that almost brought tears to his eyes, assisted him out of his stiff jeans and into his soft pajama pants. "Okay, shoes," she said, spotting them on the floor. She picked them up, expertly untied the knotted laces he’d kicked them off with, and set them by his desk. "I can help you with these in the morning."

Her simple, practical help felt like a lifeline. He was clean, comfortable, and cared for. When she left, he felt a sense of peace he hadn’t thought possible.

Later that night, the suite was dark and quiet, filled with the soft breathing of six sleeping students. But Jordan’s sleep was not peaceful. It was his room, Room 214, but it was vast, cold, and unnervingly quiet. The friendly clutter of his new life was gone. He saw his five suitemates—Aubrie, Madison, Zoe, Lauren, and Hannah—standing in the far corners of the room, their backs to him, their shoulders hunched together as they whispered.

He tried to call out to them, but his voice made no sound. He wheeled himself forward, but the floor felt like thick mud, each push of his wheels an exhausting effort. As he got closer, their whispers grew, and he could finally make out their words.

"He can't even get dressed by himself," Lauren said, her voice cold and clinical.

Madison sighed, a sound of pure exhaustion. "Such a burden. We always have to help him."

## Chapter 6: Alliances and Additions

## Chapter 7: Trial by Fire Drill

The shock and confusion sent a jolt of fear through him, causing him to freeze completely. "No," he muttered, shaking his head. "No, not here. We don't do this here."

Moving as a tight, protective unit, they navigated the chaotic hallway filled with other grumbling freshmen and exited onto the cool, quiet hillside. Away from the noise, Aubrie and Jordan's shoulders began to relax. They stood huddled together, a single family that had, just days ago, made their bond official. ## Chapter 9: The Helicopter and the Chosen Family
