# Part 1: The Administrative Prelude

The journey began on a Wednesday in late August. The seven-hour drive from the flat, humid landscape of Hampton Roads to the rolling hills of the New River Valley was a tense affair. Aubrey sat silently in the passenger seat, watching the world change outside his window, while his mother, Aubrie Sinclair, filled the car with a steady stream of anxious instructions.

They checked into a hotel in Christiansburg, one of the several clustered around the busy I-81 interchange at exit 118C. When they opened the door to their room, there was only one queen-sized bed.

"Oh, dear," his mother said with a sigh that held no real disappointment. "I asked for two beds, but this must have been all they had available with the students coming back to town. Well," she said with a shrug that made it clear it wasn't a question, "I guess we'll just have to make do for a couple of nights."

Aubrey lay stiffly on his side of the bed that night, his mother's breathing a familiar, oppressive presence beside him. He stared into the darkness of the unfamiliar room, counting the hours until Friday, when he would finally have his own bed in his own room. “Aubrey dear, come comfort your mother,” Aubrie said, pulling him close. He felt smothered but was used to it by now. He hated it but put up with it. What could he do anyways? She is his mother and is all he has ever known.

Thursday was spent on the bustling campus of Commonwealth Polytechnic Institute. Their first stop was to get his student ID, a plastic card with the name AUBREY JORDAN FULLER printed starkly beneath his nervous face.

Their next and final stop was the Office of Educational Accessibility, to verify that all his accommodations—both academic and residential—were in place.

"Hello," Aubrie Sinclair said to the counselor. "We're just here to verify that all of my son Aubrey's accommodations are in order for the semester."

The counselor smiled kindly, but addressed her questions directly to the student. "Yes, Aubrey, I have your file right here. We have you down for extra time on exams and access to a university-provided note-taker. Does that all sound correct to you?"

"Yes," Aubrey said, his voice quiet. "That's correct."

"Excellent," the counselor continued, looking at her screen. "And I also see that our office coordinated with Housing to get you an early move-in accommodation due to your disability. You are all set for tomorrow, Friday, at 9:00 AM. You'll be in Bennett Hall, Room 214, Bed A."

With all the administrative tasks verified, they returned to the hotel for one final night in the shared bed. Tomorrow, he would move in. Tomorrow, his new life would begin.
