# Part 2: Getting Settled

After his mother, Aubrie, left with a final wave and a promise to call that evening, Aubrey Jordan Fuller found himself truly alone for the first time in his new dorm room. The quiet was a welcome change from the earlier chaos of moving in. He took a long, steadying breath and began to take a proper inventory of the small space—his space.

The room was tiny, clearly designed for two people but now cramped to fit three due to the university's housing crisis. He flipped the main switch, and a large, glaring fluorescent light in the ceiling flickered on, bathing the room in a harsh, sterile glow.

He looked back at the heavy door he'd just entered. From the hallway, he'd tapped his new CPI Poly Pass on the reader, and the door had swung open for him automatically. On the inside, he saw the corresponding silver handicap push button next to the frame.

To his immediate left was a closed door, which he knew led to the shared toilet room. He wheeled over and opened it, confirming what he'd seen on the housing tour: a small, functional room containing only a toilet, but fitted with the necessary ADA-compliant grab bars. He saw the second door on the opposite wall, the one that connected to Room 216.

Back in the main room, he noted the sink and mirror area next to the toilet door. He rolled his wheelchair closer, a small smile touching his lips as he saw the open space underneath. It was a roll-under sink, intentionally designed without a cabinet so he could get close to the basin.

He turned his attention to the rest of the room. A single window was set in the center of the far wall.

- To the far left was a standalone, single Twin XL bed—Bed A, his assigned spot.

- To the far right, in the opposite corner, was a metal bunk bed, also with Twin XL mattresses.

- Both bed setups had their foot facing him, and both were just bare mattresses waiting for the pillows and blankets he'd brought.

He counted the furniture. Two desks, two dressers, and two nightstands. This confirmed the room's original two-person design. His future roommates, the ones assigned to the bunk beds, would have to share a desk and dresser. This was it. College.

He decided to set up his most important tool first: his laptop. He wheeled himself over to his desk, placed the laptop on it, and opened the lid. The battery icon blinked red. He sighed and looked around for an outlet. He spotted one on the wall, but its standard height from the floor was just awkwardly out of comfortable reach from his wheelchair.

He maneuvered his chair closer, stretching to try and plug in the bulky adapter. He could just barely touch the outlet, but he couldn't get the leverage to push the prongs in securely. He grunted with frustration, a small but significant reminder that even in an "accessible" room, the world wasn't always designed with him in mind.

"Hey there, need a hand?"

A friendly voice came from his open doorway. A young woman with a lanyard and an "RA" name tag stood there, her expression kind. "I'm Isabel, one of the RAs for one of the other floors. Just doing my rounds, helping the RA of this floor."

Aubrey felt a flush of embarrassment at being caught struggling. "Uh, yeah. Hi. I'm Aubrey. Just trying to plug this in," he said, gesturing to the power cord.

Isabel saw the problem instantly. "Oh yeah, that's a terrible spot for an outlet. No problem at all. Let's make this easier." He handed her the power strip that he pulled out of his backpack. She plugged the power strip into the wall outlet and then neatly ran the cord up to his desk, securing the strip on top with a piece of reusable adhesive she produced from her pocket. "There," she said with a smile. "Now you can plug anything you want in right from your desk."

"Wow. Thanks. Thank you," Aubrey said, genuinely grateful.

"Don't mention it. Do you need a hand with anything else? Getting your bed set up?"

Aubrey hesitated, then nodded. "Actually, yeah. The pillows and blankets are kind of a pain."

For the next ten minutes, Isabel helped him make his bed, placing his pillows and blankets exactly how he liked them. She even helped him unpack his main suitcase, putting his clothes away in the dresser drawers. She was efficient, friendly, and didn't make him feel like a burden.

"There you go," she said finally. "All set." She paused at the door. "Seriously, Aubrey, if you need any help with anything, you just find me or any of the other RAs, or the hall director. That's what we're here for."

"I will. Thank you, Isabel," he said, "Do you know who my two roommates are? Or when they will arrive?"

Isabel gave him an apologetic look. "I'm sorry, I don't have that information," she explained. "Since I'm not the regular RA for this floor, I don't have access to the detailed room roster with names and arrival times. I'm just helping out with early check-ins today."

She then offered a reassuring smile. "But I do know that the main move-in day starts tomorrow morning. So, you can expect them to start arriving tomorrow at the soonest. Looks like you've got the whole suite to yourself for tonight!"

Aubrey nodded, a wave of both relief and nervousness washing over him. One night of peace before meeting the two strangers who would become his new roommates.

She gave him a final wave and left. Aubrey looked around his room, which now felt much more functional and comfortable. He easily plugged his laptop into the power strip on his desk and watched it spring to life. He spent the rest of the day in his room alone, content in his new space. He had snacks and sandwiches his mom had packed in his bag, and he didn't want to venture outside anyway. The clouds that had been gathering when he arrived had finally opened up, and a steady, pouring rain now washed over the campus, making his quiet, solitary room feel like the coziest place in the world.

As evening fell, the rain continued its soothing rhythm against the window. Aubrey finished the last of his sandwiches and felt a wave of exhaustion from the emotional and physical toll of the day. It was time for bed. He wheeled himself over to his dresser where Isabel had so neatly placed his clothes and pulled out a soft t-shirt and a pair of pajama pants. He looked at them, then down at the jeans and layered shirts he was still wearing.

A familiar knot of anxiety tightened in his stomach. At home, his mother would help him. Her assistance was a routine part of his day, a seamless act of care. Here, alone, the simple act of changing clothes felt like an insurmountable mountain. He thought about what Isabel had said: *if you need any help with anything*. But the thought of asking a complete stranger for help with something so personal filled him with a profound sense of mortification.

He sat there for a long time, weighing the physical discomfort of sleeping in his day clothes against the social agony of asking for help. The denim of his jeans felt restrictive, and he knew he would be hot and twisted in them by morning. With a sigh of resignation, he put the pajamas back in the drawer.

He maneuvered himself from his wheelchair onto his bed, a practiced but still strenuous motion. At least he could get his shoes off. He hooked the heel of one sneaker on the toe of the other and pushed until it came loose, dropping to the floor with a soft thud. He did the same with the second shoe. It was a small measure of comfort, but it came with a sharp cost. He stared at the shoes on the floor. He wouldn't be able to put them back on. He couldn't reach them from his chair, and even if he could, he didn't know how to tie the laces. His mother had never taught him. She had never prepared him for any of this.

Her parting words echoed in his mind, dripping with a pride that now felt corrosive. *"I'm so proud of you, a real college kid now! An adult, living on your own."*

*Living on my own,* he thought with a bitter pang of clarity. She had said it with a beaming smile, but what did it really mean? She had dropped him in a dorm room hours from home without ever teaching him how to dress himself, cook a simple meal, or navigate the hundred small tasks she had always just... done. The honest, painful truth began to settle in his chest: maybe she wasn't proud of his independence. Maybe she was just glad to be rid of the burden. The thought was a cold stone in his gut. All her talk of him being an adult felt like a convenient excuse to finally be free of him.

Lying on top of the covers, still in his constricting day clothes, shoeless and stranded, he felt a profound loneliness he had never known. It wasn't just the physical discomfort; it was the chilling realization that he had been left behind. He closed his eyes, listening to the rain, and eventually drifted into a restless sleep, already dreading the stiff, unprepared morning that awaited him.
