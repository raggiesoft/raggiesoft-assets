# Part 2: Fire Alarm in the Dormitory

It was 8:00 PM, during the university's much-ignored "Health and Safety Week," when the world exploded. A piercing, metallic shriek ripped through the building, accompanied by the frantic, blinding flash of emergency strobe lights.

"Oh, you have got to be kidding me," Madison groaned from her top bunk. The other girls echoed her sentiment, all of them thinking the same thing: *I thought we were done with this crap when we graduated high school.*

They all knew what a fire drill was; they knew the procedure was to evacuate. But for Jordan and Aubrie, knowing the procedure and being able to execute it in that moment were two very different things. They were caught completely off guard.

For Aubrie, the sensory assault was debilitating. She knew she was supposed to get up and leave, but the screeching alarm felt like a physical force, pinning her to her chair. She curled inward, pressing her palms to her ears to try and lessen the overwhelming pain of the sound.

For Jordan, the trigger was the sheer wrongness of the situation. This wasn't supposed to happen. Not in college. It was a violation of the unspoken rules of adulthood he was just beginning to learn. The shock and confusion sent a jolt of fear through him, causing him to freeze completely. "No," he muttered, shaking his head. "No, not here. We don't do this here."

The other four suitemates saw the distress and immediately shifted from annoyed to active. "Okay, they're overloaded," Zoe said, taking charge. "They know what to do, they just can't do it right now. We have to help."

Madison went to Aubrie, gently wrapping a blanket around her to block the strobes. "I know, it's awful," she said calmly. "We just have to get outside where it's quiet. I've got you."

Lauren and Hannah went to Jordan. "Jordan, I know. It's ridiculous," Lauren said, her voice steady and reassuring. "We didn't think they did them in college either, but apparently they do. It's just a stupid drill. It's not real. We just have to go outside."

Her words—acknowledging the absurdity he was feeling—helped break his cognitive freeze. He was still scared, but the explanation grounded him. He nodded, allowing Lauren to take the handles of his chair.

Moving as a tight, protective unit, they navigated the chaotic hallway filled with other grumbling freshmen and exited onto the cool, quiet hillside. Away from the noise, Aubrie and Jordan's shoulders began to relax. They stood huddled together, a single family that had, just days ago, made their bond official. They had been tested not by choice, but by the random shriek of an alarm, and had proven that no matter what, they would get through it together.
