# Part 9: Halftime

The halftime whistle blew, and the CPI Marching Forgers, one of the best bands in the nation, took the field to a spattering of polite, defeated applause. The stands were emptying as thousands of alumni and students headed for the exits, disgusted by the 24-0 rout.

For Jordan, the band was just distant, garbled background noise. He was still frozen, staring at the scoreboard. HOME: 0, VISITOR: 24.

He was clearly upset, his new confidence shattered. His mother's world, the symbolic force of his abuser, was effortlessly *destroying* his sanctuary. This humiliation felt like a confirmation of his deepest fear: he wasn't safe, his new life was a fraud, and his mother's world was still all-powerful. He was pale, his hands gripping his armrests, and he had begun to rock, a low, quiet stim of rising panic.

"J? Are you okay?" Aubrie asked, her own anger at the game vanishing as she saw his genuine distress.

Jordan just shook his head, unable to speak, his eyes wide and scared.

The Group of 8 instantly shifted from "angry fans" to "protective fortress." They didn't understand the depth of his symbolic fear, but they saw their friend was clearly upset and needed help.

"Okay, new plan," Mark said, his voice low. He, Hannah, Zoe, Lauren, and Sarah immediately stood up. They turned their backs to the field and the rest of the crowd, forming a tight, human wall around Jordan's chair to give him privacy.

Aubrie knelt in front of him, taking both his hands. "Hey," she said softly, forcing him to look at her. "It's just a stupid game. It doesn't mean anything. We're right here."

"She's right, J," Madison added, kneeling beside her. "Who cares about this? Look at us. We're here. You're safe."

Jordan looked at their faces, at the ring of his friends protecting him from the world. He was still terrified, but their presence was an anchor. He took a shaky breath and gave a small, jerky nod. The halftime show continued behind them, but the Group of 8 was in their own world, focused on the only thing that mattered: holding their fortress together.
