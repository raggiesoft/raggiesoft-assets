# Part 4: Cutting the Digital Cord

Back in the quiet of the suite after their successful planning session in the lobby, the wrestling match on the TV was long forgotten. Jordan was still sitting in front of his laptop, his mother's email still open on the screen. The words seemed to burn with a cold fire: *"Aubrey, you WILL be coming home..."*

He stared at the name she used, at the tone of absolute command. He had a plan to escape her physically, but he realized in that moment that he needed to build a digital wall as well.

"I don't want to get emails from her anymore," he said, his voice quiet but firm. "Not like this. Not ever again."

Zoe, who was sitting at her own desk nearby, swiveled her chair around. "Okay," she said, her mind immediately shifting to logistics. "We can fix that. You can't block her address from the university server, but you *can* change the address she knows about. We'll submit a help desk ticket right now."

She guided him to the CPI Information Technology support portal. Together, they found the correct form and began to fill it out.

IT Support Ticket

Request Type: Student Account Management

User: Jordan Fuller (current email: afuller001)

Subject: Request for Primary Email Address Change

Description:

"Hello, I am writing to request a change to my primary student email address. My current address, afuller001, was generated based on my legal first name, 'Aubrey,' which I do not use. My preferred name, 'Jordan,' is already on file with the registrar.

An external party is using the old 'afuller001' address to send harassing messages. To protect my privacy, I am requesting that the afuller001 alias be permanently deactivated and that a new primary email address be generated based on my preferred name, Jordan Fuller.

Thank you."

Jordan read over the text one last time, took a deep breath, and clicked "Submit." The ticket was sent. A day or two later, he would receive a notification from the Help Desk confirming his new official student email address was jfuller004, and that the old one was no longer active. It was a small, bureaucratic act, but it felt like slamming a steel door shut.

Aubrie Jean, who had been watching over his shoulder, placed a gentle hand on his. "Good," she said softly. "One less way for her to get to you."

He nodded, feeling a sense of control he hadn't known was possible. They had a physical plan for Thanksgiving, and now, they had a digital one.
