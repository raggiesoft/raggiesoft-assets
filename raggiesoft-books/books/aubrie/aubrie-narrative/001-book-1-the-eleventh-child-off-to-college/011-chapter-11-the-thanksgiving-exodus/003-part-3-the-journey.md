# Part 3: The Journey

The walk from Bennett Hall to the CPI Transit Center was a sight to behold. The eight friends moved as one organism, a bustling caravan of rolling suitcases, overstuffed duffel bags, and cheerful chatter, with Jordan's wheelchair at the center of their formation.

They arrived at the busy transit center and checked the schedule. The next local Blue Ridge Transit bus to Christiansburg was due in about ten minutes. But just as they were settling in to wait, a different vehicle pulled into an adjacent bay. It was not a typical city bus, but a large, long-distance coach bus, its digital sign cycling between MAROON EXPRESS and BLUEWATER via FALLING BRANCH. With a pneumatic hiss, the doors swung open, and a clear, automated female voice immediately began to announce its route on a loop.

"BLUEWATER TRANSIT MAROON EXPRESS: BLUEWATER via FALLING BRANCH," the voice called out.

Madison's eyes lit up. "GUYS! LOOK!" she whisper-yelled, pointing with excitement. "It's the Maroon Express! It's the *real* BT!"

Seeing the others' confusion, she quickly explained. "That's my hometown bus! It operates express via the highway. It'll get us to Falling Branch in like twenty minutes instead of the forty-five the local route takes. The trade-off is it only comes once an hour, so we just got incredibly lucky! We have to take it!"

Boarding the large coach bus was a more involved process. The driver, with a friendly and practiced air, operated the wheelchair lift located at the center door. The process was a noisy, mechanical affair, a slow, whirring platform that always drew more attention than Jordan liked, but it was a necessary pain. Once on board, he transferred to one of the comfortable coach seats, and his wheelchair was collapsed and stowed below.

Aubrie immediately slid into the seat next to him, and he gratefully leaned against her, the two of them cuddling up for the short, comfortable ride. The rest of their friends found seats nearby, taking over a whole section of the coach and filling it with their happy, excited energy.

Once they were underway and cruising down the highway, Zoe, ever the logistician, immediately got to work. She sent a quick text to her parents, letting them know their arrival time had been cut by more than half. Then, she turned to the group.

"Okay, so, for the house," she began, "My parents have cleared out the whole basement living area for us. We'll have four queen air mattresses, so we'll be pairing up. And just a heads-up, my six brothers and sisters will be around, so be prepared for some chaos."

Madison grinned. "Awesome! Okay, so Hannah and Mark are obviously one pair..."

"I hope your mom makes her sweet potato casserole!" Lauren chimed in.

"We should definitely try to see my parents in Radford one day, too," Sarah added. "And Mark, we can all come visit your family in Blacksburg. We can make a whole tour of it!"

They were so deep in their enthusiastic discussion—planning meals, assigning bed partners, and mapping out a holiday week that would connect all of their local families—that the automated voice announcing their stop took them by surprise.

"Next Stop: Falling Branch Park and Ride."

They looked at each other, grinning. Their journey had been so swift, they had barely made a dent in their planning. It was time to get off the bus. Their Thanksgiving was about to begin.

Disembarking was as smooth as boarding. They stood for a moment at the quiet, suburban bus stop, the air cooler and cleaner than it was on campus. The entrance to Zoe's neighborhood was just one block away, at the corner of U.S. 11 and Simmons Road SE. From there, it was just a few more blocks to the house on Geneva Street. As they began the final leg of their journey, walking down the quiet, tree-lined street, a single house at the end of the cul-de-sac came into view, its porch light a warm, welcoming beacon. They were almost home.
