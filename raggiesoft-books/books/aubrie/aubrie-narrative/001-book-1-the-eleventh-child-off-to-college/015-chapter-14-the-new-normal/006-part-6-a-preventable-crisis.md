# Part 6: A Preventable Crisis

The rhythm of their new normal was shattered during the first week of December, right as the pressure of finals began to mount. It started simply enough. Jordan complained of a scratchy throat and a headache. The group assumed it was just a standard head cold, brought on by late-night study sessions and the stress of the impending exams.

But by the next day, it was clear this was something more. His cold had blossomed into a full-blown, body-rattling sickness. He was running a high fever, and by that evening, he was vomiting. The group's casual concern escalated into serious alarm.

In the "Jordan Comms" chat, Lauren sent a frantic update.

Lauren: Hey guys, Jordan's fever just spiked to 102. He just got sick again. I don't think this is a normal cold.

Zoe's response was immediate and decisive.

Zoe: Okay, this is now a medical situation. I'm instituting a new protocol. From now on, any health emergency uses the tag \[MEDICAL\] so it's not missed. \[MEDICAL\] Jordan's condition has worsened. I'm calling the 24-hour Student Health nurse line for an on-site visit.

An hour later, a nurse practitioner from Student Health arrived at their suite, her expression calm and professional. She took Jordan's vitals and listened to his chest. "Well, it looks like you've got a particularly nasty case of the flu strain that's been going around," she said, frowning at her thermometer. "It's hitting you very hard. Let me just look at your medical records..."

She scrolled through his file on her tablet. Her brow furrowed slightly. "That's odd. According to your file, you have a comprehensive medical exemption for most standard immunizations, including the annual flu shot. Was there a severe allergy we should be aware of?"

Jordan stared at her, completely lost. "I... I don't know what that means," he said honestly. "An exemption? Immunizations?"

The nurse's professional demeanor softened, replaced by a look of dawning, gentle understanding. She realized she wasn't just treating an illness; she was filling a profound gap in his knowledge.

"An immunization, Jordan, or a vaccine," she began carefully, "is a shot that doctors give to children to teach their bodies how to fight off a specific disease, so they don't get sick like this later. All students are required to have them to attend CPI. An exemption is a special waiver that says a student doesn't need them, usually for a very serious medical reason, like an allergy." She looked at him with kind, searching eyes. "You have an exemption for all of them. Do you know why your mother might have filed for one?"

The pieces clicked together in Jordan's mind with sickening clarity. The mysterious forms his mother always signed at the doctor's office. The times he was sent to the library in school while other kids went to the nurse. The severity of his current illness. It wasn't bad luck. It wasn't a weak constitution. It was a deliberate choice she had made for him, and she had hidden the entire concept from him his whole life.

His confusion hardened into a cold, clear resolve. He looked the nurse directly in the eye. "No," he said, his voice raspy but firm. "There's no medical reason that I know of." He took a shaky breath. "I don't want the exemption. As soon as I'm better... I want the shots. All of them. I want to get caught up unless there is a valid reason not to."

The nurse smiled, a look of immense respect on her face. "Okay, Jordan. We can absolutely help you with that. But first, let's get you well."

The next few days were a blur of intense caregiving, with the group masking up and operating their academic ferry system. Jordan, fighting through the fever, felt a new kind of determination. This illness was the last thing his mother would ever control about him. He made a full recovery just in time for his very last final, which he took at the Testing Center, weak but triumphant, his future health now firmly in his own hands.
