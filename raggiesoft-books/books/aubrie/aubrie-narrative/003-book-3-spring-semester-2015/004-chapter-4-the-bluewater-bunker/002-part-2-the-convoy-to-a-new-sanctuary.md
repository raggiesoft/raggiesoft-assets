# Part 2: The Convoy to a New Sanctuary

The minivan was quiet as Daniel drove them south on the highway, a stark contrast to the usual cheerful chatter of the group. Jordan stared out the window, his mind a whirlwind of fear and anxiety over the impending court hearing. As they merged onto Interstate 77, a large, familiar coach bus appeared in the northbound lanes.

It was the Bluewater Transit Maroon Express.

Jordan’s heart ached as it passed. He watched its destination sign cycle through the names of his new life: FALLING BRANCH, BLACKSBURG-CPI. A lump formed in his throat. He wanted to be on that bus. So badly. He didn't care which one—the Maroon Express, the RVT coach, even the slow Blue Ridge local. He just wanted the simple freedom of swiping his ID, finding a seat, and watching the world go by with his friends. Logically, he understood why Daniel was taking no chances, why he was being spirited away to Bluewater in the safety of a minivan. He understood, but it didn't stop him from hating that she had taken another piece of his hard-won normal life from him.

An hour later, they pulled onto a quiet residential street in Bluewater. As their three-car convoy came to a stop, the front door of the Scott family home opened. Diane and David stood on the porch, and in the open doorway, Emily stood protectively beside Bob, who was seated in his wheelchair.

Madison’s breath caught in her chest. It was one thing to hear about it; it was another to see it. This was the first time she had seen her brother since the assault. Shaking off her shock, she rushed up the steps and enveloped both Bob and Emily in a tight, fierce hug. "I'm so glad to see you both," she whispered.

The group didn't hesitate. Without a word, Caleb and Owen got out of their SUV and came to the side of the minivan. "We've got him," Caleb said simply to Daniel. As they expertly and gently lifted Jordan from the van to carry him up the steps, Lauren and Sarah grabbed his folded wheelchair to carry it up behind them.

Diane and David Scott met them on the porch with warm, enveloping hugs. There was no awkwardness about the stairs, no fuss about the inconvenience.

"Welcome, you all," Diane said, her voice full of genuine warmth as she ushered them inside. "We are so glad you're here. Don't you worry about a thing. We'll make it work."

And looking at the faces of this second family that had so willingly opened their home to him, Jordan knew they would. It wasn't a perfect fortress, but it was a sanctuary, held together by the simple, powerful willingness of good people to do whatever it took to keep him safe.
