# Part 3: Making It Work

Once inside, the chaotic energy of their escape gave way to the quiet, heavy reality of the situation. For Madison, the shock of seeing her brother in a wheelchair now morphed into a sharp, painful focus as she watched him navigate his own home.

"Let me just get to the sofa," Bob communicated to his mother with a few soft sounds from their Heart-Hum.

This was the first time Madison had seen him move under his own power since the assault. She watched, her heart in her throat, as Emily expertly positioned his wheelchair next to the couch and placed a walker directly in front of him. With immense effort that made his arms tremble, Bob pushed himself up, using the walker to bear his full weight. His legs, unable to reliably support him, shuffled forward as he propelled himself with his arms. The simple act of moving two feet and settling onto the sofa left him pale and breathless.

It was a gut punch. This was her little brother, her "squirt," who she used to wrestle with. Seeing him struggle so profoundly, his body so fragile, was an agony that twisted deep in her gut.

As Bob caught his breath, Emily, who seemed to have shot up another two inches since Christmas and now stood at a staggering 5'10", was already there. Without a word, she adjusted the pillows behind his back and handed him his mPad, her movements fluid and practiced. This was their new normal, and seeing it up close, in person, was infinitely more painful than hearing about it over the phone

Inside the Scott family home, Dane and David showed the trio to Madison's childhood bedroom—the only bedroom on the first floor. The space was cozy, but it was immediately clear it was designed for one teenage girl, not three young adults, one of whom used a wheelchair.

"Okay," Madison said, taking charge. "Here's the plan. Jordan, Aubrie, you two take my bed."

"Madi, no," Aubrie started to protest. "We can't take your bed."

"Not negotiable," Madison said firmly. Inspired by the incredible generosity of the Brooks family, she was determined to move mountains, too. "You two need to be together. I'll figure something out."

Her dad, overhearing from the doorway, had an idea. "Honey, what about that old pool raft we have in the garage? The simple blue rectangular one. It should hold air well enough to sleep on for a week."

Madison laughed. "Perfect. The pool raft it is."

With the sleeping arrangements settled, the next, more difficult reality presented itself.

"Aubrie," Jordan whispered, his voice low and strained. "I need... I need the bathroom. For real."

The group moved to the first-floor powder room and immediately saw the problem. The doorway was too narrow for Jordan's wheelchair to pass through. The space inside was tiny, with no grab bars.

A heavy silence fell over them as they understood the implication. There was no easy way for him to handle his most basic needs.

"Okay," Caleb said, his voice calm and decisive as he and Owen stepped forward. "This is an engineering problem. Here's what we do."

What followed was an immense operation of trust and logistics. Jordan wheeled his chair as close to the doorway as possible and locked the brakes. Caleb and Owen positioned themselves on either side of him.

"Alright, Jordan, on three," Caleb said. On the count, they lifted him smoothly from his chair. The girls quickly moved the wheelchair out of the way. Then came the difficult part. Caleb and Owen had to carefully pivot and shuffle through the narrow doorway, maneuvering Jordan's body in the cramped space to get him safely positioned over the toilet. Lauren and Aubrie were right there, guiding his feet and making sure he was stable.

Once he was securely seated, the four helpers exited the tiny room, closing the door as much as possible to give him a sliver of privacy. They waited in the hallway, a silent, dedicated team.

For Jordan, the moment was a mix of profound gratitude and deep humiliation. He hated that his body required this massive, coordinated effort from his friends. But as he listened to their quiet, reassuring murmurs just outside the door, he knew he was not a burden to them. He was family.

When he was done, the entire process was repeated in reverse. By the time he was settled back in his own chair, he was emotionally and physically exhausted.

"Well," Madison said, trying to lighten the mood with a shaky smile. "Operation Potty Break: complete."

It was difficult, it was awkward, and it was a logistical nightmare. But it was what family did. They would endure the hell of inconvenience to guarantee the heaven of his safety.
