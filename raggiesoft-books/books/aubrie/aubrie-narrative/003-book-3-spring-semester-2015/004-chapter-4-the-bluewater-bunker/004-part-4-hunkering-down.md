# Part 4: Hunkering Down

The week of Spring Break in Bluewater was not a vacation; it was a siege. After the group had settled in, Daniel Brooks pulled Diane and David aside.

"We need to talk about the kids' school schedule for this week," he said, his tone low and serious. "The PI knows to look for Jordan's associates, which led him right to Madison. He'll be looking for her family next. If Bob and Emily go to school, they create a predictable pattern. It's a trail a professional can follow right back to this house."

Diane’s face tightened with understanding. "So they can't go. To protect Jordan."

"It's the only way to be sure," Daniel affirmed. "I know it's a huge disruption, but this house is our only sanctuary right now. We can't risk compromising it." David nodded grimly, "You're right. They stay home."

And so, the house became a quiet fortress. Jordan did not leave. For his own safety, he was always accompanied by an adult—either Daniel or Laura Brooks, or one of Zoe's older brothers—in addition to the constant, reassuring presence of Aubrie and Madison.

A unique and quiet bond formed between the three teenagers at the heart of the crisis. Jordan, Bob, and Emily would spend hours in the living room, not speaking, but in constant communication. Jordan would text on his phone, and just a few feet away, Bob and Emily would type back on their mPad. They shared videos and inside jokes, their silent conversation a world away from the tension gripping the adults. Emily, naturally falling into the role of caregiver for them both, would bring them snacks and drinks, a silent, efficient helper for the two boys in her life who were navigating the world in chairs.

The days were tense, but the nights were worse.

Every bump in the night—the house settling, a branch tapping against a window—woke Jordan with a jolt of panic. He lay in the small twin bed, wedged between Aubrie and Madison down on the pool raft on the floor, their warmth a comfort, but it couldn't quiet the storm in his mind as he thought about facing his mother in court on Tuesday.

The true hell of their situation became clear in the deep silence of the second night. He woke up needing the bathroom, a simple human need that had become a logistical nightmare. Caleb and Owen, the only ones with the strength to safely lift him from his chair in this inaccessible house, were miles away at their hotel. He couldn't wake the girls to attempt a difficult and potentially dangerous two-person lift. He couldn't wake the Scotts.

Trapped by the physical limitations of the house, he made a desperate, humiliating decision. Carefully, quietly, so as not to wake Aubrie or Madison, he slid out of the bed and onto the cold, hardwood floor. His arms, strong from years of propelling his own chair, were the only tools he had. He began to crawl.

The slow, arduous journey across the bedroom floor and down the short hallway to the powder room was the most degrading experience of his life. Every movement was a stark reminder of his own physical dependency and the lengths his mother's cruelty had forced him to go. He felt like a broken animal, not a college student.

He made it back to the bed a few minutes later, his arms aching, his spirit bruised. He lay there in the dark, listening to the soft breathing of his two friends, who were completely unaware of his silent, desperate struggle. Tuesday couldn't come soon enough.
