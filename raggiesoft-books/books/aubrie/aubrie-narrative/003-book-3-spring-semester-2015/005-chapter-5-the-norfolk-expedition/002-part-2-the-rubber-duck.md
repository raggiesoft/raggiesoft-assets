# Part 2: The Rubber Duck

The three-car convoy settled into a steady rhythm on the long, open stretch of U.S. 58 heading east. To maintain constant contact and keep everyone's nerves steady, Daniel initiated the use of the walkie-talkie feature on their phones, a private channel for their small army. He took the lead, naturally assuming the call sign of the leader of the pack.

"Alright, this is the Rubber Duck to the convoy," Daniel's voice crackled through the phone speakers in all three cars. "How we lookin' back there, Watchtower?"

Caleb's calm voice came back instantly from the SUV behind them. "Got a clean shot all the way back to Pig Pen, Rubber Duck. We're lookin' good. No bogies on our tail."

A few hours into the drive, they found themselves behind a slow-moving semi-truck, a "hog" in trucker lingo.

"Pig Pen, you might want to back off some more," Daniel's voice instructed Mark in the rear vehicle. "Those hogs are messing with my sinuses." He paused. "You got a copy on me Pig Pen, c'mon?"

Mark's cheerful voice came back, playing his part perfectly. "That's a big 10-4, Rubber Duck. The Pig Pen is backin' it down. We're clear."

In the minivan, the girls stifled giggles. The playful seriousness of the men was a welcome distraction from the very real reason for their trip.

They operated as one unit. When Daniel saw a sign for a gas station, the call would go out. "Rubber Duck to the convoy, we're taking the next exit for a top-off. We all stop together." The three cars would pull into the station, surrounding a single set of pumps. As Daniel and Caleb filled the tanks, Mark and the older Brooks brothers would grab drinks and snacks for everyone inside. They were in and out in minutes, a picture of coordinated efficiency.

Lunch was the same. "Watchtower to Rubber Duck, there's a diner at the next exit. Looks clean." The convoy would pull off the highway, park in a cluster, and file inside to take over a large section of the restaurant before getting right back on the road.

The CB lingo and the tight coordination served a dual purpose. It was a practical security measure, but it was also a shield. It transformed a terrifying journey into a shared adventure, a mission they were all on together, their individual cars a single, unbreakable train. It was the Group of 17 against the world, and they were rolling east.
