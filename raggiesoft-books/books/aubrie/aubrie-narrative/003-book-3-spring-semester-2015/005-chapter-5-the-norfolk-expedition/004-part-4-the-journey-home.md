# Part 4: The Journey Home

The massive group hug in the courthouse hallway was a silent, tearful, and triumphant affair. When the group finally, reluctantly, let go, Jordan looked up at Daniel and Laura, his face a mask of anger and exhaustion.

"I want to go home," he said, his voice tight with a fury he had never allowed himself to feel before. "Now. Today. I never want to see this city or that woman ever again."

He shook his head, the injustice of the past few days hitting him. "I missed my Astronomy lab on Monday," he said, his voice cracking with frustration. "We were doing Pluto and the Kuiper Belt. I've been looking forward to it all semester, and I missed it because of *her*."

He then looked directly at Aubrie, his eyes full of a fierce, desperate need to reclaim his stolen freedom. "And... I want to ride a bus," he stated, not as a request, but as a demand. "As soon as we get back. I just want to take a normal bus ride somewhere with you."

Aubrie’s face broke into a brilliant, teary smile. "Anything you want, Jordan," she promised.

"That's the plan, son," Daniel said, his own voice firm with resolve. "We're going to the hotel, packing our bags, checking out, and we are out of this town for good."

An hour later, the three-car convoy was leaving Norfolk, heading south on I-464 to skip the toll on I-264. Longer, yes, but they didn’t have an electronic toll transponder (there’s no toll roads in their part of Virginia). I-464 South would take them to I-64 East though Chesapeake where they would get to experience one of Hampton Roads’s quirks: the sun rises in the west and sets in the east, because of the way I-64 curls back west. You could see the sunset from the eastbound High Rise Bridge, and the sunrise from the westbound High Rise Bridge. This would take them to the Bowers Hill section of Chesapeake when the promise Daniel made to Jordan when they left Norfolk came true: they were officially on U.S. 460, and to Jordan: 460 meant the New River Valley. 460 meant Home! They would follow it all the way to Roanoke, where it would merge with I-581, which would then take them to I-81 south. From there, it was a straight shot back to Christiansburg and the familiar final stretch of U.S. 460 that led to Blacksburg.

As Jordan sat in the minivan heading eastbound on I-64 through Chesapeake, Virginia, he saw the first large, green highway sign for U.S. Route 13, U.S. Route 58, and U.S. Route 460 West towards Suffolk. A profound sense of relief washed over him. That sign was a promise. It was a metal and paint symbol of home. He never wanted to set foot in Norfolk again. The New River Valley was his home now, and he was finally on his way back.
