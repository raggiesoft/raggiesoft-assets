# Part 2: The Second Summons

A week later, Aubrie Sinclair was at home, still stewing in the bitter humiliation of her courtroom defeat, when a knock came at her door. It was a process server. He handed her a thick envelope. This time, the summons was not from her; it was addressed *to* her. She had been sued.

The complaint was a brutal, systematic dismantling of her entire life's narrative. It detailed, in cold, legal language, her campaign of harassment, her slanderous accusations, and the profound emotional distress she had inflicted upon her son. It demanded financial compensation for every hotel bill, every legal fee, and a punitive sum for damages that made her blood run cold.

Her first, panicked call was to her lawyer, Mr. Davies.

"She can't do this!" Aubrie shrieked into the phone the moment he answered. "This is retaliation! This is harassment!"

"Aubrie, calm down," Mr. Davies said, his voice stripped of its earlier confidence, replaced by a weary professionalism. "They absolutely can do this. And frankly, I'm surprised they waited this long. I need you to listen to me very carefully. Do you understand?"

She took a shaky breath. "Fine."

"They have us, Aubrie," he said bluntly. "They have testimony from university officials. They have a Dean's List transcript. They have a judge who is already on the record calling your actions harassment. We can fight this in court, but we will lose. And a jury award for damages, especially punitive damages for emotional distress, could be... catastrophic. It could be everything you own, and then some."

A desperate, wild idea sparked in Aubrie's mind. A courtroom. A trial. That would mean...

"We have to fight it," she insisted, her voice suddenly eager. "If we go to trial, they have to make Aubrey testify. In person. I can see him."

A long, exhausted sigh came from the other end of the line. "Aubrie, no. That is not going to happen. First, Ms. Gable is smart. She would likely use his previous meltdown as grounds to have him testify via a closed-circuit video deposition to avoid further trauma, a request the judge would almost certainly grant. Second, even if he did testify in person, what do you think would happen? He would be testifying *against* you. Do you have any idea how that would play with a jury? Listening to a young man in a wheelchair describe the pain his own mother caused him?"

The brutal reality of his words hit her, but she couldn't let go of the one, last chance. "But I have to see him!" she cried, her voice breaking. "They've stolen him from me!"

"They haven't stolen him," Mr. Davies said, his patience finally wearing thin. "He left. And with every action you take, you are just pushing him further away and giving his new family more ammunition to use against you in court. My professional advice, as your legal counsel, is to settle this. Now. It is the only move you have left that doesn't result in your complete and total financial ruin."

Faced with a legal case she could not win, the prospect of never seeing her son in person again, and the very real threat of bankruptcy, Aubrie Sinclair had no choice. She finally, and for the last time, admitted defeat.

"Fine," she whispered, the single word a surrender. "Settle it."
