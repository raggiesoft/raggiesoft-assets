# Part 1: The 5:45 AM Problem

The alarm on Zoe's mPhone was a shrill, invasive sound in the 5:00 AM darkness of the basement. Jordan Fuller groaned, burying his face in his pillow on the new, magnificent king-sized bed. He felt Aubrie stir beside him, and on his other side, Madison let out a noise that was a curse muffled by a comforter.

"Zoe," Madison mumbled, her voice thick with sleep. "You are an inhuman monster."

"It's 5:00 AM," Zoe replied from across the room, her voice already alert. The lights clicked on. "We walk to Falling Branch in twenty-five minutes. The Maroon Express departs at 5:45 AM."

Jordan sat up, rubbing his eyes. The entire Group of 8 was grumbling. This was Zoe's new decree for their sophomore year commute. Fearing "capacity issues" and the "parking panic" that choked the satellite lot, she had mandated they catch the very first express bus of the day. To Jordan, who was finally feeling safe, secure, and well-rested, a 5:00 AM wake-up felt like a betrayal.

Twenty-five minutes later, the eight of them were walking the dark, quiet blocks from Geneva Street to the Falling Branch Park & Ride. Jordan's hands were cold on the push-rings of his wheelchair as he propelled himself over the uneven pavement. This all felt like massive overkill.

Then, they rounded the corner into the Park & Ride.

Jordan stopped, his complaints dying in his throat. The massive commuter lot, under the bright orange glow of the highway lights, was already two-thirds full. And there, at the bus bay for the CPI-bound express routes, a small, tired line of about fifteen students and faculty had *already* formed.

"See?" Zoe said, her voice devoid of any "I told you so" smugness, stating it as a simple fact. "Logistical necessity."

Mark shook his head. "She's not human. It's not even 5:45."

When the large, highway-coach-style Bluewater Transit Maroon Express pulled in, the small line shuffled forward. The driver hopped out and saw Jordan, his practiced, tired eyes betraying no surprise. "Gotta use the lift, son," he called out. "Be just a minute."

Jordan hated this part. The local Blue Ridge Transit buses had simple, quick ramps. The long-haul coaches had a "slow, mechanical wheelchair lift" positioned in the center of the vehicle. The driver hit a switch, and the lift whirred to life, unfolding onto the pavement with a series of loud, attention-grabbing clunks. Jordan rolled onto the platform, was lifted up, and then had to maneuver into the securement space. By the time he was settled, the rest of his family had boarded, and the bus was already surprisingly full.

The bus pulled out, and Jordan let out a long breath. They had made it. They were safe, and they had space. He looked at Aubrie, who had taken the seat next to his securement spot, and she gave him a sleepy, reassuring smile.

The coach arrived at the CPI Transit Center at 6:15 AM.

The group disembarked—another slow, clunky process with the lift—and stood in the center of a dark, quiet campus.

"Okay," Madison said, pulling her hoodie tighter in the pre-dawn chill. "We're here. Now what?"

"We wait," Zoe said, checking her mPhone. "The library doesn't open for another forty-five minutes."

This was the trade-off. They had beaten the rush and guaranteed their spot, but in return, they had to wait for campus to wake up. They huddled in a covered bus bay, chatting quietly until the library unlocked its doors at 7:00 AM. They camped out at a table until 7:30 AM, when they migrated again to the Newman Dining Commons for breakfast.

Finally, at 8:45 AM, they headed to their first class: SOC 101.

Jordan wheeled beside Aubrie, their hands finding each other. This was their new normal. He thought about the looming threat of the IRS audit, the "paper invasion" his mother had initiated by lying on her tax return. It was that threat that had finalized his and Aubrie's decision to take every single class together. He would never have to face a professor or a classroom alone. She was his shield.

He looked at his seven friends, all walking with him, and felt a profound sense of security. The 5:00 AM alarm was a small price to pay for this.
