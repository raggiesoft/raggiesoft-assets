# Part 3: The Transit Center

Jordan sat huddled on the cold metal bench, his hand held firmly in Aubrie's. The pre-dawn chill of the New River Valley was sharp. Even though it was late August, these mountain mornings felt like deep autumn before the sun rose.

He glanced at his friends, all looking tired and cold, and then his gaze settled on Zoe. She was sitting slightly apart, her analytical focus absolute as her fingers flew across the screen of her tablet, sketching out the architecture for her Fin# project.

A profound wave of gratitude washed over Jordan, so strong it overshadowed the cold. Her family. They had taken him in. They had given him a home, a sanctuary, and were in the process of building him a future. Her logic, her family's generosity... Zoe was the architect of his freedom. She meant *so much* to him.

He needed to be connected to her, too. He reached out his free hand and tentatively touched her arm.

Zoe looked up from her tablet, her analytical gaze softening instantly. She saw his outstretched hand, processed the request, and understood. Her analytical mind processed the new input instantly. The request was logical. Jordan's emotional state was stable, and his motivation was clear: gratitude for her family's sanctuary and a need to reinforce the security of their pact. It was a positive data point. She glanced at the other observers—Madison, Lauren, and Sarah. They were smiling. Aubrie was stable. Her conclusion was immediate: The "Pact = true, Jealousy = false" axiom was confirmed and operating at peak efficiency, even in a public setting. She liked this data. It was an efficient system.

She didn't hesitate. She took his hand, lacing her fingers through his, her grip firm and secure. Then, amazingly, she allocated her left hand to him and resumed her work on her Fin# architecture with her right, as if holding his hand while architecting a new financial programming language was the most logical, normal thing in the world.

Jordan glanced over at the other women of the pact. Madison, Lauren, and Sarah were watching. They weren't whispering or glaring. They were smiling. He saw the pride in their eyes. There was no jealousy.

That single, terrifying thought—jealousy—felt like a ghost from another life. The intimate night they had all shared just before Fall Break, solidifying their new constitution, had made this simple, multi-partner act of comfort completely normal.

He was cold, yes, but he was also anchored, holding the hands of two of the women he loved, and he felt completely, utterly safe.
