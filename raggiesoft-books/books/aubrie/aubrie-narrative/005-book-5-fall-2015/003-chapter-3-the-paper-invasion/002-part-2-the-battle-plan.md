# Part 2: The Battle Plan

After what felt like an eternity, the worst of the storm passed. The trembling subsided into shuddering breaths. The keening faded into exhausted silence. He looked at the letter, which now lay on the table beside him, and the fear in his eyes was replaced by a cold, hard anger. He opened it.

He looked up at the four faces surrounding him, his protectors, his family. "What happened?" he finally asked, his voice raw but firm. "What is a CP87A? Why did they send me this? And what do we need to do to fight back?"

Laura pulled a chair over, sitting beside Jordan so she could look him directly in the eye. "Okay," she began, her voice the epitome of calm competence. "A CP87A is not a bill or a penalty. It's a notice. It just means that the IRS computers have found a problem: two different people filed tax returns claiming the same person as a dependent."

Jordan’s jaw tightened. "My mother." It wasn't a question.

"Yes," Daniel said, his voice flat and hard. "She must have filed her taxes and claimed you, even though she knew you were claiming yourself. She either lied, or she's so deep in her own world she thinks she's still entitled to."

"So... what happens now?" Madison asked, her hand finding Jordan's and squeezing it tightly.

"Now, we prove them wrong," Laura said, her tone shifting from explanation to strategy. "The notice gives us two options. Option one is to amend your tax return and agree that your mother can claim you. We are not doing that." Her voice was firm, leaving no room for doubt.

"Option two," she continued, "is to do nothing. If neither person backs down, the IRS will audit both of you to determine who is actually eligible to claim the dependent. And based on the law—your age, the fact that you live independently and provide more than half of your own support—you are the only one who can legally claim the exemption. She has no case."

Jordan looked from Laura to Daniel, then to Aubrie and Madison. His fear, which had been a raging fire, was now being banked by the calm, steady confidence of his family.

"So we do nothing?" Jordan asked, his voice still shaky but gaining strength. "We just... wait?"

"We don't just wait," Daniel said. "We prepare. We gather all your documents—your lease from the dorm, your bank statements, your school registration. We build a file. Then we sit and wait." He met Jordan's eyes. "And Jordan, you need to hear this. This is going to be a long, slow, drawn-out process. The IRS moves at its own pace. It might be months, maybe even close to a year, before you get the next letter. So long, in fact, that you'll probably forget all about it. We'll have everything ready, but you cannot let this hang over your head. You have to live your life."

He squeezed Jordan's shoulder. "She wanted a fight. Now she's got one. And this time, she's picking a fight with the United States government."
