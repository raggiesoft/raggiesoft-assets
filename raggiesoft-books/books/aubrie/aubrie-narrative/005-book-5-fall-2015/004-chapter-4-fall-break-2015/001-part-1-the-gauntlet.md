# Part 1: The Gauntlet

It was Fall Break of their sophomore year, and the Group of 8 was on a mission to see the autumn leaves. Their plan was to drive north to Afton Mountain and spend the day on the scenic Blue Ridge Parkway.

The trip up I-81 was beautiful, the Shenandoah Valley painted in vibrant strokes of red, orange, and gold. But for Jordan, sitting in the passenger seat of the SUV with Mark driving, the beauty was tainted by a creeping dread. He was already on edge. They had just endured the "30-mile gauntlet"—the long, unavoidable concurrency of I-81 North and I-64 East between Lexington and Staunton. He had held up, but it was a near-constant, low-grade hum of anxiety.

As they approached Staunton, Mark put on his turn signal. "Okay, J," he said softly, "this is it. Exit 221. We're getting on I-64 East, just for a few miles to get to the Parkway entrance. You ready?"

Jordan gave a shaky nod, gripping the armrest. "I'm ready." He was trying to be brave.

The moment the SUV curved onto the long exit ramp for I-64 East toward Richmond, his fragile composure shattered. It was too much. The sign, the direction, the feeling of being pulled back toward the place of his nightmares—it all crashed down on him at once.

He went from being on edge straight into a severe autistic meltdown. A raw, ragged sob tore from his throat, and he began to cry uncontrollably, his body shaking with the force of it. His mind was no longer in the car; it was back in Hampton Roads, flooded with a torrent of painful memories of everything his mother had done to him.

Sarah, sitting in the back seat directly behind him, reacted instantly. She unbuckled, leaned forward, and wrapped her arms around him, holding him as tightly as she could. "I've got you, Jordan," she whispered, her voice a steady anchor in his storm. "I've got you." She had to physically restrain him as best she could in the vehicle as it merged onto the interstate, moving at the 70-mile-per-hour speed limit.

Mark, seeing what was happening, didn't hesitate. His eyes darted to the next highway sign. Exit 91: Fishersville/Stuarts Draft. He stomped on the accelerator, getting ahead of a semi-truck, and then cut across the lane to the exit ramp.

At the top of the ramp, the familiar golden arches of a fast-food restaurant promised a safe harbor. Mark pulled the SUV into the parking lot of the "Rocket Burger" and threw it into park.

The car was silent except for Jordan's ragged sobs. The group sat there for a long time, Sarah still holding him, until the worst of the meltdown had passed.

In the quiet aftermath, a silent, unanimous decision was made. They would find another way to the Parkway. They would never, ever subject Jordan to the I-64 trigger again. Some demons were too powerful to fight. The only winning move was not to play.

They sat in the "Rocket Burger" parking lot for a long time, the only sound Jordan's ragged sobs as they slowly subsided. Sarah never let go of him.

After the worst of the meltdown had passed, Zoe, from the back of the SUV, quietly cleared her throat. She had her phone out, a map displayed on the screen.

"Okay," she said softly, so as not to startle Jordan. "I have a new route. We can take this road, State Route 285, through Fishersville until it hits U.S. 250. Then we take 250 west through Waynesboro, and it will take us directly to the Rockfish Gap entrance of the Blue Ridge Parkway. We never have to get back on I-64."

Mark looked at Jordan, whose face was still buried in Sarah's shoulder. "What do you think, J? Do you want to try that, or do you just want to go home?"

Jordan took a shaky breath and gave a small, almost imperceptible nod. He still wanted to see the leaves.

"Okay," Mark said, his voice full of a gentle resolve. "New plan it is."
