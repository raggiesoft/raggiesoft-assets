# Part 1: The Transfer

The transport team arrived just minutes later, their efficiency a stark, sterile contrast to the raw panic in the ER bay. "Okay, Mr. Fuller, we're moving you to the cardiac unit now."

As they began to unlock the wheels of his gurney, Aubrie, who was still gripping Jordan's hand, took a shaky step with them.

"Ma'am, you'll need to follow behind," the transporter said, his voice kind but firm. "We need room to maneuver."

"No," Aubrie whispered, her voice a raw, desperate sob. She wasn't asking; she was stating a fact. "I'm not letting go".

The transporter looked at his partner, then at the terrified 19-year-old on the gurney, and then at the 15 other anxious faces of the Group of 8 and the Brooks family packed into the hallway. He sighed. "Fine. Just stay tight to the rail and *do not* get in our way."

The rest of the Group of 8 and the Brooks family fell in line behind them, a silent, grim procession as the gurney was pushed out of the ER and toward the main hospital elevators.

Jordan, for his part, was terrified. The pressure in his chest was a constant, heavy dread. But as they moved, his focus narrowed to the one anchor in his storm: Aubrie's hand. He was perfectly happy that she was stubbornly refusing to leave. She was his "first true Safe Person", the one who had held him through his first meltdown, and he clung to her as they navigated the unfamiliar hallways.

Aubrie was, if it was even possible, *more* scared. She stumbled along beside the gurney, her body shaking, crying for the entire transport. Her mind couldn't process the reality of the situation. *He's 19. Nineteen!* she thought, her panic a rising wave. *Heart attacks are for old people. For grandparents. This isn't real*. She was overwhelmed, terrified he would die, terrified his mother would find out and use this to prove he couldn't live without her.

When they arrived in his new inpatient room, the transport team helped him slide from the gurney onto the bed. Aubrie didn't just let go; she moved with him, scrambling onto the edge of the hospital bed, never breaking contact, her sobs muffled against his arm.

The transport team, seeing the state she was in, simply backed out of the room, leaving the family to handle the emotional fallout. Jordan lay back against the pillows, his chest aching, and used his free hand to weakly pat the head of the woman who, he knew with absolute certainty, would never, ever leave his side.
