# Part 6: The Longest Night

It was early evening on Monday, November 16th, when the CVICU waiting room doors opened. The entire Group of 17, who had been camped out in a state of suspended terror for nearly ten hours, jumped to their feet.

Dr. Hassan entered, his surgical mask hanging around his neck, his face etched with exhaustion but his eyes calm.

"It was a success," he said, his voice quiet but clear. The collective, agonizing tension in the room broke, dissolving into a wave of muffled sobs and shaky, relieved breaths. "The single bypass graft is in place, and he's responding well. He's a strong young man".

"Can we see him?" Aubrie asked, her voice a raw whisper.

"Yes," Dr. Hassan said. "But I need to prepare you." He looked at the anxious faces of Jordan's family. "He is in the CVICU. He is completely sedated and will be unconscious for the rest of the night. He is on a ventilator, which is breathing for him".

He held up a hand, his expression serious. "We are in a critical recovery phase. You can go in, a few at a time, but you must be quiet, and—this is the most important rule—you must not touch him".

For the women of the pact, those words were a separate, agonizing blow. Their entire relationship with Jordan, their method of comfort and grounding, was built on physical touch.

They filed back in small groups. The CVICU was a different world from the prep room. It was dark, save for the blue and green glow of a dozen monitors, and filled with the intimidating, rhythmic *hiss-click... hiss-click* of the ventilator that was pushing air into Jordan's lungs.

He looked small and impossibly pale, a web of wires and tubes connecting him to the tower of machines beside his bed.

Aubrie stood by the rail, her hand hovering inches from his, visibly shaking with the desperate, agonizing need to hold his hand. Madison stood beside her, her own hand clamped over her mouth, her tears falling silently. Zoe, Lauren, and Sarah stood shoulder-to-shoulder, a silent, devastated honor guard. This was the man they loved, and they couldn't even offer him the simplest comfort.

They stood there for several minutes, watching the steady, mechanical rise and fall of his chest. He didn't stir. He had no idea they were there.

A nurse gently ushered them out. "He needs to rest. You should, too. We'll take excellent care of him."

Back in the waiting room, the adrenaline of the day finally crashed, leaving only a profound, hollow exhaustion. Daniel Brooks looked at his shattered, surrogate family.

"He's stable," Daniel said, his voice heavy. "He's in the best hands. There is nothing more we can do here tonight". He put his arms around Laura and Zoe. "Let's go home to Christiansburg. We'll be back in the morning. He'll need us when he wakes up".
