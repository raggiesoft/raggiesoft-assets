# Part 1: The CPAP Crisis

Jordan’s consciousness returned slowly, pulling him up from a deep, black, anesthetic fog. The first sensation was pain—a profound, agonizing soreness that felt centered deep in his breastbone. His second sensation was something *wrong*. Something was strapped tightly to his face, heavy and claustrophobic. A constant, forceful pressure of air was being pushed into his nose and mouth.

He heard muffled voices. The world was a sterile, blurry white.

"He's starting to come around," a kind voice said. "Let his visitors in, but just for a few minutes."

The blurry shapes resolved. The door hissed open, and two figures rushed to his bedside. It was Aubrie and Madison, his original roommates. Their faces were pale, their eyes wide and red-rimmed.

"J? Oh my god, you're awake," Aubrie whispered, her voice trembling as she carefully reached for his hand, navigating the IV lines.

"Just 15 minutes, ladies," the CVICU nurse reminded them from the doorway.

Jordan tried to turn his head to look at them, but the movement was agony. As he became more aware, the sensation of the mask intensified. It wasn't just *on* his face; it was *forcing* air into him. He tried to exhale, but the pressure was still there, pushing back.

His autistic mind, already raw and overloaded from the trauma of the surgery, latched onto the only available data: *I can't breathe. This mask is suffocating me*.

A low, panicked sound built in his raw, intubated throat. His heart monitor began to beep faster, a frantic rhythm of his rising terror. He began to thrash weakly, his eyes wide with panic, his hands trying to bat at the heavy plastic on his face.

The nurse was at his side instantly. "Jordan, calm down. That's the CPAP machine. You need it," she said, her voice firm but kind. "After a big surgery like yours, it keeps your lungs open so you don't get pneumonia. You have to keep it on".

But her words were just noise. The logical explanation couldn't penetrate the autistic barrier. His brain wasn't processing "it helps you"; it was screaming "it is suffocating you". His thrashing became more frantic, and the heart monitor's alarm became more insistent.

"He can't!" Aubrie cried, recognizing the signs of a full-blown meltdown. "He thinks he's suffocating! Please, you have to take it off!"

The nurse looked at Jordan's terrified, desperate face, then at the spiking heart rate on the monitor. She knew fighting him would put more strain on his newly repaired heart than the CPAP was worth. She had lost this battle.

"Okay, okay!" she said, making a swift clinical decision. "It's not ideal, but this is better than him fighting us."

She worked quickly, *safely* unstrapping the heavy mask and pulling it away from his face. The moment it was off, Jordan gasped, taking a ragged, painful breath of plain hospital air.

"Thank you..." he rasped.

The nurse immediately grabbed a standard, two-pronged nasal cannula from the wall, hooked it to the oxygen port, and turned the flow up high. She gently fitted the lightweight prongs into Jordan's nose.

Her eyes were glued to the pulse oximeter, expecting his oxygen saturation numbers to drop. "C'mon, hold steady..." she whispered.

Miraculously, the numbers held. His O2 saturation, which should have dipped without the positive pressure, remained stable. The nurse let out a small, surprised breath of relief.

The moment the heavy, suffocating mask was gone, Jordan's panic began to recede. The thin plastic of the cannula was barely noticeable. He closed his eyes, his body trembling with the aftershocks of the meltdown, and took a deep, shuddering breath of the oxygen-rich air.

He was in agony, he was terrified, but he could *breathe*. He blindly reached out, his hands finding Aubrie's and Madison's. He gripped them tightly, his two safest people anchoring him to the world.
