# Part 4: The First Flakes

Daniel and Laura looked out the window of Jordan's recovery room. The light snow that Zoe had seen upon her arrival was no longer light; it was falling with a thick, heavy determination, and the sky had taken on a dark, ominous gray.

"Laura, look at that," Daniel said, his voice low. "If we wait until dinner, we're not getting back over the mountain tonight. We need to go now, while the roads are still passable."

Laura nodded, her face tight with worry. "You're right."

She turned to Zoe, who was sitting quietly by Jordan's bed. "Honey, are you sure you're okay being here alone tonight?"

"I'll be fine," Zoe said, her voice a calm statement of fact. "It's the logical choice. You two need to get home safely."

Daniel and Laura went to Jordan's bedside.

"We're heading out, son," Daniel said, placing a firm hand on Jordan's arm. "We'll be back as soon as the roads are clear. You listen to Zoe, and you rest."

Laura leaned down and kissed Jordan's forehead. "We love you. You hang in there."

Zoe and Jordan said their goodbyes, and the Brooks parents left, their departure signaling the official start of Zoe's long watch. After they left, Zoe walked to the window. The world outside was quickly disappearing into a beautiful, swirling curtain of white. She pulled out her mPhone and took a picture of the scene, capturing the snow as it began to cover the hospital rooftops and the distant, blurred city lights.

She brought the phone over to Jordan's bed, holding it up for him to see.

"It's really starting to stick to the roads," she said.

Jordan looked at the photo, then at her. He was still pale and in obvious pain, and now he was trapped by the weather. He looked small and vulnerable in the large hospital bed. "You're... you're really stuck here with me, aren't you?" he whispered.

"I am," Zoe said.

He held out a weak arm, his hand trembling slightly.

Zoe didn't just give him a quick, sisterly pat. She leaned down, carefully navigating the IV lines and monitor wires, and wrapped her arms around him. It was a hug that was deeper than their usual, grounding touches; it was meaningful. She held him, her own heart pounding, feeling the warmth of his skin and the shallow, steady beat of his recovering heart.

As she held him, her analytical mind, which had been processing her "significant" romantic feelings, finally compiled the data. She was in love with him. And she knew, with the absolute certainty of their pact, that Aubrie was okay with it.
