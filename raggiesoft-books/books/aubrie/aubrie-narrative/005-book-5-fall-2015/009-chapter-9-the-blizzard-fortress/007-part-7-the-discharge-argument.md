# Part 7: The Discharge Argument

On Sunday morning, the blizzard had passed, leaving the world outside the hospital window a brilliant, pristine white under a clear blue sky. The roads, according to the local news, were plowed and passable.

The entire Group of 17 had assembled in Jordan's room, a crowded, happy, and anxious audience, when Dr. Hassan came in on his morning rounds.

"Well, Jordan," the surgeon said, a satisfied smile on his face as he looked at the chart. "Your vitals are stable, your fluid is down, and you're recovering just as we'd hoped. I'm signing your discharge papers. You get to go home today".

A wave of joyous relief washed over the room.

"That's fantastic!" Daniel said, clapping his hands together. "Laura, let's get the car. Caleb, Mark, we'll need you to help us get him down..."

"No," Jordan said.

The single word cut through the happy chatter, silencing the room. Everyone turned to look at him. He was sitting up in his recliner, his expression not grateful, but stubborn.

"I'm not being driven," he stated, his voice raspy but firm. "I'm taking the bus home".

A stunned silence fell. "J, what are you talking about?" Aubrie asked, her voice laced with confusion. "The minivan is right downstairs."

"I want to make my own decision," Jordan said, his gaze defiant. He was claiming his independence. "I'm taking the bus."

Zoe, who had been standing by the window, immediately pulled up the transit schedules on her mPhone. Her "Pact = true" logic was in direct conflict with this new, inefficient data.

"Jordan, that is not a viable plan," she said, her voice a firm, analytical counterpoint. "RVT does not operate the Roanoke Express on Sundays". "There *is* no bus."

"Then I'm not going home today," Jordan shot back, crossing his arms, an action that sent a dull ache through his sternum. "I'll wait. I'll stay here until tomorrow. I'll wait until Monday".

"Absolutely not," a new voice said, sharp and authoritative. It was the CVICU charge nurse. "Jordan, I understand you want to go home, but I cannot, in good conscience, discharge a fresh post-op CABG patient to a bus stop. It is not medically safe". "You are a fall risk, you cannot be jostled, and you still require significant assistance. You must be driven home by a responsible family member."

Jordan’s face tightened. He was being overruled, treated like a child again. He looked at Daniel, his eyes pleading for him to understand.

Daniel Brooks stepped forward, his expression a mixture of deep pride and unshakeable, fatherly authority.

"Son, I know what you're doing," he said, his voice quiet but firm. "You're making your own decision. You're showing us you're an adult who is in charge of his own life, and I am so, so proud of you for that".

Jordan's expression softened. He *did* understand.

"But the nurse is right," Daniel continued, his tone leaving no room for argument. "This one isn't negotiable. Your safety comes first. So I'm putting my foot down".

He placed a hand on Jordan's shoulder. "Here's the plan. *I* am driving you home. The roads are clear, the minivan is warm, and your family is waiting. We are taking you home to Christiansburg. Today. That's the final word".

Jordan looked at Daniel's resolute face. He looked at Aubrie, who was watching him with terrified, pleading eyes. He looked at his entire family, a silent wall of loving, protective concern. He was beaten, not by his mother's malice, but by their overwhelming care.

He let out a long, frustrated sigh, finally conceding. "Fine," he grumbled. "But I'm sitting in the front."

A collective, audible sigh of relief filled the room. Daniel grinned. "You got it, son. Front seat it is."

The "Rubber Duck" convoy was rolling home.
