# Part 1: The New Feast

Thursday, November 26th, 2015, dawned bright and cold. Jordan woke on the sofa in the basement to the incredible smell of roasting turkey. This was his second Thanksgiving with the Brooks family, but it felt entirely new.

Last year, he had been a terrified boy, hiding from a looming threat. This year, he was a survivor. He had faced down that threat in a Norfolk courtroom and won. He had faced his own mortality on an operating table and won. This Thanksgiving wasn't an escape; it was a celebration.

He had spent Monday and Tuesday in a quiet, productive routine: resting on the sofa, being cared for by Daniel and Laura, and working on his classes. His professors, armed with the documentation from Star City, had been incredibly accommodating, granting him extensions and moving his final exams to the university's testing center. He was on track to pass.

Now, as the full Group of 17 gathered, the house was filled with a warm, happy chaos. The feast itself had been meticulously re-planned by Laura and the group to accommodate his recovery. This was a heart-healthy Thanksgiving.

There was still a magnificent roasted turkey, but the skin was removed before serving. The mashed potatoes were made with skim milk and olive oil instead of heavy cream and butter. The green bean casserole was made with a low-sodium mushroom soup, and the stuffing was rich with herbs and vegetables rather than salt.

Jordan, who was now strong enough to sit in his wheelchair at the massive, extended dining table, looked at the incredible spread. He was still on sternal precautions and moved slowly, but he was *there*, at the table, surrounded by his entire family.

Daniel stood at the head of the table, his voice thick with emotion. "I have never been more thankful than I am today," he said, his gaze landing on Jordan. "To have all of us here, safe, healthy, and together. Happy Thanksgiving, family."

A chorus of "Happy Thanksgiving!" echoed around the room.

Jordan ate his plate of turkey, mashed potatoes, and green beans, his heart so full he could barely breathe. He was home. He was safe. And he had won.
