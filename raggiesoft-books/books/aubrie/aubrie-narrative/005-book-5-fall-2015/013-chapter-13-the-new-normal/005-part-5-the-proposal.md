# Part 5: The Proposal

Friday evening in the Brooks' basement was a buzz of new, academic energy. The Group of 8, home for the weekend, was gathered around the large dining table, their laptops open. The English 102 group project was their main focus: a proposal to fix a problem on the CPI campus.

"The accessibility idea is brilliant, Jordan," Hannah said, "but where do we even start? The campus is huge."

"We start here," Jordan said, tapping his laptop. He had pulled up the university's official accessibility website. "Here's the map they provide for students with disabilities. It shows ramps, elevators, and push button doors."

The group leaned in. The map was, to be blunt, awful. It was crudely drawn, looking more like a cartoon than a functional guide.

"Look at this," Jordan said, zooming in. "This icon for a push button door isn't even near the actual entrance. We know that. We use it every day.".

He then scrolled to the part of campus where their old dorm, The Old Lady on the Hill, used to be. "And look. Bennett Hall. It's just... a building. There's nothing here."

"What do you mean?" Mark asked, leaning in.

"Bennett Hall had an elevator that let you cut through the building to get up the hill," Jordan explained, his frustration growing. "It was a major accessibility route, but it's not marked on the map at all. There's no explanation that a student ID could access the first and second floor entrances to use it". He pointed to the corner of the PDF. "And look, the map is old. It came out when Bennett was still open, so it *should* be on here".

He sat back, a look of sudden, sharp clarity on his face. "This is it," he said, his voice full of a new, focused energy. "This is our proposal."

"What, to get them to fix the map?" Lauren asked.

"Not just the map. The *information*," Jordan said. "We can't ask the university for millions of dollars to build new ramps and elevators. That's not a realistic proposal for one semester". "But this? Updating a PDF? Fixing the icons? Writing clear, accurate descriptions of *existing* accessible routes, like the one at Bennett? That's not expensive. That's just... work. It's something that can be easily fixed".

A wave of unanimous agreement went through the group. It was the perfect idea: focused, achievable, and deeply personal. This would be their project.

Megan, who had been sitting with them at the table, clapped her hands together, her face a mask of pure pride. "Jordan, that is brilliant!" she cheered. "Yes! That's exactly what you should do! People with disabilities deserve to succeed in school without having to guess where a ramp or an elevator is. You show them how to fix it!".
