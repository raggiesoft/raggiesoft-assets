# Part 5: The Box Office Confrontation

It was Wednesday afternoon, five days after the ticket drop. The email notification had arrived that morning: **"Your Ceremonial Ticket Stubs are ready for pickup at The Crucible Box Office, Gate 1."**

The walk from the transit center to the stadium was short, but for Jordan, it felt like walking into a war zone. The massive concrete structure of **The Crucible** loomed over them, casting a long shadow. His heart began to race, a phantom echo of the panic he had felt two years ago during the 38-0 shutout.

He wasn't alone. Zoe was pushing his wheelchair, her presence a steady, logical force behind him. On his left, his wife Aubrie held his hand, her thumb rubbing his knuckles in a soothing rhythm. On his right, Megan held his other hand, her grip firm and sisterly. He was flanked by his fortress.

"You okay, J?" Megan asked softly.

"Yeah," Jordan breathed, staring at the gate where he had once fled in terror. "Just... memories."

"We're making new ones today," Aubrie promised.

They joined the short line at the box office window. Directly in front of them was a man in a cheap suit, his face red, waving a stack of printed papers at the young woman behind the glass.

"This is unacceptable!" the man shouted, his voice echoing off the concrete walls. "I have clients willing to pay top dollar for floor seats! I have a bot net that hit your server at 10:00:01! How is it possible that *every single* floor seat was unavailable?"

The box office worker, a student with a bored expression, didn't flinch. "Sir, as the website stated, the floor and lower bowl were reserved for the Forger Nation presale. Those tickets are sold out."

"That's discrimination!" the scalper spat. "I'm a legitimate broker! I demand to speak to a manager. I want to know who bought those tickets!"

"Students, sir," the worker said, popping her gum. "Students bought them. And since the tickets are locked to their student IDs, you couldn't resell them even if you had them. Now, do you want to buy a nosebleed seat or not?"

The scalper looked like he was going to explode. He slammed his hand on the counter. "This band is a joke! They're leaving money on the table! Amateur hour!"

He stormed away, muttering furiously, brushing past Jordan's wheelchair without even seeing him.

Zoe watched him go, a small, satisfied smirk on her face. "Inefficient business model," she noted dryly. "He failed to account for the 'Loyalty' variable."

Jordan looked at the retreating scalper, then at the stadium. The fear in his chest loosened. The big bad wolf of the ticket world had just been defeated by a student worker and a band that cared more about *him* than about money.

"Next," the worker called out.

Zoe rolled Jordan up to the window. He let go of Megan's hand just long enough to pull his wallet from his pocket. He slapped his **Poly Pass** onto the metal tray.

"Pickup for Jordan Shawn Kelly," he said, his voice steady. "Section 104, Row A."

The worker's bored expression vanished instantly. She smiled, recognizing the prime location. "Oh, front row. Nice. Let me grab those."

She disappeared for a moment and returned with a heavy, gold-foiled envelope. She slid it through the slot.

Jordan picked it up. He opened the flap and pulled out the four heavy cardstock stubs. They were beautiful—maroon and orange, with gold foil embossing of **The Iron Forger** mascot and the tour dates.

He handed one to Aubrie, one to Megan, and reached back to hand one to Zoe. He held his own up to the light. It was just a piece of cardboard, a souvenir that couldn't even get him into the building. But holding it in the shadow of the stadium that had once broken him, it felt like a medal of valor.

"We got 'em," he whispered. "We're really going."
