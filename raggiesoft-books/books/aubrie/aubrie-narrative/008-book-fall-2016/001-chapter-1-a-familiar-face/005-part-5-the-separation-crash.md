# Part 5: The Separation Crash

The first day of their junior year, Monday, was drawing to a close. The group of nine—the Group of 8 plus Jessica—had spent the entire day together, moving from Latin, to lunch, to their shared Astrophysics lecture, and then to the library for a study session. Jordan was vibrating with a joy he hadn't felt in years, his hand securely holding Jessica's.

They arrived at the CPI Transit Center, the central hub where their paths would have to diverge. The reality of the moment began to settle in. The express bus back to the Christiansburg compound was arriving, and Jessica, who lived in a dorm on campus, would have to walk back.

It was time to say goodbye.

"Well," Jessica said, her voice hesitant as she started to gently pull her hand away. "This was... this was an amazing day, Jordan. I'll see you in class tomorrow."

The simple act of her letting go was the trigger.

Jordan's grip on her hand tightened with a desperate, white-knuckled strength. "No," he whispered, his voice suddenly small and tight. "You... you can't leave."

"Jordan, I have to," she said gently. "My dorm is that way."

"No!" he said, his voice cracking. The terror of his 16-year-old self resurfaced, the memory of his mother's rude and nasty phone call and the agony of losing his first, secret girlfriend. His brain couldn't distinguish between a temporary goodbye and the permanent, traumatic separation his mother had forced on them. He was losing her all over again.

A low, guttural hum started in his chest. His body began to rock back and forth in his wheelchair. He was having a massive autistic meltdown, right there on the crowded bus platform.

The Group of 8, who could not bear to see Jordan in so much emotional distress, immediately formed their protective fortress around him.

"J, you're okay, you're safe," Madison said, her hand on his shoulder.

Mark and Hannah exchanged a panicked look. They knew he was no longer on sternal precautions, but his sternum was still surgically repaired. A full, violent meltdown where he tensed his chest was still dangerous. They had to pull him out of the meltdown, not just wait for it to pass.

"Jordan, stop tensing!" Mark said, his voice firm.

But Jordan was lost, his eyes squeezed shut, shaking his head. "She's leaving... she's leaving..."

Aubrie Kelly, his wife, immediately understood. This wasn't a normal overload; this was a trauma response. She stepped forward, putting her hand on Jessica's arm.

"He can't say goodbye," Aubrie said, her decision absolute. "So don't. Come home with us. To Christiansburg. Just for the night. He just needs to know you're not gone."

Jessica stared at her, her eyes wide with confusion. "What? To your... your *house*?" she stammered. "Aubrie, I can't. You're his *wife*. I'm not... I can't intrude."

"This is the No Jealousy Pact," Aubrie insisted, her voice firm but kind. "He's hurting. He needs *you*. That's all that matters. Please."

Jessica looked past Aubrie at Jordan, who was now openly crying, his face a mask of terror, still rocking. She couldn't stand to see him in this much pain. Then, she thought of her own dorm. She thought of her loud, obnoxious roommates she was not getting along with. A night away from them, in a safe, quiet house, was a blessing in disguise.

She made her decision. She took Jordan's other hand. "Okay," she said, her voice clear. "Okay, Jordan. I'm not leaving. I'm coming with you."

Instantly, the rocking slowed. The low hum in his chest subsided. He let out a long, shuddering, tearful breath. He was still overwhelmed, but the trigger—the separation—was gone.
