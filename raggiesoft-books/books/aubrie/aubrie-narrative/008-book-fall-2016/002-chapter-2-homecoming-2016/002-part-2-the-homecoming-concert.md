# Part 2: The Homecoming Concert

Later that Friday afternoon, the celebration moved outside. As part of the official Homecoming festivities, **The Stardust Engine** was playing a free, open-air mini-concert on the steps of **Newman Hall**, facing down the Alumni Mall toward the Pylons.

But it wasn't just a concert; it was a feast.

The air wasn't just crisp with autumn leaves; it was thick with the delicious, savory smell of burgers, fries, and tacos. Lined up along the bottom of the steps were a dozen official CPI Dining Services food carts. But there were no cash registers, and no one was swiping a meal plan.

A massive banner draped over the center cart read: **LUNCH IS ON THE ENGINE ROOM. EAT UP, FORGERS.**

Jordan sat in his wheelchair in the front row, a half-eaten taco on a paper plate in his lap and a cold soda in the cupholder of his chair. His family formed their familiar, protective semi-circle around him. On his left, his wife Aubrie held his hand. On his right, Jessica held the other. They were all eating, laughing, and surrounded by 5,000 other students who were currently enjoying the universal currency of college: free food.

Ryan O'Connell, the band's guitarist, stepped up to the mic, strumming a lazy, happy chord on his acoustic guitar.

"How's the food?" Ryan asked, his voice booming over the lawn. "You guys getting enough to eat?"

The crowd cheered, many raising their paper plates and sodas in a toast, mouths full.

"Good," Ryan said, his smile turning a little wistful as he looked up at the building behind him. "Because I remember starving on these exact steps waiting for a student loan check that never came. We used to scrape up nickels just to buy a bag of chips in that hallway behind me."

He gestured to the carts. "But today... the menu is open, and the tab is settled. Eat up, Forgers!"

The crowd roared their appreciation. It was the ultimate "Rich Alumni" move, but it felt grounded and real. They weren't buying a library; they were buying lunch for the kids who needed it.

"This set is for the ones building the future on an empty stomach," Ryan said, kicking the band into a tight, high-energy set of their hits.

They played for an hour, feeding off the energy of the home crowd. Then, as the sun began to dip lower, **Cassidy O’Connell**, the lead singer, stepped to the mic for the finale.

"We couldn't leave without playing this one," she said, smiling at the crowd. "This is for everyone who found their color right here in Blacksburg."

She hit the opening piano motif of **"Electric Color"**.

The crowd cheered, but for Jordan, the world fell away. He wasn't thinking about the football game, or his mother, or the surgery. He was just feeling the music.

Oh, I'm seeing in electric color!

When the chorus hit, Jordan sang along at the top of his lungs, his voice joining the thousands of other Forgers. He squeezed Aubrie's hand. He squeezed Jessica's hand. He looked back at Mark and Hannah, at Zoe and Madison, at Lauren and Sarah and Megan.

He was so happy. The song swelled to its final, triumphant chord, ringing out across the campus. Jordan closed his eyes, letting the sound wash over him. He was home, he was loved, and his life was bright, electric, and entirely his own.
