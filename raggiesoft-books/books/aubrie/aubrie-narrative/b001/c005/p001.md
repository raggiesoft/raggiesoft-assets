# Part 1: A Welcome Contrast

Later in the day, the atmosphere in the suite was one of quiet, protective support. Lauren moved like a shadow, her eyes red-rimmed and hollow. The girls, and Jordan, stuck close to her, a silent pack ensuring she was never truly alone.

That evening, Hannah’s boyfriend, Mark, came to visit. He was a stark contrast to Tom—a guy with a warm, easy smile and a relaxed confidence that put everyone at ease. Hannah introduced him to the group. When he got to Jordan, he didn't blink an eye.

"Hey man, Mark. Good to meet you," he said, reaching out to shake Jordan's hand. "Hannah told me about the housing mix-up, that's wild. Glad you guys were cool about it." His immediate, unquestioning acceptance was a breath of fresh air.

They all settled in Room 216, and the conversation eventually, carefully, turned to the previous night.

"Yeah, I heard what Tom did," Mark said, shaking his head with disgust as Hannah filled him in. "Honestly? Not surprised. The guy's always been a loser."

"You know him?" Madison asked.

"Yeah, he's in my PolySci lecture," Mark said. "I never liked the guy. He's so two-faced. Acts like a player, but then he's always draped all over his girlfriend, Sarah, in the dining hall like he's the perfect boyfriend."

The name hung in the air for a moment before Lauren looked up, her expression confused. "Sarah?" she asked, her voice small. "Who's Sarah?"

Mark looked at her, then around at the other suddenly still faces, and the awful realization of what was happening dawned on him. He had just walked into a minefield.

"Uh..." he stammered, his friendly demeanor evaporating into horror. "His girlfriend? Long brown hair, always wears a green jacket? They've been dating for like, two years."

The description was a lightning bolt. Lauren had seen that girl. She’d asked Tom about her once, and he had brushed it off, calling her a "friend from his hometown." The truth crashed down on Lauren with the force of a physical blow. The looks, the whispered conversations she’d ignored, the times he was suddenly unavailable. It all clicked into place in the most sickening way possible.

She hadn't been cheated on. She hadn't been dumped for being suspicious. She had been the other woman. Her entire relationship, every "I love you," every promise, had been a lie. She was the affair.

The sound that escaped her throat this time wasn't one of heartbreak. It was a sound of pure, hollowed-out horror.
