# Part 1: Roommate Change Agreement

It was the third week of September, which the university had designated as "Health and Safety Week." The official roommate-change period had closed the week prior, and the six residents of Suite 214/216 had made their decision by doing nothing at all—they simply let the deadline pass, a silent testament to their shared desire to stay together.

But for the university, silence wasn't going to be enough.

That Friday evening, Kelsey, their Hall Director, knocked on the door of Room 214, a clipboard in her hand. "Hi everyone," she said, her expression professional but kind. "I know the change period has officially ended, but I need to do one final check-in with you all."

She held up the clipboard, which had an official-looking university form on it. "Because your suite's situation is highly unusual due to the initial clerical error, verbal consent isn't enough for the university's records. We need a formal, written confirmation from everyone."

She presented the form to them. It was titled "Suite Arrangement Consent Form." It stated, in clear, bureaucratic language, that all six residents were aware of the co-ed nature of their suite, that they all consented to the living arrangement willingly, and that they wished for it to continue for the remainder of the academic year. "This is for your protection as well as the university's," Kelsey explained.

It felt less like a form and more like a treaty. It was a contract for their family.

One by one, they passed the clipboard around. Each of them read the short paragraph and then solemnly signed their name on the line next to their printed one. Madison Elizabeth Scott. Zoe Wren Brooks. Hannah Rose Parker. Lauren Nicole Barnes. Aubrie Jean Kelly. And finally, Aubrey Jordan Fuller. Six signatures, sealing their pact in ink.

"Thank you," Kelsey said, taking the clipboard back. "This is now officially on file. I'm glad you were all able to make this work."

With their unconventional family now officially sanctioned and documented by the university, a new sense of permanence settled over them.
