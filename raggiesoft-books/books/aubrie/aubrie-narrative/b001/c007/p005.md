# Part 5: Transforming Jordan into a Forger

The first crisp, cool air of late September had arrived in Blacksburg, and the energy on campus was electric. Homecoming Week was just around the corner, and the student body was already a buzzing sea of maroon and orange.

On a sunny Friday afternoon, the Group of 8 decided to walk to the main campus bookstore in Newman Hall to pick up some supplies.

The moment they entered, Jordan was hit by a wave of pure, unfiltered school spirit. He had never seen anything like it. His mother had only ever bought him the cheapest, most functional clothes. This store was a shrine to an identity he desperately wanted. Racks overflowed with CPI Forgers t-shirts, maroon hoodies, and orange baseball caps.

"Okay, I *need* this shirt for the game," Madison announced, holding up a glittery "Forgers" t-shirt.

"Nice," Mark said, grabbing a CPI baseball cap. "Gotta represent."

Jordan just wheeled himself quietly through the aisles, his hands gently brushing the soft fabric of a maroon hoodie. He had never owned anything like this. He imagined wearing it, feeling like he was a part of the "GO FORGERS!" chants he'd heard on campus. He could almost feel a sense of belonging.

He quickly checked the price tag. His stomach dropped. There was no way. His mother had given him just enough money for his textbooks and nothing else. He was a "burden," and "frivolous" things like school-spirit wear were out of the question. He let his hand drop from the sleeve and began to wheel away.

Aubrie, who had been watching him, stepped in front of his chair. "You're not getting it?" she asked softly.

Jordan flushed, his eyes dropping to his lap. "I... I can't," he mumbled, his voice low with the familiar shame. "My mom... I don't have the money for stuff like this."

The rest of the group, who had been laughing by the t-shirt wall, immediately went quiet. They all turned, their expressions shifting instantly from playful to fiercely protective.

"Oh, no, that's not how this works," Madison said, walking over.

"Dude, Homecoming is next week," Mark added, coming to stand beside her. "You *have* to have the gear. It's, like, a mandatory part of being a Forger."

"But I can't pay you back," Jordan stammered, his mother's voice ringing in his ears.

"This isn't a loan, J," Hannah said, her "mom friend" voice full of gentle, unshakeable authority. "This is your initiation. You're part of our family now, which means you're part of this, too. We're all pitching in."

Before Jordan could protest, his family sprang into action. "Get the hoodie," Aubrie insisted, pulling it from the rack. "He needs the 'Beat Norfolk!' shirt for the game," Madison declared, grabbing one. "And the hat," Mark finished, placing the CPI cap he'd been holding onto Jordan's lap.

Jordan looked at the pile of maroon and orange clothing in his lap, then at the eight smiling, expectant faces of his new family. They weren't pitying him; they were *welcoming* him. He felt a sharp, overwhelming prickle behind his eyes, a positive meltdown of pure, unadulterated gratitude.

"Okay," he whispered, his voice thick with emotion. "Okay."

He left the bookstore wearing the new hoodie. It was warm, soft, and for the first time in his life, he didn't just feel like a boy in a wheelchair. He felt like a CPI Forger.
