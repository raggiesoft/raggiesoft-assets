# Part 2: A Basement Feast

On Thanksgiving morning, Jordan woke to the soft, rhythmic breathing of his seven best friends sleeping around him. In the dim light of the basement, he saw the comfortable jumble of blankets and air mattresses that filled the large room. The scare from the previous day—the knowledge that his mother’s reach had found them—had been terrifying. But waking up here, literally surrounded by a fortress of his chosen family, he felt an overwhelming sense of gratitude and security. They were all here together. No one could get to him.

A little later, after people had started to stir, Daniel Brooks came down the internal stairs, a cheerful smile on his face. "Happy Thanksgiving, everyone!" he boomed. He looked around at the sleepy group. "Just so everyone knows the plan for the day: we're having Thanksgiving right down here."

He saw the surprised looks on their faces and held up a hand. "Your mom and I talked about it last night," he explained, his gaze settling on Jordan with kind firmness. "After the business with that car yesterday, your safety is our number one priority, Jordan. We're not taking any chances. So, my crew and I will handle all the cooking upstairs, and we'll bring the entire feast down to you when it's ready. You all just relax, watch the parade, and enjoy the day. No one needs to worry about a thing."

The group exchanged grateful glances. The Brooks family wasn't just offering them shelter; they were altering their own family traditions to create a shield.

The day unfolded in a cocoon of warmth and safety. In the morning, they all gathered around the big TV, joined by several of Zoe's siblings, to watch The Hudson's Thanksgiving Day Parade, loudly critiquing the floats and singing along to the musical numbers. The air was filled with the smell of roasting turkey wafting down from the kitchen above.

Around 3:00 PM, the "ferry service" began. Zoe's brothers and sisters started a steady procession down the stairs, their arms laden with platters. First came the magnificent roasted turkey, carved by Daniel himself. It was followed by bowls of mashed potatoes, stuffing, green bean casserole, cranberry sauce, and fresh-baked rolls. The coffee table and every available flat surface were soon groaning under the weight of the feast.

Their Thanksgiving dinner was loud, chaotic, and perfect. They sat on the floor, on the sofa, on the edges of their air mattresses, plates balanced on their laps. The sounds of sixteen people laughing, talking over each other, and arguing playfully about who got the last dinner roll filled the basement. Jordan sat in the middle of it all, a plate full of food he'd never had before, a genuine, unburdened smile on his face. This was what a holiday was supposed to feel like.

Later, as he was helping clear away some plates, Daniel Brooks put a hand on his shoulder. "Thank you, Mr. Brooks," Jordan said, his voice thick with emotion. "For... all of this."

Daniel smiled warmly. "You're family, Jordan," he said simply. "This is what we do."
