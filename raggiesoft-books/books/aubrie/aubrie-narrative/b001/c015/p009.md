# Chapter 13 Sidebar: An Unhinged Idea

While Jordan and his friends were focused on their final exams, a ghost from the past was drifting through their town. Aubrie Sinclair, running on pure obsession and fumes, had made the seven-hour drive from Hampton Roads back to the New River Valley. She didn't have a plan, only a burning, desperate need to see her son.

She drove onto the CPI campus and parked where she had a clear view of Bennett Hall. "The Old Lady on the Hill" looked quiet, students occasionally coming and going. She watched for an hour, but the memory of her last visit—the calm authority of Kelsey, the silent presence of the campus police—was a fresh and potent fear. She was too scared to even get out of the car.

Defeated, she spent the next few days driving aimlessly around Blacksburg, peering into coffee shops and scanning the sidewalks, hoping for a chance glimpse of "Aubrey." The search was fruitless.

On her last day, cold and running out of hope, she sought refuge in the warmth of the Blacksburg public library. She sat down at a public computer, her search queries a desperate jumble of her fears. Through this searching, she stumbled upon two words she had never considered before: Guardianship and Conservatorship.

A feverish, desperate hope ignited in her mind. She didn't see the high legal bar; she saw only the definitions: "make decisions for" and "manage financial affairs." *This was it.* This was the legal tool she needed. A judge would have to understand.

Her aimless wandering was over. She left the library with a new, dangerous, and completely misguided sense of purpose. As she got in her car for the long, lonely drive back to Hampton Roads, her financial situation gnawed at her. She barely had enough money to cover this trip; hiring that private investigator again was completely out of the question. The thought of her dwindling bank account only fueled her obsession—this legal path felt like her only remaining option.

As her car merged onto I-295 to bypass Richmond, a sudden, nagging thought wormed its way into her mind: *Why didn't I check the address on Geneva Street?* The PI had given it to her. She had been just a few miles away. The thought was there for a moment, a flicker of logic in the fog of her obsession, but she quickly pushed it away. *No,* she told herself. *They wouldn't be there. They're trying to hide him. The court is the only way.*

She focused on the road ahead, her new plan solidifying, completely unaware that her one, forgotten clue was the only one that had ever mattered.
