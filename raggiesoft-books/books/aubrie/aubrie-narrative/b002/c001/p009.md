# Chapter 14 Sidebar: The Unbudgeted Blizzard

While the Brooks' household was waking up to a day of cozy, snowed-in bliss, Aubrie Sinclair was waking up in a cold panic. She had spent the night in a cheap motel off U.S. 460 in Blacksburg, her fruitless search for Jordan leaving her too exhausted to make the seven-hour drive home. When she pulled back the thin curtain, her heart sank. The world was gone, replaced by a wall of white. The blizzard had trapped her.

Her first call was to her job back in Hampton Roads, her voice a mixture of real panic and feigned helplessness.

"I can't make it in," she said to her manager. "I'm completely snowed in. The roads are closed."

There was a pause on the other end of the line. "...Snowed in?" her manager asked, his voice full of confusion. "Aubrie, it's just a miserable, rainy day here in Norfolk. Where are you?"

"I was... visiting a sick aunt in the mountains," she lied, the excuse sounding flimsy even to her own ears.

Her manager was not sympathetic. "This is an unexcused absence, Aubrie. You know the policy. Consider this your formal written warning. I'll have the paperwork waiting for you when you get back."

She hung up the phone, her hand trembling with rage. A written warning. She checked her bank account online and saw the charge for the motel room—money she didn't have to spare.

The irony was suffocating. If she had just stayed home, this storm would have been nothing more than a dreary day at the office. But because of her obsession, because of her single-minded, futile pursuit of a son who did not want to be found, she was now stranded. She was trapped hundreds of miles from home by a blizzard she had willingly driven into, paying for a hotel she couldn't afford, with her job now in jeopardy.

She had lost control of her son, the university, the police, and now, even the weather and her own livelihood seemed to be conspiring against her. She sat on the edge of the lumpy motel bed, utterly alone, a prisoner in a cold room, while just a few miles away, the object of her obsession was safe, warm, and happy in the heart of a loving family, completely unaware of her pathetic predicament.

Aubrie Sinclair sat on the lumpy motel bed, the angry words of her manager still ringing in her ears. A written warning. The thought was terrifying. Her job was the only thing keeping her afloat, the only thing paying for the gas for this pointless trip, for this cheap, cold room. A flicker of cold, pragmatic fear cut through her obsessive rage. She could not lose her job. She made a silent, firm vow: this would be the last time. She would never jeopardize her employment again, no matter what.

But having made that one rational decision, her mind, with nowhere else to go, snapped right back to its singular focus: *Aubrey*.

She opened her laptop, the screen's glow a stark blue in the dim room. She navigated back to the legal aid and family law websites she had found at the public library. For hours, as the blizzard raged outside, she dove into the articles on Guardianship and Conservatorship.

Her eyes flew across the screen, her mind performing a masterful act of selective reading. She completely ignored the dense paragraphs detailing the legal standards, skipping over phrases like *"clear and convincing evidence,"* *"least restrictive alternative,"* and *"must be proven to lack all decision-making capacity."* That was just boilerplate, legal jargon for other people's situations.

Instead, her mind latched onto the empowering phrases, the ones that fed her narrative of righteous control.

"The guardian may determine the ward's residence..."

"The conservator shall have control over all financial assets..."

"The court may grant the authority to make all medical decisions..."

A feverish, triumphant hope began to build in her chest. It was a magic wand. That's what it was. A single legal process that would solve everything. With one stroke of a judge's pen, she could force him to come back home to Hampton Roads. She could override the university, the manipulative friends, even Jordan himself. She could take control of his financial aid and ensure he saw the doctors *she* chose. She could force him to accept that she was in charge of his life, for his own good.

She never once read the sections about a guardian's duty to file annual reports with the court, or a conservator's legal obligation to provide a detailed accounting of every single penny spent. To her, this wasn't about responsibility. It was about power.

Trapped by the snow in a motel room she couldn't afford, with a formal reprimand waiting for her at a job she was now terrified to lose, Aubrie Sinclair felt more powerful than she had in months. She had found her weapon.
