# Part 4: The Great Journey

While the boisterous game of Warlords of the Sphere raged on at the main table, Jordan and Aubrie sat at a card table that had been set to an accessible height for his wheelchair. Between them lay the sprawling, colorful board of Odyssey: The Great Journey.

"Okay," Aubrie said, pointing to the starting point. "So you have your first choice to make right now. You can take the 'Career' path and start earning money right away, or you can take the 'College' path, pay tuition, but have a chance at a better career later."

Jordan stared at the two branching paths. It was the first real, significant choice he had ever been asked to make, even in a game. His mother had always decided everything. There was no hesitation.

"College," he said, his voice firm.

He picked up the small plastic car, a vibrant blue, and carefully placed a single peg in the driver's seat. It was him. He spun the colorful wheel for the first time, the clicking sound a tiny, satisfying note of progress. His car moved along the path, and a few turns later, he landed on a "Career" space.

Aubrie fanned out a deck of cards. "Okay, draw your career."

He reached out and took one. He turned it over. Printed in bold letters was the single word he was working so hard for: ASTROPHYSICIST.

A huge, incredulous grin spread across his face. "No way," he breathed. "It's fate!" Aubrie laughed, giving his arm a squeeze.

They continued their journey. He landed on the "Get Married" space. Aubrie read the card aloud, her voice soft. A quiet, intense look passed between them. The noise from the other games, the rest of the world, seemed to fade away.

He looked at her, his eyes full of a question that had nothing to do with the game. In a quiet voice, full of hope and vulnerability, he asked, "One day?"

Tears immediately welled in Aubrie's eyes. She reached across the table and took both of his hands in hers, her grip firm and sure. She looked him right in the eye, her entire heart in her gaze.

"Yes," she whispered, her voice thick with emotion. "One day."

It wasn't a proposal, and it wasn't an acceptance. It was something more fundamental. It was a promise. A shared dream, spoken aloud for the first time. He carefully added the second peg to his little plastic car, the tiny piece of plastic now representing a future he finally, truly believed he would have, with her.

He bought a house, a tiny plastic building he placed on the board with a sense of pride. He collected "Odyssey" cards that represented small life events: "Win second place in a chili cook-off," "Adopt a stray dog."

Then, he drew a card that made him flinch. Aubrie read it, her voice faltering slightly. "A medical emergency drains your savings. Pay the bank fifty thousand dollars."

The playful mood evaporated. The words "emergency" and "drains your savings" were too close to his deepest fears—the fear of being a financial burden, the fear his mother had instilled in him his whole life. He instinctively started to pull his hand back from the table.

Aubrie immediately saw the change in him. She put her hand over his, her grip warm and steady. "Hey," she said softly, her eyes meeting his. "It's just a game. And even if it wasn't... you're not alone anymore. We would figure it out. All of us. Together. That's what family does."

Her words were an anchor. He took a deep breath and nodded, pushing the game money into the bank, the fear receding. A few turns later, he landed on a space with a baby rattle.

"Congratulations!" Aubrie beamed, reading the card. "It's twin girls! Add two daughters to your car."

His hands, a little clumsy with emotion, took the two tiny pink pegs and carefully slotted them into the back of his little blue car. He looked up from the game, his eyes full of a lifetime of vulnerability, and looked directly at Aubrie.

"Do you think I could be a good one?" he whispered. "A good dad?"

The question was so honest and raw it made Aubrie's heart ache. She reached across the table, her hands covering his.

"Jordan Kelly," she said, her voice firm with an absolute, unshakable conviction that seemed to silence the room around them. "I *know* you would be. You would be the best dad in the whole world, precisely because you know what a child should never have to feel."

The name hit him with a gentle, surprising jolt. *Jordan Kelly.* He had never heard those two words together before. In his mind, he was still Jordan Fuller—a name that was half his, and half a painful reminder of a past that wasn't his.

But hearing *Kelly* attached to his chosen name sparked a new, radical thought. He'd always just assumed that if he ever got married, his wife would take his last name. That's just what people did. But... did they have to? For the first time, a new possibility unfurled in his mind, a path he had never considered. What if a man could take his wife's name? What if he could shed "Fuller"—the name of a father he never knew—and take on a name that represented love and safety and home? What if he could shed both "Aubrey" and "Fuller" forever?

The thought was so new, so revolutionary, that he could only respond with a small, watery, but profoundly happy smile. Her words were a balm on a wound he didn't even know was still bleeding, and her casual use of her own name as his had just shown him a doorway to a future he never knew could exist. He gave her hand a grateful squeeze and spun the wheel for his next turn.

He stared at it—the tiny plastic representation of a life. A career. A wife. A house. Children. A family.

He eventually reached the end of the board, the lush, green squares of the "Retirement" space. They counted up their money, but it didn't matter who won. Jordan just stared at the winding path his car had taken.

"So..." he said, his voice full of wonder. "That's a life? That's what it's like?"

Aubrie smiled, her eyes shining with tears. "It's one version of it," she said softly. "And now, Jordan, you get to build your own, for real."
