# Part 2: Lunch with the Scotts

At 11:15 AM sharp, the Orange Line bus hissed to a stop at the main entrance of the Bluewater Town Plaza. Waiting right at the bus stop, as promised, were Madison's parents, Diane and David Scott, her brother Bob, and her cousin Emily.

"Hey, squirt!" Madison cheered, rushing off the bus to mess up her brother's hair before pulling him into a one-armed hug.

Bob grinned, looking past her at the large group assembling behind her. He let out a delighted chirp—a clear sound of happiness in their Heart-Hum—as he pulled his sister into a proper hug.

Madison's eyes then found her cousin. "Emily!" she exclaimed, her voice softening as she pulled the younger, now much taller girl into a tight, meaningful embrace. This was her second time seeing her cousin since Emily had escaped the toxic, conspiracy-filled environment of her own home. "I'm so glad you're here," Madison whispered. "You have really grown!"

After introducing Caleb and Owen to her family with a round of firm handshakes and polite "Pleased to meet yous," they all headed inside the smaller, calmer mall.

"So, what's everyone in the mood for?" Diane asked. "I was thinking 'The Corner Bistro,' Madi, since I know it's your favorite."

"Yes! Perfect!" Madison beamed.

They found a large round table in the back of the cozy bistro, and the group arranged themselves with a familiar ease. Madison sat happily between her parents, with Lauren making sure to sit next to Emily to include her in the conversation. Bob and Emily took out their shared mPad, propping it on the table between them. A durable case with an integrated keyboard allowed them to type out their thoughts, giving them a clear voice without needing a family member to interpret.

David Scott leaned forward, his expression one of genuine curiosity. "So, Madison tells us you're studying astrophysics, Jordan. That's incredible. What got you interested in the stars?"

It was a simple, direct question that put Jordan immediately at ease. He began talking about the order and logic of the cosmos, and soon he and David were deep in a conversation about recent discoveries. On the mPad, Bob typed a quick message for Jordan to see: That's so cool! I love space.

When the meal was over, the waiter placed the bill in the center of the table. Before anyone else could reach for it, Jordan's heart started to pound with a nervous, excited energy. This was his chance.

He reached for his wallet and carefully slid out the \$50 prepaid gift card that Daniel and Laura had tucked into his stocking on Christmas morning. For eighteen years, he had been an expense, a line item, a burden. But now, holding this gift from his new parents, he finally could contribute. It wasn't just paying for a meal; it was an act of love.

He placed the card firmly on top of the bill. "Please," he said, his voice full of a quiet pride he'd never felt before. "Let me get some of this."

David and Diane Scott looked at him, genuinely touched by the gesture. "Well, thank you, Jordan," David said warmly. "That is very generous of you."

The gift card didn't cover the entire meal for their party of eleven, but it took care of a good portion of it. The rest of the group happily and seamlessly pitched in to cover the difference without a second thought. It was a monumental, defining moment—he had helped treat his family to lunch.
