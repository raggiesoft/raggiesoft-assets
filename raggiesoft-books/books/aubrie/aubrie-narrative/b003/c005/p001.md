# Part 1: The Convoy Rolls Out

Monday morning arrived, dark and heavy with dread. The week of hiding in the Bluewater bunker was over. It was time to travel. It was time to face the court.

Jordan was very upset. He should have been in his calculus class, followed by the long, comfortable block of his Astronomy lecture and lab. Instead, he was missing three full days of his education, all because his mother was trying to prove he couldn't handle it. The irony was a bitter pill to swallow.

Laura Brooks put a comforting arm on his shoulder. "Don't you worry about your classes, Jordan," she said gently. "Ms. Gable sent all the documentation to the Office of Student Support last week—the court summons, her letter explaining that she tried to get a date during the break, everything. Your professors know this is a required, excused absence. They're on your side."

In the front yard of the Scott family home, the entire Group of 17 was assembled. Before they moved to their assigned vehicles, Madison went to say one last goodbye. She hugged Emily fiercely, whispering, "Take care of him for me."

She then knelt in front of Bob's wheelchair, taking his hands in hers. The two cousins looked at her, their faces etched with worry, and let out a quiet, somber series of sounds from their Heart-Hum—low, questioning chirps and worried moans. It was their last chance to "speak" to her before she left for the battle ahead.

Madison’s face softened, understanding them perfectly. "I know, I'm scared too," she whispered, her voice thick with emotion. "But we have to do this for Jordan. We'll be okay. I promise I'll text you guys the second we have any news." She gave Bob one last, tight hug before standing up.

In the front yard of the Scott family home, the entire Group of 17 was assembled. Daniel Brooks, acting as the convoy's commander, did one last check. "Alright, everyone. We stick together. No stops unless the whole convoy stops. We take U.S. 58 the whole way. It's a five-hour drive, so let's get moving."

The group moved to their assigned vehicles. Aubrie hugged Jordan tightly, her embrace a silent promise of unwavering support. Then Mark stepped up, giving him a firm, brotherly hug. "We've got your back, man," he said, his voice a steady anchor.

The three-car convoy—Daniel's minivan in the lead, followed by Caleb's SUV and Mark's sedan—pulled out onto the street and headed eastbound on U.S. 58.

Jordan wasn't ready for this. He was terrified. But as he sat in the van, with Aubrie's hand holding his and the headlights of the other two cars a constant, reassuring presence in the side mirror, a different feeling began to take root. He was scared, yes, but he was not alone. He was heading into battle with an army at his back. He had no choice. He had to contest the hearing. He had to fight to live his own life as a fully functional adult. And he had to win.
