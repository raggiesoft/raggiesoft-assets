# Part 9: The Summer Exodus

The final week of the Spring 2015 semester was a whirlwind of organized chaos. The air in Suite 214/216 was thick with the scent of cardboard boxes and the sounds of finals-week stress, but it was all underscored by a current of pure, joyous anticipation. Their first year of college was over.

The "Jordan Comms" chat, once a lifeline born of crisis, was now the buzzing, happy hub of their daily lives. And Jordan, no longer the shy boy who needed others to speak for him, was one of its most active participants.

Jordan Comms: Zoe: \[LOGISTICS\] Final room inspection is at 3:00 PM. All personal items must be removed from the suite by then. Jordan: Roger that, Commander Zoe. My stuff is packed and ready for extraction. Mark: My room (312) is officially empty. Who needs help carrying boxes down? Jordan: Thanks, man. I think the girls and I have it handled, but we'll ping you if we need the big guns.

After his last final, Jordan wheeled back into the now-empty Room 214. He stopped beside the stripped-bare mattress of Bed A. It felt strange to see it so stark. This was the first place he had ever found real peace, the first bed he had ever felt truly safe in, starting with the night Aubrie had climbed in to chase away his nightmares. He hoped the next student who got this spot wouldn't have to deal with a co-ed clerical error, though he willingly admitted to himself that he was grateful for it. That mistake had given him these women, the greatest group of friends a frightened, lonely boy could ever ask for.

Down at the curb, the final move-out was in full swing. Daniel and the Brooks brothers were there with the minivan and an SUV. It took a few trips, but working together as a practiced team, they moved everything out of the different rooms: the six suitemates from 214 and 216, Sarah's things from 505, and Mark's belongings from 312. They stripped the rooms of everything that belonged to the occupants, leaving nothing but the university-provided furniture—beds, dressers, and desks were left in the room, but stripped bare.

As they pulled away from "The Old Lady on the Hill" for the last time that school year, the shy, terrified boy who had arrived nine months ago was gone forever. In his place sat a confident man, surrounded by his family, ready for the summer and the future he had fought so hard to win.
