# Part 2: The 45-Minute Wait

The slow, mechanical whine of the wheelchair lift was the only sound in the 6:15 AM stillness of the CPI Transit Center. Jordan maneuvered his chair onto the platform, was lowered to the pavement, and waited as the driver stowed the lift, a process that felt like an eternity in the pre-dawn chill.

As the Maroon Express pulled away, the handful of other passengers—mostly staff and contractors who kept the campus running—dispersed quickly into the darkness, their ID cards clicking as they unlocked the buildings they were there to open.

In seconds, the Group of 8 was left alone in the vast, empty transit center. The campus was dark and silent.

"Okay," Madison said, pulling her hoodie tighter and blowing into her hands. "We're here. Now what?"

Zoe, "inhuman monster" and architect of their morning, was already checking her mPhone. "We wait," she stated simply. "The library doesn't open for another forty-five minutes".

A collective groan went up from the group. "Forty-five minutes?" Lauren chafed. "We're just going to sit here in the cold?"

"This," Mark said, gesturing to their empty surroundings, "is the trade-off, I guess. No more rolling out of bed in 'The Old Lady' ten minutes before class".

This was the new hassle of commuting: the long, early bus ride from Christiansburg just to beat the crowds, only to arrive on a campus that was still asleep.

"It's not ideal, but it's secure," Zoe said, ever the pragmatist. "Let's find shelter."

She led them to a nearby bus bay that was shielded from the wind by thick brick walls on two sides. They huddled together on the cold metal benches, a small island of warmth and grumbling. Mark and Hannah shared a pair of earbuds, while Sarah and Lauren leaned against each other, trying to nap.

Jordan sat in the middle of it all, with Aubrie on the bench beside him, her head resting on his shoulder. He was tired, he was cold, and he wished he were back in the king-sized bed in the basement. But as he looked at his friends, all of them here with him, he felt a quiet, steady sense of rightness. This commute was a hassle, yes, but it was *their* hassle. It was the price of their freedom, and they were paying it together. He took Aubrie's hand, lacing his fingers through hers as they waited for the campus to wake up.
