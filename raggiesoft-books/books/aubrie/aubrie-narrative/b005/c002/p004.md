# Part 4: The Campus Wakes Up

The heavy glass doors of the library unlocked with an audible *click* precisely at 7:00 AM, signaling the official start of the campus day. The Group of 8, stiff and cold from their 45-minute wait, stood up and stretched.

"Okay, I don't know about you guys, but I need a bathroom," Mark announced to the group.

Jordan nodded. This was the new logistical hurdle of his commuter life, but one his family had already solved. "I've got you, man," Mark said, his voice easy and reassuring. He accompanied Jordan to the nearest men's restroom, assisting him in the handicap stall with the same quiet, brotherly trust they had established at the mall.

When they all regrouped a few minutes later, the consensus was unanimous: they were hungry, and the cafeteria opened in twenty minutes. "A hot breakfast is the logical next step," Zoe affirmed.

"I'll push," Lauren said, stepping behind Jordan's chair. She and Hannah, as the strongest women in the group, had naturally fallen into the role of managing his wheelchair on the notoriously hilly CPI campus.

The group moved out, a familiar, tight-knit unit. As they started up the first major incline, Jordan felt a profound sense of peace. He was back. He was free. He reached out his hands. To his left, Sarah took his hand, her grip warm and steady. To his right, Madison did the same.

Aubrie, walking just ahead with Zoe, glanced back. She saw Jordan holding hands with two other women of their pact, and her face broke into a brilliant, loving smile. Madison, catching Aubrie's eye, beamed back. There was no jealousy, only the deep, shared joy of seeing the man they loved so happy and secure.

Their path took them right past their old home, Bennett Hall. Jordan stopped for a second. The "Old Lady on the Hill" was gone. In her place was a tall construction fence, surrounding a deep pit of dirt and rubble. The dorm was officially being torn down, set to be replaced by a modern residence hall. A strange pang of nostalgia hit him. They were among the last students to ever sleep in those rooms, the last to use that flawed connecting bathroom. That building, and the clerical error that put him there, was where his entire life had truly begun.

Lauren pushed him the last few yards to the Newman Dining Commons. They arrived with a few minutes to spare, joining a small line of other early-bird students, ready for a warm breakfast before their first class.
