# Part 5: A Flaw in the System

The eight friends huddled around a table in the Newman Dining Commons, the warmth of the cafeteria and their hot breakfast a welcome relief. It was 7:35 AM. They had been on campus for an hour and twenty minutes, having spent the first forty-five minutes waiting in a cold, dark bus shelter for the library to open.

"I'm never going to get used to that 5:00 AM alarm," Mark grumbled, taking a large bite of scrambled eggs.

"It's just inhuman," Madison agreed, nursing a large coffee. "I get *why*, Zoe, but... ouch. We still have an hour and a half until our first class."

"It was the only logical solution to guarantee capacity on the express coach," Zoe stated, her tone matter-of-fact as she ate her oatmeal. "The 6:45 AM and 7:15 AM buses would have been at standing load."

"It's fine for today," Hannah said, setting her fork down. Her "mom friend" instincts were overriding her exhaustion. "But I'm worried about the long term. It's late August and it was *freezing* out there this morning. What are we going to do when it's November and pouring rain? Or January and snowing?".

The table went quiet. She was right.

Hannah looked directly at Jordan, her expression full of a deep, sisterly concern. "We can't have him sitting outside a locked library for forty-five minutes in the freezing cold, Zoe. Not when he's still medically vulnerable".

This was the variable Zoe's pure logic had missed. The group knew Jordan was in the middle of his long, multi-year "catch-up" vaccination schedule. His mother's anti-vax stance meant he was still vulnerable to illnesses that the rest of them took for granted. A simple flu, which he had already battled once, could be a serious medical event for him.

Zoe stopped eating. Hannah was correct. Her current solution minimized commute-time congestion but maximized environmental exposure. It was an unacceptable risk to the pact's most important member.

"You're right," Zoe said, her voice quiet as her mind immediately began re-calculating. "The 5:45 AM departure is not a sustainable all-weather solution. It fails to balance efficiency with Jordan's medical needs".

Jordan looked at Hannah gratefully. He had been dreading the coming winter, but now the problem was out in the open. They had to find a new plan—one that still beat the rush but didn't leave them stranded in the cold.
