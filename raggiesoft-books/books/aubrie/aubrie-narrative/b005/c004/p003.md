# Part 3: The Basement Sanctuary

The drive back from Afton Mountain was long and profoundly quiet, a stark contrast to the cheerful anticipation of the morning. The winding path down U.S. 60 from the Blue Ridge Parkway had been beautiful, but the residual tension from Jordan's meltdown was a heavy blanket in both vehicles. When the convoy finally pulled onto Geneva Street, a collective, unspoken sigh of relief filled the air. They were back at the Brooks’ house, the first place Jordan had truly thought of as a home, a sanctuary.

The other members of the group, understanding Jordan was emotionally and physically depleted, gave him space. He was still fragile, the tremors from the I-64 trigger still humming beneath his skin. He headed straight for the basement, his fortress, needing to decompress. He transferred from his chair to one of the four sturdy queen-sized beds that had replaced their old air mattresses. He lay on his side, curled up, but the anxiety wouldn't abate.

He looked up as Aubrie entered the room, her face etched with a deep, loving concern.

"Hey," she whispered, coming to sit on the edge of the bed. "How are you doing? For real."

He just shook his head, the simple question too big to answer. "Will you..." he asked, his voice quiet and raw, "will you lie down with me? Just for a little while. I just... I need you here."

"Of course," Aubrie said, not hesitating for a second. She slipped off her shoes and lay down on the bed beside him, pulling the comforter over them both. She didn't crowd him, just lay at his side, her presence a warm, steady anchor in the storm that was still swirling in his mind.

A moment later, Madison, his other original roommate, came over. "How's our guy doing?" she asked softly. She sat on the edge of the bed by Jordan's other side, a quiet, protective guardian.

"Trying not to think about it," Jordan mumbled, his face half-buried in the pillow as he fought the memory of the highway sign.

Aubrie, knowing exactly what he needed, shifted the topic to the safest, most logical place she knew. "Did you finish the reading for Dr. Alistair's class?" she asked, her voice light. "The new chapter on red giants? I was still a little confused about the helium flash..."

Jordan turned his head, his mind gratefully latching onto the safe, orderly topic. "It's the electron-degenerate matter," he said, his voice a little stronger. "The core doesn't expand when it heats up, so the fusion just... runs away. It’s not like a normal star".

"See, I'm glad you said that," Madison chimed in, her interest genuine. "I was trying to read that chapter for fun just to keep up with you two, and I thought 'helium flash' sounded like something from a superhero movie."

Jordan and Aubrie both let out small, watery laughs. "It's not just you," Aubrie smiled. "It's really complex."

Madison, seeing them both finally begin to relax, decided to join them fully. "Scooch over, J," she said, lying down on his other side.

The three of them lay there, talking quietly about stellar evolution, neutron stars, and the comforting, predictable laws of the cosmos. The conversation, so logical and vast, was the perfect antidote to the sharp, specific terror of the interstate. Jordan was in the middle, cocooned between the two women who had been with him since the beginning.

He closed his eyes, the heavy weight in his chest finally beginning to lift. The last time he had felt this profoundly safe, this completely and utterly grounded, was the day he met them. It was the same overwhelming feeling of security he'd had when Aubrie first wrapped her arms around him during his move-in day meltdown.

He trusted everyone in this house—Daniel, Laura, Zoe, Lauren, Hannah, Sarah, and Mark—with his entire life. But these two... they were different. They were his origin story. In the quiet of his own mind, a new, definitive truth settled, clear and bright as a constellation.

*They are my safe people,* he thought, a profound sense of peace washing over him. *My autistic safe people.*

He didn't need to say it aloud. Lying there between them, the tremors finally stopped, and the lingering fear of I-64 dissolved, replaced by the quiet, steady, and unconditional gravity of the two women who held his new universe together.
