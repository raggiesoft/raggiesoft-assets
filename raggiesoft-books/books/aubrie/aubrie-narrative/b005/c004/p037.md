# Part 37: The Missing Social Script

Tuesday evening, the last night of Fall Break, was quiet. Dinner was a simple, practical affair: leftovers from the weekend's feasts—baked ziti, roasted chicken, and mashed potatoes—served in the basement living area. The energy was relaxed, but a new, palpable anticipation hummed just beneath the surface.

Mark and Hannah gathered their overnight bags. "Alright, team," Mark said with a warm, knowing smile. "We're heading upstairs to Zoe's old room. You kids... try not to burn the basement down."

"Have a good night, guys," Hannah added, giving Jordan a warm, sisterly hug.

As they left, the five women of the pact began their own wind-down routine. But as they chatted, Aubrie noticed Zoe was unusually quiet. She was sitting at her laptop, but she wasn't working on Fin#. She was just staring at a blank finsharp.md file, a faint, uncharacteristic blush high on her cheeks.

Aubrie came over and sat beside her. "Hey," she whispered gently. "You okay? About tonight?"

She assumed Zoe was nervous about the "trial run" of the pact. "I meant what I said in the bathroom and at the mall," Aubrie reassured her. "I'm not just *okay* with this; I'm happy about it. There's no jealousy. You're safe".

"It's not that," Zoe said, her voice small. She closed the laptop, unable to focus. "The data is stable. The 'Pact = true, Jealousy = false' axiom is sound". She looked at Aubrie, her brilliant analytical mind for the first time completely failing her. "I... I don't have the social script for this".

Aubrie looked confused. "A script for what?"

"For *this*," Zoe said, a note of genuine frustration in her voice. "This is... my first time. With anyone. My brain... it can't find the file. It keeps searching for the 'correct' procedure, for the logical, efficient steps for a 'first sexual encounter,' and it's... it's just returning null. I'm 'lagging.' I can't process the *feeling* part of it, and it's making my 'CPU' spike."

She looked at Aubrie, then at Jordan, who had wheeled over, sensing the serious conversation. "It's what you two describe," she said, her voice vulnerable. "That feeling of being overwhelmed by data you can't process. Like your system crash this morning. I'm... I'm starting to wonder... am I autistic, too?"

Jordan and Aubrie looked at each other, a profound, silent understanding passing between them.

"For me," Jordan said softly, taking her hand, "it was always about the *rules*. My mother gave me a rule: 'affection equals jealousy and conflict.' When I cuddled you, and you \[he nodded to Aubrie\] weren't mad, the rule was broken. My brain didn't have a file for that, so it crashed".

"And for me," Aubrie added, taking Zoe's other hand, "it's about the *input*. The world is too loud, too bright, too much, all the time. My brain's 'volume' is stuck at 100. When I met Jordan, it was the first time I met someone whose brain felt... *calm*. Whose 'noise' was on the same frequency as mine".

Zoe processed this. A system crash from conflicting logical rules. And a system crash from pure sensory overload.

"Mine isn't like that," Zoe said, her mind finally identifying the variable. "Mine... my anxiety spikes when the *system* is wrong. When it's inefficient, illogical, or chaotic." She gestured to her laptop. "Fin# calms me down because it's a system of *perfect* order. Tonight... this... it's a system with no rules. It's all feelings. I can't analyze it, and it's making my processor overheat."

Jordan and Aubrie looked at each other and then smiled, a shared, gentle smile.

"Zoe," Aubrie said, squeezing her hand, "that sounds an *awful* lot like you're autistic. Or... something just as unique. Honestly, what's the difference?"

"She's right," Jordan added. "We all 'crash' for different reasons. But we're all here. We're all safe."

Zoe looked at her two friends—the boy whose logic-crash she'd just helped stabilize, and the girl whose "noise" she finally understood. Her brain was still "lagging," and she was still nervous about the night ahead. But for the first time, she understood why. She wasn't broken; she was just... Zoe. And in this family, that was perfectly, logically, acceptable.
