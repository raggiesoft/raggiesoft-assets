# Part 8: The Longest Wait

The doctor left the room, leaving a heavy, stunned silence in his wake. The verdict was in: the angioplasty had failed, and Jordan had almost died.

The new reality settled over them: they were now just waiting. Waiting for a bed to become available at the specialty heart hospital in Roanoke. Until then, Jordan was trapped in this room at New River Valley, a critical patient in a holding pattern.

His stomach growled, a mundane complaint that felt absurd. He was still "NPO" from before the failed procedure, and now, with another major surgery looming, he would remain that way. He was starving, scared, and in pain.

Aubrie, who was still by his side, finally seemed to find her voice, though it was raw with unshed tears. "I'm staying," she said, looking not at Jordan, but at Daniel and Laura. It wasn't a request. "I'm spending the night".

Jordan, who had been dreading the thought of being left alone in the dark with his fear, felt a wave of relief. He squeezed her hand, "glad she's spending the night".

Daniel nodded, his face grim. "Of course. Laura and I will go back to Christiansburg and get you a bag with clothes, pillows, and blankets".

Mark, desperate for a distraction, turned on the small television mounted in the corner. The local evening news was on, the sound a low, distracting murmur. They half-listened, until the weather report came on.

"And as we look ahead, folks," the meteorologist said cheerfully, "we're tracking a significant low-pressure system. It's too early to predict exact totals, but we could be looking at some *accumulating snow* in the mountains in a couple of days. We'll be watching it closely, so stay tuned".

The group barely registered the warning, their entire world contained within the four walls of the hospital room.

Jordan let his head fall back against the flat hospital pillow. He looked away from the TV, his gaze falling on the faces of his friends, who were scattered around the room in various states of shock. He was the one in the bed, but he could see the toll this was taking on them.

"How are you guys holding up?" he asked, his voice barely a whisper.

His eyes found Sarah and Lauren, who were sitting huddled together on the small visitor's sofa. He knew they had their own trauma, their own ghosts.

Lauren looked up, forcing a small, watery smile. "We're okay, J," she said, her voice strained. "We're as okay as we can be".

Sarah nodded, her expression fierce. "We're right here. We're not going anywhere."
