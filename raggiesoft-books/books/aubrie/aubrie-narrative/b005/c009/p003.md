# Part 3: The Longest Day

Wednesday, November 18th, was a day of agonizing, repetitive slowness. The blizzard, as predicted, had stalled, raging outside the hospital window in a relentless, howling whiteout. Inside, Jordan’s world was a small loop of medical necessities.

The diuretic was working with shocking efficiency. A nurse came in just before noon to check the external catheter's canister, which was nearly full.

"Well, look at that," she said, unclipping the 1-liter canister. "This medicine is no joke. We're definitely getting that fluid off you."

Jordan stared in disbelief. "I... I can't believe I had that much pee inside me," he murmured. Zoe, sitting in the recliner, just gave him a supportive smile.

The day was a blur of interruptions. Routine vitals checks, every two hours. Every hour, Zoe would gently hand him the incentive spirometer. "Time for your breathing exercises," she'd say. He would brace himself, his hand pressed against his chest, and take the ten slow, agonizingly painful breaths.

The nurse also had him doing modified exercises in the bed, simple ankle rotations and heel pumps, to keep the blood in his legs from clotting.

A few hours later, the nurse returned again. "You're not going to believe this, Jordan," she said, unclipping the *second* full 1-liter canister.

"That's *two liters*," Jordan said, his mind boggling. "Where is it all coming from?"

"It's the fluid they pumped into you during the bypass," Zoe's voice supplied, her research clearly having been productive. "Your system was overloaded. This is a good thing. It's an efficient draining of the data."

By mid-afternoon, Jordan was going stir-crazy. He was in pain, but the monotony was almost worse.

"I have to get out of this room," he pleaded with the nurse.

She smiled. "I was just about to suggest that. You're not ready for a marathon, but we do need you to sit up in a chair for a while." She pointed to a hospital-issued wheelchair in the corner. "Normally we'd have you walking the corridors, but since that's not an option, we're going for a 'ride' instead".

Getting him from the bed to the chair was a careful, two-person operation, but once he was settled, it felt like a monumental victory.

"Can we?" he asked, looking at Zoe.

"Just a short trip," the nurse said, "and only on this unit. You stay in this hallway, and you have to be supervised".

Zoe took the handles, and for the first time in two days, Jordan left his room. They lapped the CVICU's figure-eight hallway, a slow, quiet victory lap. Just seeing a different set of walls, the quiet hum of the nurses' station, felt like a return to the world.

Back in the room, he felt a small, fragile sense of progress. The day was passing, and talks of his discharge were beginning to feel real.

"You're doing great, Jordan," the nurse said during his 4:00 PM check. "If you keep this up, we could be talking about getting you home in a day or two."

Jordan looked at Zoe, a flicker of hope in his eyes. But then, his gaze drifted to the window. It was late afternoon, and the blizzard was *still* raging. The world outside was a furious, monochrome gray.

The question wasn't just when the hospital would let him go. It was when the *snow* would let him go, and when the roads would be safe enough for his family to come and get him.
