# Part 4: A Virtual Reunion

After the video call, Jordan felt a new, fragile sense of determination. He was still in the hospital bed, but he was mentally ready to fight back against the disruption.

"Zoe," he said, "I need to do something. I can't just lie here. Can you get the nurse?"

Zoe pressed the call button. When the nurse, Beth, returned, Jordan made his request.

"I'm going crazy in this bed. Can I... can I please sit in that recliner chair for a while?"

Beth smiled. "That's the best idea I've heard all day. Absolutely. Getting you sitting upright is a huge step in your recovery."

With practiced, gentle movements, the nurse, not Zoe, helped Jordan, navigating the web of wires and IV lines, and safely transferred him from the bed to the large recliner. It was the chair Zoe had uncomfortably slept in.

Sitting upright, even though his chest ached, felt like a monumental step toward normalcy. Zoe pulled a regular chair up beside him and took his hand.

"Now," Jordan said, his voice firm, "can you get my laptop? I need to do some homework".

Zoe looked at him, surprised, but she complied, bringing his laptop and placing it on the rolling table over his lap. Jordan opened his calculus notes, but the frustration was immediate. The thick bundle of medical leads connecting his chest to the heart monitor was a constant, awkward tether. The wires draped over his arm and got tangled in the keyboard. He couldn't move his arms freely or lean forward to see the screen properly.

"This is..." he grunted, trying to shift a wire.

"It's an inefficient setup," Zoe agreed, watching him struggle.

He tried for nearly an hour. The complex calculus was impossible; his mind wasn't clear enough, and the physical restrictions were too much. But he was able to "make it work" for his simpler assignments—reviewing his English 102 notes and outlining a short response for his sociology class.

He was fighting. He was trying.

Zoe, seeing he had found a small, manageable rhythm, knew she had to do the same. This snowstorm wasn't an excuse to fail. She moved to the small desk across the room, opened her own laptop, and dove into her own assignments. They all needed a fighting chance of passing this semester.

A new, quiet, productive silence settled over the room. Jordan and Zoe, his snowed-in guardian, worked on their homework, a strange, domestic normalcy in the eye of the blizzard.
