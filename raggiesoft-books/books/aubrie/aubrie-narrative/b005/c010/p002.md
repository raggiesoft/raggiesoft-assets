# Part 2: The System Crash

Jordan closed his eyes, his head resting in Aubrie’s lap, letting her words wash over him. *I won't care if you have sex with all five of us...*.

He processed the data. He was allowed to be romantic. He was allowed to kiss Zoe. And now... *sex*. With all of them. The final firewall of his mother's programming—the part of him that had been sheltered and opted out of every Family Life and Sex Ed class—slammed into this new, radical, joyous reality.

His brain, still weak from surgery and anesthesia, couldn't handle the conflict. It was a positive data overload, a logical impossibility. His safe people were offering him a future that his legacy Aubrie Sinclair script defined as shameful, forbidden, and impossible.

He tensed. A low, guttural sound ripped from his chest, a vibration of pure, overwhelming system panic.

The room went silent. Everyone froze. This wasn't the sound of a normal meltdown. This was a deep, physical sound coming from a man whose breastbone was held together with wires.

"Jordan?" Aubrie whispered, her own face going pale with a new kind of terror.

Daniel Brooks was on his feet instantly. "Jordan! Stop that. Son, listen to me. You *cannot* tense up like that. You will pop your stitches!"

The family understood immediately. This was not a normal meltdown they could simply ride out; this was a medical emergency. They had to de-escalate him, now.

"J, shh, shh, look at me," Aubrie said, her hands moving from his hair to his shoulders, applying firm, grounding pressure. "It's okay. It's safe. Just breathe. Don't tense."

Madison, seeing his hands clenching the sofa, leaped from the armchair and knelt in front of him, grabbing both his hands. "Jordan, squeeze my hands," she commanded, her voice a sharp, clear anchor. "Focus on me. Squeeze my hands, not your chest. Breathe with me. In... out..."

But he was still crashing, his body rigid, the guttural sound still rumbling in his chest.

We are the new, stable variable. Accept the new axiom. Do not tense your muscles. You are physically endangering your recovery."

The combination of commands finally broke through. The abstract terror of the emotional conflict was replaced by a new, concrete, logical fear: *I will hurt myself*.

He forced his body to obey. He focused on Madison's hands, transferring all his tension into his grip. He focused on Aubrie's firm, steady pressure on his shoulders. He focused on Zoe's words.

The guttural sound subsided, replaced by a shuddering, painful sob. He was crying, but he was no longer tensing. The immediate danger had passed.

Aubrie, Madison, and Zoe, his three safe people, didn't move. They just held him—Aubrie stroking his head, Madison gripping his hands, Zoe's hand a steady presence on his arm—letting him cry out the last of the poison, their combined presence a shield that was, for the first time, protecting him from himself.
