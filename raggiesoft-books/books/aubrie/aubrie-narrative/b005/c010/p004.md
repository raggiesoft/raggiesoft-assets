# Part 4: The Washing Protocol

Jordan fell asleep for another nap, a deep, healing sleep on the sectional sofa, still anchored by Aubrie’s presence on his lap and Madison’s hand on his arm. When he finally woke, the late afternoon light was filtering into the basement. His first sensation was a familiar, urgent pressure. The diuretics were still working.

"Zoe," he said, his voice a little stronger than before. "I... I need the bathroom. And I really need to get washed up. I feel... I feel dirty".

Zoe, the group's designated logistics lead for his home recovery, nodded immediately. "Okay. Logistics." She looked across the room. "Hannah, Lauren, transfer protocol."

Hannah and Lauren, the only two in the group strong enough to perform the lift together, moved to the sofa. Zoe grabbed a thick, three-ring binder from her desk—the post-op recovery guide the hospital had sent home with them, which she had already tabbed and highlighted.

They brought Jordan's wheelchair next to the sofa. They knew that while they *could* lift and carry him the short distance, it was far safer to use the chair, given his sternal precautions.

"We're just going to pivot you, J," Hannah said softly. "No pulling on your arms. Let us do the work."

Working as a single, coordinated unit, they executed a perfect pivot transfer, moving him from the sofa to his chair while taking extreme care to not put any stress on his healing chest.

The three women wheeled him into the spacious, accessible basement bathroom. While Hannah and Lauren helped him with the toilet, Zoe consulted the binder, her face serious.

"Okay," Zoe announced, reading from the discharge papers. "Here are the rules for washing. 'Baths are strictly forbidden until the surgeon clears you'". "And the primary rule: the healing incision *cannot* get wet. At all".

"His hair is a mess," Lauren noted sympathetically. "And he's been in hospitals for days. He needs this."

"We adapt," Zoe said. She retrieved a clean washcloth, a basin of warm water, and a fresh towel. "We'll do a sponge bath."

Lauren and Hannah gently helped Jordan remove his pajama shirt. The incision was a raw, angry line down his chest, held together by small white bandages. The two women worked with a tender, clinical efficiency, carefully washing his body while avoiding the critical area.

"Now for the hair," Hannah said. "This part's tricky."

They wheeled his chair backward, positioning him in front of the roll-under sink. "Okay, J, lean your head back as far as you can," Lauren instructed. She carefully tilted his head back over the basin, while Hannah used a cup and a small amount of shampoo, quickly and efficiently washing his hair, making sure no water dripped down toward his chest.

Twenty minutes later, Jordan was back on the sofa in a fresh set of pajamas, his hair damp and clean. He felt a million times better. The diuretic was still working, but the psychological grime of the hospital was finally gone. He was clean, he was home, and his team was in complete control.
