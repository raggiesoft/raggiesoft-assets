# Book 5: High School

## Chapter 1: Marching Band (Monday, August 9<sup>th</sup>, 1999)

### Part 1: Jessica’s Wake Up Call – 6:00 AM

The alarm clock sounded, and Jessica prodded me awake. "My last band camp!" She sounded excited, but I was still struggling to wake up.

"Jess, five more minutes," I groaned, with a yawn mixed in.

"Wakey-wakey," She giggled, "I will get changed in the bathroom and I want you to meet me downstairs for breakfast I will have cereal waiting." My sister then hugged me and exited the bedroom. I slowly got up, and got dressed.

I exited the bedroom, and saw that Jess was still in the bathroom. I made my way downstairs and used the bathroom on the first floor. I petted Baby and Archie then went to do my business.

Jessica soon joined me downstairs and, after petting the dogs, proceeded to make us cereal. "We are going to have breakfast then make our way to Northwood. I need to be there sooner because I am in charge of our little section of the band," She said while making breakfast.

Rachel wandered downstairs in her pajamas and said, "I wanted to say goodbye to you two before you left for band camp." The dogs bounded for her, wanting attention and pets, which she happily gave to them.

"Pour yourself a bowl of cereal, and come join us," Jessica said. Rachel groaned, and Jessica said, "You are twelve years old now. I think you are old enough to pour your own cereal."

"I can’t reach the top of the fridge," She pouted.

Jessica rolled her eyes, but got up as she said, "True, and mom and dad don’t want you using the step ladder. I’m sorry Rachel, I forgot that you were not supposed to use the step ladder."

We had our breakfast, hugged Rachel and our dogs goodbye, and got in the car. "I’m going to have you stay with me when we arrive. There are some things I need to take care of before we get started," Jessica said as she made the five minute drive to the high school.

### Part 2: Band Camp (8:00 AM)

We arrived and Mr. Panoff, the band director, was there to greet us. "Good morning Jessica," He said.

"Good morning. Meet my brother Michael," She said. I nervously offered a handshake.

"Good morning," I said nervously.

Jessica had me sit off to the side while completing Pit Captain administrative duties. Directing his attention to Jessica, Mr. Panoff said, "Here is the list of Pit students." They then discussed this and that, and I tuned the rest of it out.

Soon, Jessica came over to join me. She sat at my side, put her arm around me, and asked, "You ready to begin the best four years of your life?" Suddenly, there was a massive fart. Jessica looked up with a groan. "Travis is here," She groaned, "Bathroom," Jessica sniggered, to which Travis chuckled. She plugged her nose then whispered to me, "Ask me later."

The clock ticked eight, and it was time to begin. Jessica, and three others got up and stood alongside of Mr. Panoff who began introductions, introducing the lead instructor, his two assistants, my sister Jessica as the Pit Captain, and Brandon as the Drumline Captain. He went over how we were to obey them as we obeyed him, and other welcome to band camp business.

Jessica came back over to us and, along with one of the assistant instructors, started with warm ups. "Eights," Kenny said.

I was not prepared for how loud the room would get when all the instruments were being warmed up, but Jessica and Kenny pressed us on, and I tried my best to keep up. I quickly got bored, but I did not want to let my sister down on her Senior year and final season of Marching Band.

## Chapter 2: The Concrete Walkway (November 1999)

### Part 1: Black Friday – 4:15 PM

The day after Thanksgiving was an unshakeable routine. Dad always spent Black Friday hanging the holiday lights. By late afternoon, the chilly Virginia autumn air had settled in, and the fading daylight made the Delaney Street house look even more distinctly 1990s—its original bright orange wooden siding contrasting sharply with the dark brown paint of the garage door.

I was fourteen, navigating my freshman year of high school, and currently sitting in my upstairs bedroom with the front window wide open. Sitting on my dresser was a heavy silver AudioStar boombox, its radio dial firmly locked onto 93.7 The Coast. The station was already cycling through their holiday crossover rotation, currently blasting the upbeat, driving saxophone and wall-of-sound production of Darla Vance’s "Solo on Christmas Eve"—the famous track from the *Solo Kid 2: Manhattan Mayhem* soundtrack.

Outside, my nineteen-year-old sister Jessica was standing on the sloping roof of the front landing, right below my window sill.

"Yeah, I like the idea of the multi-color flower lights outlining my window," I said over the loud, cheerful music, leaning my elbows on the sill to watch her work.

"I thought you would like the flower lights," Jessica replied, her breath visibly pluming in the cold air. She carefully inserted a small nail into the dark wooden trim, perfectly securing the green wire of the lights. She was meticulous, moving with the confident authority of a high school senior who practically ran the household.

She finished the line across the top of my window frame and connected the end of the string to a heavy-duty, bright orange extension cord. She shifted her weight, carefully turning to make her way back toward the aluminum ladder resting against the edge of the overhang.

"Jessica!" I screamed.

It happened in a fraction of a second. She vanished from my line of sight. Over the blaring, upbeat sound of Darla Vance singing about holiday cheer, I heard a sickening, hollow crack, immediately followed by a heavy thud against the concrete walkway below.

Panic instantly hijacked my nervous system. I shoved myself away from the window, bolted out of my bedroom, and took the stairs two at a time. I grabbed a startled Rachel by the arm on the first-floor landing, dragging my twelve-year-old sister with me as I threw open the front door.

Dad was already there. He had been stringing white icicle lights along the upper gutters, but the moment he heard the fall, he had abandoned the ladder entirely. The string of lights lay forgotten in the dead grass. He was kneeling on the cold concrete walkway, his face pale and completely drained of color, his hands hovering over Jessica, terrified to move her.

This was the dad we knew back then—the protector. The man who would drop everything and tear the world apart if his kids were in danger.

"I think I slipped off the ladder," Jessica said. Her voice was shockingly calm, but her eyes were wide with a deep, vibrating fright.

We were all terrified, but the atmosphere shattered completely when Dad gently touched her leg to assess the damage. He pulled his hand back instantly, realizing the bone structure beneath her jeans was fundamentally, devastatingly wrong.

"I think I missed a step somewhere," Jessica whimpered. Her pragmatic, tough-older-sister exterior finally cracked, giving way to pure pain.

"Call 9-1-1!" Dad cried out. His voice broke, laced with a raw, frantic parental terror that I had never heard from him before.

It was Rachel who snapped into action. While I stood frozen on the concrete, entirely paralyzed by the sudden, violent break in our routine, Rachel didn't hesitate. She didn't speak—she rarely did—but she spun around, her socks slipping slightly on the entryway tile, and stormed back into the kitchen to rip the phone off the wall.

She couldn't vocalize the emergency to the dispatcher, but she knew how to dial the numbers and leave the line open so the operator could hear Dad's frantic shouting from the front yard.

The paramedics arrived within minutes. The heavy, rhythmic pulsing of the ambulance's red and white emergency lights reflected off the orange wooden siding of our house. It wasn't long before they had her strapped onto a stretcher and loaded into the back of the rig, leaving the cheerful multi-color flower lights half-hanging over my bedroom window, swaying gently in the cold November wind.

## Chapter 5: The Catalyst Initiative

### Part 1: The Bare Metal (Tuesday, February 29, 2000 – 1:00 AM)

The digital perimeter was collapsing, and it sounded exactly like a dying lawnmower.

Under the slatted wooden stairs of 1852 Delaney Street, the beige tower of Dad’s consumer-grade desktop was screaming. The hard drive activity light was locked into a solid, panicked green. The 5400 RPM drive was violently thrashing, mechanically grinding as the operating system desperately tried to write overflowing memory to a swap file that was already full.

Sitting in her manual wheelchair, parked tightly into the alcove, Jessica didn't flinch. She just watched the heavy CRT monitor.

The screen flickered. The taskbar vanished. The icons on the teal desktop suddenly turned into corrupted black squares as the 64-Kilobyte GDI heap was completely obliterated. A second later, the monitor violently snapped to the dreaded Blue Screen of Death.

*Fatal Exception 0E at 0028:C0011E3C.*

Jessica let out a long, heavy exhale. She leaned forward, using her triceps to stabilize her core, and pressed her finger hard against the physical reset button on the front of the tower. The grinding stopped. The BIOS beeped, beginning the agonizing three-minute reboot cycle.

It was 1:00 AM. I was sitting on the floor just outside the alcove, a textbook open on my lap, keeping the night watch. Rachel was asleep on the sofa bed a few feet away.

"Another GPF?" I asked quietly, keeping my voice down so I wouldn't wake our parents upstairs.

"It's the enterprise executable," Jessica muttered, rubbing her eyes behind her tinted glasses. "The memory leak is completely catastrophic. Whoever wrote this corrupted file at Quantum designed it to spawn hundreds of user interface threads simultaneously. The second I launch it, it crushes the OS 95 architecture like an aluminum can."

She stared at the black screen as the memory check ticked upward in white text.

"The other kids in the competition are going to try and fix this using high-level wrappers," Jessica analyzed, her mind operating ten steps ahead. "They'll use standard C++ libraries to try and catch the leak. But this computer can't handle the overhead. If I add a bulky, modern runtime library to this duct-taped OS, the system will just crash faster."

The familiar teal desktop finally booted back up. Jessica didn't open a standard code editor. She opened a disassembler.

The screen instantly filled with thousands of lines of raw, hexadecimal machine code.

"I have to go underneath it," Jessica whispered, her eyes tracking the columns of hexadecimal data. "I have to write the patch in pure x86 Assembly."

I looked at the screen. It looked like the Matrix running backward.

"Assembly?" I asked. "You mean the bare metal?"

"Yes," Jessica said, her fingers hovering over the keyboard. "No variables. No safety nets. No compiler holding my hand. Just manually pushing data in and out of the CPU's physical registers. EAX, EBX, ECX. If I write the patch in pure Assembly, I can bypass the 16-bit foundation entirely. The executable will be microscopically small. It will run with zero overhead, and it will speak directly to the kernel."

She typed a few lines of code, manually pushing parameters onto the system stack. *PUSH EBP. MOV EBP, ESP.*

She stared at it for a long moment. Then, she hit backspace, deleting the lines.

"I can't do it," Jessica said, the frustration tightening her jaw.

"Why not? You know the syntax."

"Because it’s like trying to build a skyscraper with a pair of tweezers," Jessica said, hitting the armrest of her chair in a rare show of physical frustration. "Writing a memory intercept this complex in pure Assembly would take me three months of typing. The submission deadline for the Catalyst Initiative is March 15th. I have exactly two and a half weeks. I don't have the time."

### Part 2: Severing the Runtime (1:45 AM)

She sat back in her chair, the blue light of the monitor reflecting off her lenses. She was trapped between the absolute limitations of a cheap 1995 computer and the brutal ticking of a 2000 deadline.

Assembly was too slow to write. Standard C was too heavy to run.

Suddenly, her posture shifted. Her spine straightened, and her hands went back to the keyboard.

"I won't use Assembly for the whole thing," Jessica realized, her voice dropping into a razor-sharp register. "I'll use pure ANSI C for the logic. But I'm going to rip the engine out first."

"Rip what engine out?" I asked, watching her pull up the Borland compiler interface.

"The C-Runtime Library," Jessica declared.

She navigated deep into the compiler's advanced settings, finding the linker configuration. With a few aggressive keystrokes, she flipped the compiler switches, permanently disabling the standard background library that came with the language.

By checking that box, she had just completely blinded the compiler. She could no longer use standard commands like printf or malloc. The safety nets were gone.

"If you kill the runtime, how does the program know how to start?" I asked, leaning closer.

Jessica opened her main source file. She deleted the standard void main() entry point that every programming student in the world is taught to use.

She replaced it with \_start.

"It doesn't," Jessica said, a fierce, predatory smile touching the corner of her mouth. "I have to tell it exactly how to wake up, and exactly how to talk to the Q32 API."

She began to type. It wasn't the slow, methodical coding of a student. It was the rapid, aggressive execution of an architect pouring concrete. Because she had severed the runtime, she was forced to use raw, direct Q32 API calls to build her patch.

Instead of asking a high-level library to find her some memory, she reached directly into the operating system's kernel.

*VirtualAlloc(NULL, memory_size, MEM_COMMIT, PAGE_READWRITE);*

"I'll build the structural logic in C," Jessica explained, her hands flying across the keys. "But for the surgical strikes—the exact moments where I need to intercept the corrupted enterprise leak—I'll use inline Assembly. I'll drop the bare-metal CPU instructions right into the middle of the C code."

She was creating a hybrid weapon. It had the speed of C, the absolute, microscopic precision of Assembly, and zero bloat to trigger the dreaded 64KB graphics crash.

### Part 3: The Hold (3:00 AM)

By 3:00 AM, the patch was written.

Jessica's shoulders were tense. She hadn't shifted her weight in the wheelchair for almost two hours. She reached up and hit the F9 key to compile the executable.

Because the processor was a slow, low-end Cyrix chip, the compilation wasn't instant. We sat there in the silence of the alcove for four excruciating minutes, watching the progress bar crawl across the screen, praying she hadn't missed a single semicolon.

*Compile Successful. 0 Errors. 0 Warnings.*

"Okay," Jessica breathed. Her hand moved to the mouse. "Deploying the patch and launching the corrupted executable."

She double-clicked the icon.

I braced myself, waiting for the horrific, mechanical grinding of the hard drive thrashing. I waited for the screen to glitch out into black squares. I waited for the Blue Screen of Death.

Instead... there was nothing.

The hard drive clicked quietly once. The teal desktop remained perfectly stable. A sleek, highly complex enterprise testing environment rendered flawlessly on the screen.

Jessica quickly pulled up the system resource monitor. The GDI graphics heap, which had been crashing at 0% all week, was sitting comfortably at 85% free. Her hybrid patch had successfully intercepted the massive memory leak, bypassed the 16-bit duct tape, and manually routed the data directly to the physical RAM.

It was a masterclass in software engineering, executed on a machine that had no business running it.

Jessica slowly pulled her hands away from the keyboard. She looked at the stable UI, and then she looked over her shoulder at me. The exhaustion in her face was completely eclipsed by the sheer, undeniable reality of her own intellect.

"It's holding," Jessica whispered.

She hadn't just fixed the code. She had beaten the machine.
