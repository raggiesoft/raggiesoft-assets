# Part 4: The Corporate Armor – 9:45 AM (PST)

Jessica didn't have to navigate a maze of city streets to find a bank. The Quantum campus was practically its own sovereign city, fully equipped to handle every logistical need of its tens of thousands of employees.

She led us through the automatic glass doors of the adjacent building. The lobby was warm, modern, and buzzing with tech professionals. Nestled right in the center of the atrium was the Quantum Employees Federal Credit Union (QEFCU).

"Jessica, how can we help you today?" a sharply dressed credit union employee asked from his desk as we approached.

"Nigel, I would like for my brother to open an account," Jessica replied smoothly, parking her wheelchair at the edge of his desk.

"We can absolutely do that. Let’s get you signed in," Nigel smiled.

"First-name basis?" I whispered, leaning down toward Kristin.

"Yep," Kristin whispered back, her eyes bright. "You will like it here. It's run by employees, for employees."

Nigel was immediately ready for us. I took the heavy, padded chair across from him, but I absolutely refused to do this alone. I looked back at Jessica and Kristin, gesturing for them to join me. Kristin pulled up a chair to my right, and Jessica wheeled into position on my left. I was perfectly flanked.

I reached into my pocket, placing the uneven stack of bills and loose change totaling \$294.87 onto the desk. I felt a momentary flush of embarrassment at the small pile of cash, but Nigel didn't bat an eye.

"Perfect," Nigel said warmly. "Michael, because we are a credit union, you aren't just a customer here. You are a member, which means you're technically a part-owner. To establish your membership, federal regulations require us to open a Share Savings account. The minimum deposit is only five dollars. The rest—two hundred eighty-nine dollars and eighty-seven cents—will go directly into your free checking account."

I blinked. Delores had constantly used my lack of funds as a weapon, convincing me traditional banks would laugh at me. But here, my meager escape funds were treated like legitimate capital.

"Okay," Nigel continued, tapping his pen. "I just need your photo ID."

I pulled out my Virginia Driver's License and slid it across the desk. Nigel looked at it, his brow furrowing slightly.

"Alright, we have a slight logistical hurdle," Nigel explained apologetically. "Post-9/11 Patriot Act laws require me to have a verified, current physical residential address on file. This license lists an apartment on Greenway Court in Norfolk, Virginia."

I froze. My pulse spiked. I couldn't use Delores's address in Portsmouth, and I didn't have a lease in Washington. I was a ghost.

Jessica immediately stepped in, neutralizing the threat.

"I am officially sponsoring his membership," Jessica told Nigel, projecting total corporate authority. "You can list my apartment complex in Renton as his physical residential address to satisfy the KYC compliance. As for his mailing address, route his welcome packet and his physical debit card directly to my apartment as well."

"Are you sure, Jess?" I asked. "I can just get a P.O. Box."

"You will," Jessica smiled. "But the post office requires two forms of ID showing your new address to open a box. When Nigel mails this bank packet to my apartment in your name, it becomes an official financial document. After Thanksgiving, you'll take that envelope and your Virginia license to the Renton Post Office, and you'll have everything you need to open your own P.O. Box."

The sheer, staggering brilliance of her logistics washed over me. She was meticulously constructing a bulletproof, legal paper trail for me from scratch.

"Excellent," Nigel said, typing the Renton address into his system. "Now, since you are setting this up through your sister's employee sponsorship, did you want to add Jessica as a joint account holder for easy transfers?"

Operating purely on the traumatic muscle memory of the last two years, where I wasn't trusted to handle a single variable on my own, I immediately nodded. "Yeah. Put Jessica on it."

"No," Jessica interjected. Her voice was quiet, but it carried the absolute, unyielding force of a concrete wall.

I looked over at my older sister. "Jess, it's fine. I trust you."

"I know you trust me, Michael," Jessica said, looking me dead in the eyes. "And Rachel and I trust you with our lives. But you are never having a joint bank account ever again. Not even with us."

I stared at her, my processor stalling.

"Rachel has her own separate account here," Jessica explained, her tone softening but remaining incredibly firm. "I have my own account. True safety means having your own money, completely inaccessible to anyone else. Delores stripped you of your autonomy by forcing you to share a ledger. We are giving it back." She pointed a finger at the paperwork. "Your name. Only your name. You are an adult, Michael. This is your money."

A heavy, emotional lump formed in my throat. "Okay," I whispered. "Just my name."

"Alright, that’s finalized," Nigel smiled, hitting a key on his keyboard. "Your accounts are open. Will there be any external wire routing set up today?"

"Yes," Jessica said casually, pulling her pristine Quantum laptop from her messenger bag. "I'm initiating an internal transfer from my checking. Seed his new checking account with one thousand dollars, and put five thousand dollars into his new Share Savings."

My jaw dropped. "Jess—"

"I'm not finished," Jessica said, her fingers flying across her keyboard. "Nigel, generate his new routing and account numbers for external linkage. I am opening an individual brokerage account for Michael at Meridian Wealth Management, and I am seeding it with a wire transfer of fifty thousand dollars."

The entire desk went dead silent.

My head literally felt like it was going to explode. My processor catastrophically stalled. *Fifty thousand dollars.* Plus the six thousand she just dropped into my credit union accounts.

I sat frozen, staring at the twenty-six-year-old woman sitting in the wheelchair next to me. The sheer, incomprehensible magnitude of what she had done for me over the last week suddenly crashed over my brain in a tidal wave. She had bought me a ten-pound, top-of-the-line digital sanctuary laptop. She had absorbed me into her apartment. She had paid for us to travel to Portland to sit in the front row for Monday Night WAR. And this Sunday, we were sitting in front-row Ringsider seats for the *No Quarter* Pay-Per-View at The Emerald Coliseum in Seattle—tickets she had bought months ago at an astronomical premium.

How did a twenty-six-year-old have this much financial firepower?

The realization hit me with the force of a physical blow: *She didn't just survive while I was trapped on the East Coast. She conquered. She built a corporate empire, and her absolute first instinct was to use every single ounce of her wealth to buy my freedom.*

"Jess, no," I stammered, my hands flying up, completely overwhelmed by trauma-logic. "No. Absolutely not. I can't pay you back. I can't take that. It's too much—"

"Michael, stop," Jessica commanded. The sharp tone cut right through my panic. She leaned forward, dropping completely into her Senior Technical Program Manager persona to bypass my emotions and speak directly to my logic.

"I don't do charity," Jessica stated flatly, her eyes locking onto mine with terrifying intensity. "I manage assets. For two years, a parasite drained your potential. You were a mismanaged resource. I am injecting capital into a high-yield Meridian asset because I know your ROI—your Return on Investment—is going to be astronomical."

She reached out, resting her hand firmly over my shaking fingers. "This isn't a gift. It's a deployment fund. It sits at Meridian, and it grows. You never have to worry about being homeless or trapped again. Zero strings attached. You owe me nothing but your success."

I stared at her, tears violently blurring my vision. It felt entirely too good to be true, but staring into her eyes, I knew it was absolute reality.

"Before I close my dashboard," Jessica said, turning back to Nigel. "I need to set up one ACH external transfer. One thousand dollars." She looked directly at Kristin. "What is your routing and checking number?"

Kristin's eyes widened in sheer shock. "Jess, no! You are not paying me. I didn't do anything—"

"You threw your body between my brother and a monster today," Jessica interrupted, her voice thick with fierce, unyielding gratitude. "You are not related to him. You have no blood obligation to this pack. But you stood on that transit platform and acted like a Sentinel. I protect the people who protect my family. Give Nigel your account and routing number, Kristin."

Kristin looked at Jessica, completely stunned by the magnitude of the gesture, and slowly pulled a voided check from her wallet to hand to the banker.

I couldn't hold it in anymore. The suffocating terror of Delores, the exhaustion of the transit grid, and the overwhelming weight of Jessica's weaponized love all collided at once.

I stood up from my padded chair. I reached down, wrapping my arms around Jessica’s shoulders, burying my face into the side of her head. A second later, I felt Kristin stand up and wrap her arms around both of us, her taller frame easily encompassing my shoulders.

We held the massive, three-person embrace right there in the middle of the Quantum credit union. I cried—heavy, silent tears of pure, unadulterated relief. I was safe. I was funded. I was loved.

"Alright," Jessica whispered, her own voice thick with emotion as she patted my back. "Dry your eyes, Michael. I promised I would treat both of you to lunch, and the cafeteria is calling."
