# Part 2: Jessica's Train Ride – 8:15 AM (PST)

*(Jessica's POV)*

I wheeled up the mini-high platform, navigating the metal ramp so I could board the TAPS Commuter Rail at the designated accessible seating area.

Logistically, taking the train into Seattle made absolutely no sense. I worked in Redmond. The fastest, most direct route to the Overlake Transit Center was to just wait at the bay for the TAPS Route 564 express bus. But the 564 wasn't there, and the train was boarding right now. My tactical brain had completely short-circuited under the sheer, suffocating need to get away from Delores. I just wanted to be on a moving vehicle, putting immediate physical distance between myself and the toxic squalor of that house.

*Michael, please come with me,* I thought to myself, the words echoing desperately in my mind as I unlocked the brakes on my chair.

As I wheeled onto the carriage, I could have sworn I heard him faintly mumble, *"Jess, help me"*. But the freezing morning wind had been whipping across the platform, and my logical brain immediately miscategorized the auditory data. It didn't meet the volume threshold for a standard request for help, so I dismissed it as the wind.

The heavy pneumatic doors hissed shut. The silver and green commuter train vibrated slightly as it pulled away from the Kent Station platform, leaving my brother standing in the freezing morning mist with that monster.

The absolute second the train pulled away, the adrenaline of my escape evaporated, leaving behind a crushing, paralyzing regret.

I sat alone in the designated bay, the reality of what I had just done crashing into me with the force of a physical blow.

I reached into my heavy messenger bag with trembling hands. Before I pulled out my Quantum-issued laptop, my fingers closed around a thick, rectangular piece of matte plastic. It was a pre-release prototype of the Quantum Zephyr—a dedicated digital media player our hardware division was launching this month. I had been tasked with dogfooding the firmware on my daily commute.

I shoved the earbuds in, desperately needing a wall of sound to drown out the mechanical hum of the train and the screaming guilt in my own head. I spun the tactile directional pad and blindly hit play on my saved library.

A heavy, sweeping synthesizer chord filled my ears, immediately followed by the driving arena drums of a mid-tempo 1980s pop-rock anthem by Steel Horizon.

*When the steel doors close and the platform clears,*

*You can hear the truth grinding in the gears.*

*Heed the warning hiding in his eyes,*

*Before the distance leaves your soul behind...*

The lyrics hit me like a physical strike. It was a flawless, devastating transcription of my exact failure. The soaring, melodramatic vocals felt like an indictment broadcast straight into my skull, completely overriding my corporate firewall.

*Trust the silence when he calls your name...*

My breath hitched. The mumble on the platform. It wasn't the wind. He had literally begged me for help, and I had rolled onto the train anyway.

The heavy, driving bassline built into the second verse, and the vocalist belted out the final, agonizing truth:

*Wishing to God I had pulled you away...*

That realization hit me so hard my vision completely blurred. My processor stalled, the rigid armor I maintained to survive the baseline adult world shattering into a million pieces.

“The train is approaching Tukwila Station… Tukwila Station… Look around for personal belongings and watch your head on the luggage rack,” the automated PA system announced overhead.

The robotic voice was completely indifferent. It didn't care that my heart was breaking, or that my perimeter had catastrophically failed. It was just a machine, grinding forward on a schedule, perfectly mirroring the cold, mechanical reality of my own logic.

As the heavy air brakes engaged to slow the train, my analytical brain desperately tried to find a variable to blame. I tried to blame the transit schedule. I tried to blame the physical distance.

I ran a quick, brutal simulation: *What if the direct bus to Overlake had pulled up instead of the train?*

The math instantly trapped me. If the bus had been there, the operator would have only needed to see my Quantum Blue Badge, which counted as valid bus fare. But bus fareboxes were strictly cash-only. I didn't have any paper bills on me. To get Michael on that bus, I would have been forced to stay on the freezing platform with Delores, wheel over to the TVM, use my debit card to buy him a paper train ticket, and then use that ticket as a transfer credit.

The sickening truth crystallized in the ambient hum of the carriage. I wouldn't have waited. The urge to escape Delores’s toxic airspace was so absolute, so overwhelmingly suffocating, that I would have flashed my badge, boarded the bus, and left my baby brother behind anyway.

It wasn't the schedule. It wasn't the machine. *I* was the point of failure.

I was full-blown crying. I pulled the earbuds out, letting them dangle over the wheel of my chair.

"Jessica?" a soft, familiar voice interrupted.

I looked up, wiping roughly at my cheeks. Standing in the aisle was Ellen, a senior developer on my team.

"What are you doing..." Ellen started, her voice trailing off as she took in the absolute devastation on my face. She immediately stepped closer. "Jessica, what happened?"

"It’s my brother," I sobbed, the dam completely breaking. I didn't care that I was crying in public. "He’s trapped with this absolute slob of a girlfriend, and that’s being polite".

A TAPS fare inspector stepped into our carriage, holding his scanner.

"Tickets and passes, please," he announced.

I fumbled with my lanyard, my hands shaking too violently to find the plastic edge. But Ellen immediately stepped in, acting as a flawless physical buffer. She flashed her own Quantum Blue Badge to the inspector before gently pulling mine forward so he could see the corporate insignia.

"She’s having a severe family crisis right now," Ellen explained quietly to the inspector. "We are all getting off at King Street".

The inspector offered a sympathetic nod and moved down the aisle without demanding my attention.

By the time the heavy commuter train rolled into the dark tunnel beneath Downtown Seattle, my processor had completely shut down. I was so entirely numb, so paralyzed by the horrific data of the morning, that I couldn't even unlock my own brakes.

Ellen didn't ask. She simply grabbed the rubber push-handles of my chair and wheeled me off the carriage. With Kyle and Lucy flanking us, they silently navigated me through the station and up the elevator to the street-level transit spine.

We waited in the freezing drizzle until a heavy, 40-foot high-floor transit bus pulled up to the curb, its bright orange digital destination sign glowing through the gloom: 545 BEAR CREEK.

The driver deployed the mechanical wheelchair lift. Ellen pushed me onto the metal platform, holding on securely as the hydraulics hoisted us up to the cabin floor, hooking the heavy nylon straps to my frame.

The bus lumbered up 4th Avenue, lurching through the morning traffic. The driver wrenched the heavy steering wheel, making a wide right turn onto East Olive Avenue, before finally merging onto the SR-520 floating bridge.

On paper, the transit schedule claimed the express ride across Lake Washington took thirty minutes. But this was 2006. The old, narrow two-lane SR-520 bridge was a notorious, inescapable bottleneck. We hit the bridge and immediately slammed into a crawling, bumper-to-bumper nightmare.

The heavy diesel engine idled, inching forward at an agonizing pace. Being trapped in the gridlock felt like a physical extension of my own helplessness. I was trapped in a metal box over the water, crawling north and east at five miles an hour, while my brother was being dragged further south into the squalor of Pierce County.

The bus groaned past the Evergreen Point Freeway Station, and then the Yarrow Point Freeway Station. Finally, the driver accelerated onto the exit ramp.

"NE 40th Freeway Station!" the bus operator called out loudly over his shoulder, his voice carrying clearly over the rumble of the engine.

It wasn't the main transit hub; it was the specific bus stop located directly on the exit ramp, designed explicitly for Overlake workers transferring to the corporate campus. Ellen unhooked my tie-downs and wheeled me back onto the mechanical lift, lowering me off the bus and directing me toward the loading bay for the Quantum corporate shuttles.

My eyes were swollen and red, and I was still actively crying.

"Jessica, what’s wrong?" Lucy asked, as the hydraulic lift loaded me onto the small shuttle bus.

"It’s my brother," I sobbed, the sheer, crushing guilt hitting me again. "He’s trapped. And now I wish I had just pulled rank and forced him to catch the train with me."

"Jessica, please take it easy," Kyle said gently, sitting in the row next to my chair. "If you need anything—anything at all—let us know. We’re all here for you."

"Thanks, everyone," I managed to say, reaching up to pull the yellow stop-request cord as the shuttle approached my building on the Redmond campus.

My coworkers knew me. They knew I operated on pure, pragmatic logic. I didn't cry at work. I didn't break down over stressful deadlines. The fact that I had been crying non-stop from Kent, onto the TAPS Commuter Rail, and crawling all the way across the SR-520 was setting off massive alarm bells across the team.

"We know how fiercely protective you are of him," Mark, a senior project manager, said as he stood up. "Please, call him and try to get him up here. We can have someone meet him at the transit center."

Mark pulled out his own cell phone and dialed. "Andromeda? It’s Mark. Get the team to the shuttle drop-off. We have a situation with Jessica."

"Rachel and Kristin are going to try to intercept him on one of the Pierce County buses," I explained, unlocking my brakes as the shuttle doors opened. "I’m having him check in via text every time he changes routes."

"Jess, you should turn back. Just make your way down to Pierce County and go physically get him," Kyle urged, stepping out onto the sidewalk with me.

"I have a job to do," I said stubbornly, gripping my push-rims. "And I am going to do it."

Several of my coworkers formed a tight, protective perimeter around my chair as I wheeled up the concrete path to our building.

When the automatic doors slid open, it felt like half the department was waiting in the lobby. Andromeda, my direct supervisor, immediately stepped forward, her face lined with deep concern.

"Jessica, what happened?" Andromeda asked softly.

Through my continued sobs, the entire story finally spilled out. "I just saw my brother for the first time in two years," I choked out, pausing to wipe my face. "And he is with this... disgusting pig of a girlfriend. I am just so upset at how she treated both of us when I spent the night. They were visiting her aunt’s place in Kent, and I’m just terrified for him."

My phone buzzed in my lap. I looked down at the text message.

*Downtown Tacoma. About to board the 2, vehicle \#186.*

I didn't reply to him. I immediately hit speed dial for Kristin.

"Kristin," I ordered, my tactical brain desperately trying to override my panic. "They’re on the Number 2. They are about to leave 10th & Commerce on Bus 186. I need you to intercept them discreetly on that exact route. But listen to me: do not, for any reason, try to talk to Michael if Delores is with him. She is incredibly toxic and jealous. She threw a fit just over him hugging Rachel and me. So you only talk to him if he is physically alone."

"Understood," Kristin replied with military precision. "His timing is perfect. That is the exact bus I need to catch for school anyway."

"Thank you. I have to go," I sobbed, hanging up the phone.

I pushed myself through the lobby and pulled up to my workstation. I just could not stop crying. I opened my IDE, fully intending to do my job, but my vision was too blurred to read the syntax.

My desk phone rang. It was an external number—The Spot, the massive big-box retail store down in Lakewood where Rachel worked. I snatched the receiver up instantly.

"Quantum, this is Jessica," I answered, my voice shaking.

"Jess, it's Rachel," my little sister's raspy voice came through the line. "I’m here at work. My GSTL let me use the phone at the Guest Services desk. I just saw Michael, Kristin, and Delores walk by the transit center. Jess... Michael and Delores look like they haven’t showered or groomed in ages. She was practically barking orders at him. I’ve made up my mind. He needs to leave her today. I have to go, my break is over."

"Thanks, Rachel," I sobbed, the confirmation of his physical deterioration hitting me like a truck.

Andromeda stepped quietly into my cubicle.

"Jessica," Andromeda offered gently. "Would you like me to route all of your incoming calls to my desk? I can act as a filter and only let people through on a case-by-case basis so you don't have to deal with work calls right now."

"Please," I nodded gratefully. I grabbed a small sticky note and quickly scribbled down a list of names and numbers. I handed it to my boss. "These are the only numbers that may be routed through to me. No questions asked."

The list was absolute. It contained only my immediate, trusted perimeter: my little sister Rachel (home, cell, and work), her best friend and roommate Kristin (home, cell, and work), and my baby brother Michael (cell). No one else was allowed through. I deliberately left our dad completely off the list.

Andromeda didn't just walk back to her cubicle to make the switch on her end. She stepped around my chair and leaned over my desk. Her fingers flew across my external mechanical keyboard as she pulled up the Quantum Communicator client, spreading the software interface across my dual LCD monitors. I sat back in my wheelchair, wiping my eyes as I watched her navigate the unified communications software with flawless, executive efficiency.

I sat back in my wheelchair, wiping my eyes as I watched her navigate the unified communications software with flawless, executive efficiency. She clicked my global status, instantly changing the green available icon to a solid red *Do Not Disturb*. Then, she opened my advanced call-routing preferences and engaged the delegation rules, forcing every standard incoming call on my direct inward dialing line to instantly and silently forward to her desk.

Finally, she looked down at the sticky note. She manually added Rachel, Kristin, and Michael to my active directory contact list. With a few quick clicks, she right-clicked their profiles and elevated their specific phone numbers to the highest software override available in the beta: *Personal Access Level*.

Because they were flagged with personal access, they were the only three people on the planet mathematically permitted to bypass the digital firewall and make my desk phone ring. Everyone else was blocked out.

"Your perimeter is secured," Andromeda promised quietly, giving my desk a reassuring tap before stepping back. "I will handle the rest."

As 11:30 approached, a small group of my team members gathered around my desk.

"We’ll walk with you to the cafeteria if you’d like, Jess," Kyle offered.

We took the elevator down to the ground level and got in line at the campus food court. I wasn't hungry, but I let them guide me.

"Jessica, would you like to take the afternoon off and go collect your brother?" Trevor, our team lead, asked seriously. "I can drive you down to Kent right now and take you two back to Renton. Your wheelchair will fit in the back of my SUV, no sweat."

"Thanks, Trevor," I sobbed, staring blankly at the salad bar. "But I want to work my hours. Besides... he isn't in Kent anymore. He’s all the way down in Lakewood."

"Then have him come up to Redmond," Trevor insisted. "Bring him here, onto the campus, and we will physically get him out of this mess."

I pulled out my phone and quickly texted Michael.

*Please come to Overlake (take 545 from Downtown Seattle). Coworkers want to meet you. Call when on 545.*

My phone buzzed almost immediately.

*On 410 to S Hill, transferring to 564 to Kent. Cousin wants us.*

My blood ran cold. They weren't just riding buses; Delores was actively pulling him back toward the squalor of that house in Kent.

*Going to Call You,* I texted back.

I motioned for my coworkers to lean in closer, and I placed my phone on the cafeteria table, switching it to speakerphone.

Andromeda held up a hand. "Let me do the talking, Jessica. I have an idea to bypass her control."

I nodded, wiping my eyes. "Okay."

The line rang twice before Michael picked up.

"Michael?" Andromeda announced, pitching her voice into a sharp, intimidating, high-level corporate register. "This is Andromeda, Jessica’s boss at Quantum. We would like for you to come up to the Redmond campus immediately so we can meet you. Jessica says you are currently on the bus to South Hill?"

Across the table, Kyle was rapidly scrolling through the regional transit schedules on his phone. He jotted down a route combination and slid the paper over to Andromeda.

"We need you to route up to Quantum," Andromeda continued smoothly, leaving zero room for argument.

Over the speakerphone, we heard Michael’s voice, muffled as he turned to speak to Delores.

"My sister’s boss is on the phone with me. She wants us to come to the Quantum campus right now."

I grabbed a napkin and a pen from the table. My hands were shaking with pure, unadulterated rage at the woman controlling him. I quickly scribbled a note and shoved it across the table to Andromeda.

It read: **Please have lunch ready for my brother. Do not feed her! Make any excuse you need.** I underlined *DO NOT FEED HER* three times in bold, black ink. I wanted it absolutely clear to my entire team just how deeply, viscerally I hated this woman for what she was doing to my baby brother.
